You are drowning in explanations. The website went down — was it a hacker, a bad
deploy, or a cosmic ray? Your friend is an hour late — did they forget you, get
hit by a bus, or just lose track of time? Every event in your life arrives with a
fan of possible causes, and most of them are wrong. You need a quick way to bet
on which one to chase *first*.

That's what a **razor** is: a rule of thumb that "shaves off" unlikely
explanations so you can focus on the promising ones. It isn't a proof. It's a
*prior* — a smart starting bet you make before the evidence is all in. This course
teaches the two most useful razors ever invented.

**Occam's razor** says: among explanations that fit the facts equally well,
prefer the one that requires the **fewest assumptions**. **Hanlon's razor** says:
never attribute to **malice** what is adequately explained by **incompetence** or
plain carelessness. One trims complexity; the other trims paranoia. Both make you
faster and less wrong — *most* of the time.

This course assumes you've met first-principles thinking and want sharper tools
for the everyday job of choosing among explanations. By the end you'll know how to
count the assumptions an explanation smuggles in, why fewer assumptions really
does mean fewer ways to be wrong, how Hanlon's razor defuses your conspiracy
reflex — and, crucially, the cases where each razor leads you straight off a
cliff. A razor that you trust blindly stops being a tool and becomes a blindfold.
