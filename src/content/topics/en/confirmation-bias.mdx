In 1960, a London psychologist named Peter Wason sat people down with a tiny
puzzle. *"I have a rule in mind that some sequences of three numbers obey,"* he
told them. *"The triple **2, 4, 6** obeys it. Propose your own triples, and I'll
tell you whether each one fits. When you're sure you know the rule, say so."*
People got to work. They tried `8, 10, 12` — *fits!* They tried `20, 22, 24` —
*fits!* — and `100, 102, 104` — *fits!* Confident now, they announced the rule:
**"even numbers going up by two."** Wrong. The real rule was almost insultingly
broad: *any three numbers in increasing order.* `1, 2, 3` would have fit. So would
`5, 50, 500`. The reason almost nobody found it is the reason this entire course
exists: every triple they tested was one they **expected to pass**. They went
looking for *yes*, got *yes* every time, and mistook a pile of confirmations for a
proof. Not one of them tried a triple designed to come back **no** — the only kind
of test that could have revealed the rule.

That is **confirmation bias**: the deep, automatic tendency to seek, interpret, and
remember evidence in ways that confirm what we already believe — and to skate
right past whatever would prove us wrong. It is not stupidity, and it is not (only)
dishonesty. The sharpest, most educated minds do it; in fact, as we'll see, a
sharper mind often does it *better*, because it can build a more airtight case for
the conclusion it already wanted. The 18th-century essayist Francis Bacon
diagnosed it four centuries ago: *"The human understanding when it has once adopted
an opinion draws all things else to support and agree with it."* The mind, in
other words, behaves like a **lawyer** defending a client it has already decided is
innocent — not like a **scientist** trying to find out what's true.

This course turns that lawyer back into a scientist. You'll learn the precise
definition and the **three places** the bias operates — in what we *search* for,
how we *interpret* what we find, and what we later *remember* — and you'll watch
the Wason task happen to *you* in an interactive demonstration, so the trap stops
being a story and becomes an experience. You'll see why the same study can leave
two opposed camps each *more* convinced they were right, why echo chambers and
algorithmic feeds are confirmation-bias machines, and why the bias is so universal
that it must have been useful once. Then we build the antidote — a small set of
deliberate moves that don't take willpower so much as a habit: **consider the
opposite**, **steelman the other side**, **seek the disconfirming case**, and
**pre-register what would change your mind.** It assumes you've met one earlier
idea — that incentives quietly bend belief — and builds the cognitive machinery
underneath it. By the end you'll have the rarest reflex in all of thinking: when
you feel most certain you're right, you'll have learned to ask the one question the
lawyer never does — *"What would prove me wrong, and have I actually looked?"*
