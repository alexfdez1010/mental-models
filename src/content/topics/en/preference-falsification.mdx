A committee nods along to a plan that half the room thinks is doomed. A whole
country cheers a leader that private grumbling says almost nobody wants. A team
ships a feature everyone quietly believes is a mistake. Then one day — a joke, a
leak, a first brave voice — and the polite consensus collapses so fast that
afterwards everyone insists they "always knew."

That is **preference falsification**: when saying what you truly think carries a
social, professional, or political cost, you publicly express the opinion you
believe is *safe* rather than the one you privately *hold*. Coined by the
economist **Timur Kuran** (*Private Truths, Public Lies*, 1995), it is one of the
most powerful models in the whole latticework, because it explains a genuinely
weird fact about human groups: **the public consensus can be a facade almost
nobody privately believes**, held up only because each person underestimates how
many others secretly agree with them.

This course builds the idea from the ground up. It separates your *private*
preference from your *public* one and shows why the gap opens; why the **cost of
dissent** is the engine, and why repression can *raise* measured agreement while
hollowing out real support; the **hidden-distribution problem** — pluralistic
ignorance, where everyone thinks they are the lonely dissenter; the **cascade**
that unravels it when the cost drops or a few brave speakers go first (tying to
critical mass and information cascades); the **persistence trap**, where
falsification freezes bad norms, censorship, and unloved policies in place for
years and then they evaporate at once; and how the same silence quietly
**corrupts knowledge itself**, so that even the rulers end up reasoning from fake
information. It ends where every good model does — at its limits: not every hidden
preference is a silent majority waiting to erupt, and the model is far too easy to
abuse as a way to claim everyone secretly agrees with *you*.
