Pull up a subway map and you'll see a clean tangle of coloured lines and evenly
spaced dots. It is a triumph of design — and it is a *lie*. The stations aren't
really evenly spaced, the lines don't really run at those angles, and the river
is the wrong shape. The map is wrong on purpose, and that is exactly why it
works: it threw away everything except the one thing you need underground —
*which line takes me where.*

That is the single most important idea in all of clear thinking, and it has a
name. In 1931 Alfred Korzybski wrote the line that every model-thinker eventually
quotes: **"The map is not the territory."** A map — any model, any theory, any
mental shortcut — is a compressed stand-in for reality, never reality itself.
It leaves things out. It *has* to. And the skill that separates careful thinkers
from confident-but-wrong ones is knowing **where your map stops matching the
ground** — before you walk off the edge of it.

This course assumes only that you've met the idea of a mental model. By the end
you'll be able to say precisely what "the map is not the territory" warns against,
read George Box's "all models are wrong, but some are useful" the *right* way,
spot the three situations where the gap between map and territory bites, name the
trap of **reification** (mistaking the model for the thing), and — most usefully —
carry a handful of habits that keep your maps honest without leaving you
paralysed and map-less.
