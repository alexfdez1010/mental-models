You have now spent a whole track meeting the ways a mind misfires: it hunts for
evidence it is already right (**confirmation bias**), it trusts the vivid over the
frequent, it clings to what it has already spent, and it feels far more certain than
its track record earns (the overconfidence you measured in *Calibration*). Here is
the uncomfortable punchline the whole psychology path has been building toward:
**knowing all of that does almost nothing to fix it.** You can recite the sunk-cost
fallacy word-perfect and still throw good money after bad an hour later.

The reason has a name. The **bias blind spot** (Emily Pronin) is a bias *about*
biases: people rate themselves as less biased than the average person, and they spot
distortion in everyone else while feeling clear-eyed and objective themselves. It
works because introspection lies to you — biases operate *below* awareness, so when
you look inward for them you find nothing and conclude you are fine. "I know about
anchoring, so it won't get me" is itself the blind spot talking.

This course is the meta-skill the rest of the track was preparing you for: not
*naming* biases but actually *reducing their grip on your own judgment*. And it
starts by killing a comforting illusion — that awareness is enough — and replacing it
with tools that work whether or not you happen to catch yourself in the act.

The route sorts every fix into two families. **Change the thinker** — disciplines you
run inside your own head: the **outside view** (ask how similar cases actually turned
out instead of reasoning from inside this one), **considering the opposite**, and the
**pre-mortem** (imagine the plan has already failed and explain why). These help, but
they still lean on your willpower in the moment. **Change the environment** — fixes
built into the process so they do not depend on you: **checklists** that offload the
judgment, **red teams** and structured dissent that institutionalise disagreement,
**decision hygiene** (independent estimates before discussion, to stop anchoring and
cascades), **simple rules and algorithms** where the data allows, and
**pre-commitment** — binding your future self before the distortion arrives. That
gives the debiasing hierarchy: **structure beats incentives beats training beats mere
awareness.**

You will drive a hands-on **debiasing bench** — realistic judgment calls, each with a
lurking bias — and watch the error meter as you apply different tools, discovering for
yourself that "just be aware" barely moves the needle while a checklist or a red team
cuts deep. And you will learn where debiasing itself lies to you: using "you're just
biased" as a rhetorical weapon while exempting yourself (the blind spot again),
over-correcting into uselessly wishy-washy hedging, paying for expensive hygiene on
low-stakes calls, and forgetting that no technique removes bias — it only shrinks it.

By the end you will own the rarest thing in all of judgment: not a longer list of
biases, but a working system for catching your own mind in the act — and the humility
to build the structure precisely because you cannot trust yourself to catch it.
