A general lands his army on the enemy's shore, and the first thing he does is *burn his own ships*. Retreat is now impossible — his soldiers must win or die. This looks insane. He has just destroyed his own options, thrown away a lifeline, made his own situation strictly worse on paper. And yet it is one of the oldest winning moves in the book, because of what it does to the *other* side's reasoning. The enemy, watching the smoke, now knows something it did not know before: these men cannot flee, so they will fight to the last. An enemy that expected an easy rout of retreating invaders may now decide the fight is not worth it — and never attack at all. The general made himself weaker in options and stronger in *outcome*. That is the paradox at the heart of this course: **the power of a credible commitment.**

A **commitment** is a move that deliberately restricts your own future freedom — you give something up, close a door, or bind yourself to a course of action *in advance*. A commitment is **credible** when the other side genuinely believes you will follow through, because you have arranged things so that following through is now your only option, or plainly your best one. And the reason to do any of this is **strategic**: by changing what you *can* and *will* do, you change what a rational rival *expects* you to do — which changes what they do, which is the whole point. This is the counterintuitive engine of Thomas Schelling's *The Strategy of Conflict*, the work that won a Nobel Prize and reframed everything from nuclear standoffs to labour strikes to why you hand your car keys to a friend before a party. Freedom is usually an asset. Here it is sometimes a *liability* — and the skill is knowing when to throw it away, and how to make the throw believed.

This is an **expert strategy** course, and it stands on two you should already have. From **Nash equilibrium** it borrows the idea of a resting point where no player can gain by moving alone, and the master move of *changing the game* rather than scolding the players — a commitment is precisely a way to change the equilibrium by changing what your rival's best response has to reckon with. From **mechanism design** it borrows the idea that a well-chosen *rule* can steer self-interested behaviour; a commitment is a rule you impose *on yourself*. From there we build the whole model. We start with the core paradox — how **tying your hands** changes a rival's best response, using a two-move game you can play with directly. Then the thing that makes or breaks every commitment: **credibility**, and the toolkit for manufacturing it (contracts and penalties, public promises, delegating to an agent who *must* follow through, and automaticity — the "doomsday machine"). We separate **credible threats from credible promises**, and **deterrence** (stopping an action) from **compellence** (forcing one). We turn to **brinkmanship** — deliberately raising a shared risk of catastrophe to make the other side blink, and why "a threat that leaves something to chance" can be *more* credible than a precise one. We take the model to its most consequential arena, **deterrence and mutual assured destruction**, second-strike capability, and the unsettling logic by which *vulnerability* can be destabilising. And we bring it all home to **commitment devices in ordinary life** — Ulysses lashed to the mast, Christmas savings clubs, staking your reputation — before an honest accounting of where the model bites back: bluffs that get called, and the **commitment trap** where you have locked yourself into a disaster you can no longer escape. By the end you will be able to spot a commitment problem, tell a credible threat from an empty one, and know when burning a bridge is genius and when it is just arson.
