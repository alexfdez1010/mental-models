A restaurant with a queue out the door pulls in more diners; a book near the top
of the bestseller list sells more copies *because* it is near the top; an app
with five downloads and an app with five million look identical on quality, yet
the second one keeps winning. In each case people are not being stupid. They are
doing something perfectly reasonable: when your own information is thin, watching
what everyone else chose and copying it is often the smartest move you can make.

That is the unsettling heart of this course. An **information cascade** happens
when rational people, seeing only each other's *actions* and not each other's
*reasons*, sensibly abandon their own private evidence and copy the people ahead
of them — so information stops flowing into the group, and a whim can lock a
whole crowd onto a possibly-wrong choice. The individuals are rational; the herd
is fragile and often wrong. This course builds the idea from the canonical urn
experiment and its Bayesian arithmetic, shows why cascades aggregate almost no
knowledge (and so shatter and flip at the first credible nudge), tours their real
faces — queues, bestseller lists, bubbles, standing ovations, citation and
research bandwagons, reputational herding — separates a genuine cascade from mere
conformity and from real consensus, and ends with the cure: designs that keep
*independent* signals alive so a crowd can actually pool what it knows.
