Ask someone what their morning coffee costs and they'll say "four dollars." That's
the **sticker price** — the cash that left your wallet — and it's the smallest, least
interesting part of the answer. The real cost of the coffee is *everything else those
four dollars and those ten minutes could have been*: the book chapter you didn't read,
the four dollars that didn't compound in an index fund, the slightly earlier start you
didn't make. Economists have a name for that real cost — the value of the best
alternative you gave up — and it's the most quietly powerful idea in all of
decision-making: **opportunity cost**.

Here's why it matters. Resources are **scarce** — there is exactly one of your next
hour, one of your next dollar, one of this engineer's next month — so spending any of
them on one thing means *not* spending it on everything else. Every "yes" is a stack of
silent "no"s. Most people only ever count the sticker price and the obvious benefit;
they never look at the no's. That blind spot is where bad trades hide: the cash sitting
"safely" in an account while it loses a fortune in forgone growth, the meeting that
costs nothing to schedule and an afternoon to attend, the flashy feature shipped instead
of the boring one that would have made ten times the money.

This course takes one deceptively simple sentence — *the cost of anything is the
next-best thing you forgo* — and turns it into a reflex. You'll learn to separate the
**explicit** cost you pay from the **implicit** cost you forgo, to make *"compared to
what?"* your automatic first question, to see scarcity as the budget underneath every
decision, and to tell a genuine **trade-off** from a false either/or someone is trying
to sell you. We'll work real numbers — an MBA that "costs" \$80,000 and actually costs
\$220,000, \$10,000 in cash that quietly forfeits \$66,000, a feature that loses money by
being built — until the comparison happens on its own, every time. It assumes only the
foundations: that a mental model is a portable thinking tool. By the end, you won't be
able to *un*-see the road not taken — which is exactly the point.
