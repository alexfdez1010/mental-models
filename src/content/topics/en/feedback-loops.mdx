You already know to ask *"and then what?"* — to trace a decision past its first,
obvious result into the world's reaction. **Second-order thinking** told you those
later effects exist and matter. This course tells you *where they come from* and
*why they so often explode, vanish, or wobble* instead of behaving.

The answer is the **feedback loop**: an arrangement where a system's output loops
back to become its own input. A whisper into a microphone returns from the speaker
a little louder, re-enters the mic, and within a second you have an ear-splitting
screech — nothing changed the rules, the output just fed back in. That is the whole
of **systems thinking** in one image: stop staring at parts in isolation and start
watching how they loop back on each other. A system is not a list of pieces; it is a
web of loops, and the loops decide everything.

There are only two kinds, and the entire course hangs off the difference. A
**reinforcing (amplifying) loop** feeds an effect back so it grows — compound
interest, viral posts, bank runs, the screech. A **balancing (stabilizing) loop**
feeds an effect back so it shrinks toward a target — a thermostat, your body
sweating to cool down, prices rising until demand falls. Reinforcing loops are why
small things become huge; balancing loops are why most things stay put. And the
villain that makes *both* misbehave is the **delay**: when the feedback arrives
late, a stabiliser overshoots its goal and starts to oscillate — the reason
showers scald, supply chains whipsaw, and economies boom and bust.

This course assumes you've finished **second-order thinking** — loops are the
machinery *underneath* those downstream effects, the reason a consequence compounds
instead of fizzling. By the end you'll speak the language of **stocks and flows**,
spot reinforcing and balancing loops in the wild, predict when a delay will turn a
calm system into an oscillating one, and know the highest-leverage place to push a
system to actually change its behaviour. The world stops looking like a row of
dominoes and starts looking like what it is: a tangle of loops, humming.
