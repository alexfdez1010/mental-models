Most of the time, you don't actually think — you *copy*. You reach for whatever
the people around you did, whatever worked last time, whatever "everyone knows."
That shortcut is called **reasoning by analogy**, and it's fast, cheap, and right
often enough that you barely notice you're doing it.

**First-principles thinking** is the opposite move: instead of copying the
answer, you tear the problem down to the handful of things you *know* are true —
the bedrock facts that can't be reduced any further — and rebuild a solution from
those, ignoring how it's "always been done." It's slower. It's harder. And it's
the single most reliable way anyone has ever found to reach an answer nobody else
could see.

This course assumes you've met the idea of a mental model and want a sharper tool
for cracking hard problems. By the end you'll be able to spot when you're
copying, run the "why" ladder down to bedrock, separate a real constraint from an
inherited assumption, and — crucially — know when *not* to bother, because
analogy is the right call far more often than the gurus admit.
