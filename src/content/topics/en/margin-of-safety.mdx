A bridge rated to carry the heaviest truck that will ever cross it is a bridge
waiting to collapse. Engineers don't build to the expected load — they build to
*several times* it. A walkway designed for the crowd you expect is built to hold
five times that crowd; an elevator cable sized for its rated weight is spun to
hold ten times more. The extra is not waste and it is not timidity. It is the
single most important idea in engineering, and one of the most transferable
models you will ever carry: the **margin of safety**.

The logic is brutally simple. Your estimate of the load is *wrong* — not because
you're careless, but because the world contains things your estimate didn't:
a heavier-than-spec truck, a corroded joint, a gust of wind, a once-in-a-century
storm, a material flaw nobody measured. You cannot list those surprises in
advance; that's what makes them surprises. So instead of trying to predict every
one, you leave a **buffer** big enough to absorb the ones you can't foresee. The
margin of safety is the gap between *what you expect* and *what you can survive* —
and it is the difference between being wrong and being ruined.

This model was born in **structural engineering**, but it leaves the workshop
almost immediately. Benjamin Graham made it the central idea of intelligent
investing — buy a dollar of value for fifty cents, so that even if you've
*misjudged* the dollar, you're still whole. It's why you keep a cash cushion
instead of spending to the last penny, why a careful plan leaves slack in the
schedule, why a pilot carries more fuel than the flight plan needs. Every one of
these is the same move: **don't optimize for the expected case; survive the bad
one.**

This course assumes you've met **second-order thinking** — the habit of asking
"and then what?" — because a margin of safety is largely a defense against the
second- and third-order surprises you can't trace in advance. By the end you'll
know where margins come from, how to *size* one against your own uncertainty (the
fuzzier your estimate, the fatter the buffer), how redundancy and fail-safes are
just margins wearing different clothes, and — crucially — the trap on the other
side: that a margin has a *cost*, and a system optimized until every scrap of
slack is gone is a system engineered to snap. Build for more than you expect.
Here's how much more, and why.
