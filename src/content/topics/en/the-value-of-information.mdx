Before you pay for one more test, study, survey, or expert opinion, there's a
single question that cuts through almost every "should we gather more data?"
argument — and almost nobody asks it: *could the answer actually change what I
do?* If you'd make the same move no matter what the test says, the test is worth
exactly **zero**, however accurate, cheap, or fascinating it is. All the value of
information comes from the mistakes it lets you **avoid**.

This is the **value-of-information** model, straight from decision analysis
(Ron Howard, 1966). It gives you three sharp tools: the **would-it-change-my-action
test** for spotting worthless data instantly; **EVPI**, the most a perfect answer
could ever be worth — the ceiling; and **EVSI**, what a *real*, noisy test is
worth once you fold in its errors and subtract its price. It's the model that
tells you when to look before you leap — and when looking is just expensive
procrastination.

This is an **expert-tier** course. It assumes you can already revise a belief from
evidence (**Bayesian updating**), weigh outcomes by their odds (**thinking in
probabilities**), and see why a convex payoff rewards keeping your options open
(**asymmetry and optionality**). We'll put all three to work turning "gather more
data" from a reflex into an expected-value decision you can actually compute.
