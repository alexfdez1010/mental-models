Most people run the world on two settings: *true* and *false*. Will it rain?
Will the startup work? Is this person lying? The mind reaches for a verdict — yes
or no — and then defends it. The trouble is that almost nothing worth deciding is
actually yes/no. It's *maybe*, and the only honest question is **how much maybe**.

**Thinking in probabilities** is the discipline of replacing that yes/no switch
with a dial. Instead of "the deal will close," you say "about 70% likely." Instead
of "that's impossible," you ask "how rare, exactly — one in ten, one in a
thousand?" It sounds like a small shift in wording. It is actually the difference
between a mind that gets blindsided by the obvious and one that saw it coming and
priced it in. The world is uncertain whether or not you admit it; thinking in
probabilities just stops you from pretending otherwise.

This course assumes only that you know what a mental model is — that a good model
is a portable tool you carry from one situation to the next. From there it builds
the probabilistic toolkit a careful decider can't do without: the gap between
**possibility and probability** (and why "it's possible" is almost useless on its
own), **base rates** and the outside view (the single most ignored number in any
forecast), **expected value** — the formula `EV = Σ(probability × payoff)` that
lets you weigh a gamble in one line — and the habit of **thinking in ranges**
instead of false-precise point estimates. It ends on the trap that ruins more
good thinkers than any other: confusing a **good decision with a good outcome**,
and learning to judge the bet, not just the result. You'll drive an interactive
expected-value calculator and a base-rate visualizer along the way. By the end,
"how likely?" will be your reflex — and that reflex is the doorway to the whole
advanced path of probability, from Bayesian updating to fat tails.
