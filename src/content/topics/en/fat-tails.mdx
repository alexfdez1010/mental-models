You already learned to think in probabilities — to trade the yes/no switch for a
dial, anchor on base rates, weigh bets by expected value, and respect ruin. This
course is where that dial meets the part of reality it was built for: the **wild**
domains, where the rules of the mild ones quietly stop applying.

Here is the whole problem in one sentence. In some worlds, no single event can move
the total much — your height, the temperature tomorrow, the weight of the next
person through the door. Add a thousand of them and the average is rock-solid; the
biggest one is barely larger than the typical one. Call that world **Mediocristan**.
In other worlds — wealth, book sales, war deaths, market moves, pandemics, city
sizes — a *single* observation can be larger than all the rest put together. One
bestseller outsells ten thousand titles combined; one bad day erases a decade of
gains; one outlier *is* the dataset. Call that world **Extremistan**. Almost all of
our intuitions, our averages, and our tidy bell curves were built in Mediocristan —
and they get people killed in Extremistan.

A **fat tail** is the technical name for that second world: a distribution where
extreme values are far more frequent, and far more extreme, than a normal
bell-curve intuition expects. The course builds the idea from the ground up: the
two worlds and why one sample can or can't dominate the total; the **normal
distribution** and exactly how it under-counts the extremes; **power laws** and the
80/20 family that generate fat tails; why the **mean and standard deviation
mislead** when the tail is fat (and why backtests, averages, and "it's never
happened" all lie); **black swans** — rare, high-impact, and rationalized only after
the fact — and the **turkey problem** that breeds them; and finally **ergodicity and
ruin**: why, in a fat-tailed world, the first rule is to never be exposed to the one
outcome you can't come back from. You'll drive an interactive sampler that lets you
fatten the tail with a slider and watch a single draw swallow the whole total. By
the end you'll have the rarest skill in forecasting — knowing *which world you're
in* before you trust the average — and the doorway to calibration, the expert
capstone of the probability path.
