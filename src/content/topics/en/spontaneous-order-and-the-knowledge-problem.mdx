Every morning, a city of ten million wakes up expecting bread, and bread is
there. Nobody is in charge of that. There is no Ministry of Breakfast, no
master spreadsheet of croissants — just millions of strangers, each acting on
scraps of local information the others will never see, coordinated into
something that works better than anything a committee could draw up.

This course is about that trick: **spontaneous order** — coherent, adaptive
order that is *the result of human action but not of human design* — and the
reason it isn't optional: **the knowledge problem**. The information needed to
run an economy, a language, or a legal tradition doesn't exist in one place. It
lives in scattered, often unspeakable fragments inside millions of heads, and
no planner, however brilliant, can collect it. You'll see how prices act as a
telecommunications system that compresses the world into a single number, why
nobody on Earth knows how to make a pencil, how footpaths, common law, and ant
colonies all run on the same logic — and then where the model lies to you:
spontaneous is not the same as good, and "let it emerge" can be as lazy a dogma
as "let me plan it."
