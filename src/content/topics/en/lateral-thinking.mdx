Some problems give way the moment you push harder. Most of the interesting ones
don't. You think and think, each thought a careful next step from the last, and
you end up exactly where the first step pointed — deeper into the same hole. The
harder you dig, the more stuck you get, because the digging was never the
problem. *Where you started digging* was.

**Lateral thinking** is the move that fixes that. Coined by the psychologist
**Edward de Bono** in 1967, it's the deliberate art of *not* attacking a problem
head-on — of stepping sideways to a completely different starting point instead of
grinding forward from the obvious one. Where ordinary "vertical" thinking
deepens a single line of reasoning, lateral thinking *changes the line* — it
reframes the question, challenges the assumptions nobody noticed they were
making, and generates ideas on purpose rather than waiting for a lucky flash.

This course assumes no prior knowledge — only that you've met the idea of a
mental model and want a reliable way to get *unstuck*. It leans lightly on two
sibling tools you may already know: **first-principles thinking** (stripping a
problem down to its base truths) and **inversion** (attacking a goal from its
opposite end). Both are ways of refusing the default framing; lateral thinking is
the broadest of the family.

By the end you'll be able to tell vertical from lateral thinking, spot and drop
the hidden assumption that's holding a problem shut, run de Bono's provocation
and random-entry tools on purpose, diverge before you converge — and, just as
importantly, know exactly when "think outside the box" is the wrong advice and
patient, rigorous vertical thinking should lead instead.
