You have spent a whole run of courses meeting the cognitive biases one at a time — how a mind **defends a belief** once it forms (*confirmation bias*), how **reward quietly bends perception** (*incentives*), how the fear of losing outweighs the hope of gaining. Useful, but incomplete in one specific, slightly unsettling way: those courses treated bias as something that happens *inside* your head, by accident. This course is about what happens when someone *outside* your head knows those biases too — and pulls them on purpose.

Because that is what persuasion actually is. Not charisma, not silver-tongued magic, not a gift some people are born with. **Persuasion is a small, nameable set of psychological levers** — six or seven of them — that reliably move a person from "no" or "not sure" to "yes". A salesperson, a campaigner, a con artist and a good friend are all, whether they know it or not, reaching for the same handful of levers: the pull to **repay a favour**, the pull to **stay consistent** with what we've already said, the pull to **copy the crowd**, to **defer to authority**, to **say yes to people we like**, to **grab what's scarce**, and to help **"one of us"**. Robert Cialdini, the psychologist who spent three years going undercover in sales and fundraising training to find out what really works, distilled the whole messy art down to these principles — and this course teaches each one as a **mental model** of how a mind is moved to comply.

This is the **advanced psychology** rung of the site, and it is where "cognitive biases" stop being a list of private errors and become a *system of levers other people can pull.* It builds directly on two courses you've already done: **incentives** (so you understand the reward that quietly aligns everyone in the room the same way) and the **bias** courses like **confirmation bias** (so you understand how a mind, once nudged, defends the nudge). Every lever you'll meet is really an **incentive and a bias working together** — a shortcut that normally serves us well, turned into a tool. And when several levers fire at once, you get the multiplicative pile-up you may have met as the **lollapalooza effect**: persuasion's most powerful and most dangerous form.

Across six teaching lessons you'll drive an interactive **compliance meter** — flip the levers on a sales scenario one by one and watch the modelled chance of a "yes" climb, then keep stacking and watch it *backfire* as the target feels handled and pushes back. You'll take each principle apart in turn — **reciprocity** (the free sample, the door-in-the-face), **commitment and consistency** (the foot-in-the-door, the written pledge, why small yeses precede big ones), **social proof** (canned laughter, "best-seller", the queue), **authority** (titles, uniforms, the long shadow of the Milgram experiments), **liking** (similarity, compliments, the halo effect), **scarcity** ("only 3 left", the deadline, loss aversion wearing a price tag), and **unity** (the shared "we") — each with the mechanism that makes it *normally sensible*, a fully worked real example, and a defence against its misuse. Then the lesson that matters most: the **ethics** of all this — the line between persuasion and manipulation, and how to *inoculate* yourself by naming the lever out loud and asking, "would I still want this if the tactic were removed?"

By the end you won't just know the levers. You'll feel them click as they're pulled on you — in an ad, a checkout page, a negotiation, a recruitment pitch — which is the moment they lose most of their power. The best defence against being moved by a lever you can't see is learning to see it.
