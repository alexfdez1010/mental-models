In his 1996 letter to Berkshire Hathaway shareholders, Warren Buffett gave the
clearest career advice in finance, and then immediately said it wasn't really about
finance: *"What an investor needs is the ability to correctly evaluate selected
businesses. Note that word 'selected': You don't have to be an expert on every
company, or even many. You only have to be able to evaluate companies within your
**circle of competence**. The size of that circle is not very important; knowing its
boundaries, however, is vital."* That last sentence is the whole model. A **circle of
competence** is the set of subjects where your mental maps are accurate enough to trust
your own judgment — and the skill that matters is not making the circle huge, but
knowing exactly where its edge lies.

Here's why that flips most people's intuition. We're trained to admire range — the
person with an opinion on everything, the expert who never says "I don't know." But a
small circle whose boundary you've mapped honestly is *safer* than a vast one with a
fuzzy edge, because every disaster lives in the same place: just *outside* your circle,
in the band where a subject still feels familiar enough that your confidence keeps
talking after your competence has stopped. The far-away unknowns don't hurt you — you
know to leave them alone. The damage comes from the things one step past your edge, the
ones that look enough like what you know that you never notice you've crossed the line.

This course teaches you to draw that line. You'll learn to separate **"I understand
this"** from **"I'm familiar with this"** (recognition is not knowledge), to spot the
**danger band** at your boundary where overconfidence does its work, to **map your real
circle** by tracking your own forecasts instead of trusting the warm feeling of
expertise, to **widen** the circle deliberately — through study and feedback — rather
than faking range, and to hold the line under social pressure, where the hardest and
most valuable move is Charlie Munger's: drop the thing in the **"too hard" pile** and
walk away. It assumes only one thing before it: that *the map is not the territory* —
that you carry models of the world, and they're reliable for some terrain and not for
the rest. The circle of competence is simply the honest answer to *which* terrain. By
the end, "I don't know" will stop feeling like a confession and start feeling like
exactly what it is: the single most profitable sentence a thinker can say.
