Every action has an immediate result — the one you can see, the one you intended.
Eat the cake and you feel good. Cap the rent and tenants pay less. Hire the person
who solves today's crisis and the crisis goes away. These are **first-order
effects**: the obvious, immediate, intended consequences of a choice, and they are
the only ones most people ever count.

**Second-order thinking** is the discipline of asking one more question — *"and
then what?"* — and then asking it again. The cake spikes your blood sugar, which
crashes an hour later, which makes you hungrier than before. The rent cap makes
building new flats unprofitable, so fewer get built, so the housing shortage you
were trying to ease gets *worse*. The person who fixes today's crisis becomes
irreplaceable doing it, so they never get promoted and quietly burn out. The
second-order effect routinely **flips the sign** of the first — the move that
looked good turns out bad, and the reason hides exactly where ordinary thinking
stops looking.

This course assumes you've met a couple of foundational models — **inversion**
(thinking backwards from failure) and **first-principles thinking** (reasoning up
from what's actually true). Second-order thinking is the natural next rung:
inversion asks *how could this fail?*, and the most common answer is *because of a
consequence I didn't trace*. By the end you'll be able to build a **consequence
tree** several orders deep, spot the incentive and feedback patterns that generate
non-obvious downstream effects, connect every choice to its **opportunity cost**,
and — just as importantly — know when to *stop* forecasting before second-order
thinking curdles into paralysis. The first move is easy. We're here for the rest of
the board.
