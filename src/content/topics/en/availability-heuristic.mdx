Quick question, no peeking: in an average year in the United States, which kills
more people — sharks or falling vending machines? Most people feel a tug toward
sharks, because the moment you read the word a whole scene assembles itself: the
fin, the music, that one news segment. The honest answer is that both are
astonishingly rare, the numbers are roughly comparable, and *neither* belongs
anywhere near the top of any sane list of things to worry about — yet one of them
feels far deadlier than the other purely because it stars in a famous movie. Your
fear didn't track the frequency. It tracked the **footage**. That small, slightly
embarrassing gap between what feels common and what *is* common is the entire
subject of this course.

The shortcut behind it has a name. In the early 1970s the psychologists **Daniel
Kahneman** and **Amos Tversky** noticed that when people are asked how likely or
how frequent something is, they rarely go and count. Instead they do something
faster: they ask themselves *how easily can I bring an example to mind?* — and the
easier the examples come, the more common the thing feels. They called this the
**availability heuristic**: estimating probability or frequency by the ease of
recall. It is a genuinely useful rule of thumb most of the time — things you
encounter often *are* usually easier to remember, so ease and frequency really do
correlate. The trouble is everything *else* that makes a memory easy to summon:
how vivid it was, how recent, how emotionally loud, how heavily the news repeated
it. A shark attack is rare but unforgettable; heart disease is the leading killer
in the developed world but almost never makes the front page. Run your odds off
*availability* and you will systematically fear the wrong things, buy the wrong
insurance, and misjudge the actual texture of risk all around you.

This course takes that one slippery mental move and pulls it fully into the light.
You'll get the precise definition and the idea underneath it — **attribute
substitution**, the mind quietly swapping the hard question ("how frequent is
this?") for an easy one ("how easily does it come to mind?") and never telling you
it made the trade. You'll meet the four engines that crank availability up
independent of any real frequency — **vividness, recency, emotion, and
repetition** — and watch each one distort a real risk in a worked example. You'll
see, in an interactive chart, how far a population's fears drift from its actual
causes of death, and why a newsroom doing its job perfectly will still hand you a
wildly miscalibrated map of danger: rare-but-dramatic events get wall-to-wall
coverage while common, slow killers get none. You'll learn how a single story,
repeated until it feels true, can swell into an **availability cascade** that
stampedes a whole society. And you'll connect it back to the model just below it
on this path — **thinking in probabilities** — because availability is the precise
psychological mechanism by which people drop the **base rate**: the vivid case in
front of you simply shouts louder than the boring statistic that should have
anchored your estimate.

Then comes the part that makes it a tool rather than a curiosity: the fix. You
can't delete the heuristic — it fires before you're aware of it — but you can
learn to catch it, to ask *"am I estimating from data or from the last thing I
saw?"*, to deliberately reach for the base rate, and to discount any risk that
arrives pre-loaded with dramatic footage. By the end you'll have a reliable
new reflex: when a probability *feels* obvious, you'll pause and ask whether
you're measuring how likely it is — or just how easily you can picture it.
