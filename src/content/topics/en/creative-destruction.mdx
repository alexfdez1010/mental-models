We like to picture economic growth as *accumulation* — a rising pile of gadgets,
firms, and jobs stacked politely on top of last year's. Joseph Schumpeter looked at
the same numbers and saw something far more violent: a **"gale of creative
destruction"** — a "process of industrial mutation that incessantly revolutionises
the economic structure *from within*, incessantly destroying the old one,
incessantly creating a new one." Growth, in this view, is not addition. It is
*replacement*. The car did not join the horse-and-buggy trade; it **killed** it.
Streaming did not sit beside the video-rental store; it **buried** it. The transistor
did not supplement the vacuum tube; it **ended** it.

That is the whole idea and the whole discomfort of this course: **progress and ruin
are two faces of one event.** Every new S-curve rises only as an old one collapses,
and the people, capital, and skills tied to the old curve are genuinely destroyed —
not gently upgraded. The prize is enormous (this is the engine of nearly all
long-run growth), and the cost is real (someone's livelihood is the "old order" being
swept away). A serious model has to hold both at once.

This is an **expert-tier** course. It leans on three models you've already met.
**Supply & demand** explains *how* a challenger wins — it undercuts the incumbent on
price or quality and shifts the whole market curve. **Feedback loops** explain the
*rhythm* — innovation compounds, and destroying an incumbent frees the capital and
labour that fuel the next wave. And **moats** explain the *tension* — every moat that
protects an incumbent is, on a long enough clock, a countdown timer. We'll turn all
three into a single, unsentimental lens for reading which industries are about to be
remade, why the incumbents rarely see it coming, and what the model gets wrong when
people wield it as a slogan.
