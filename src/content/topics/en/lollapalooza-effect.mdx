You have spent several courses meeting the cognitive biases one at a time, in the lab, under glass — **confirmation bias** here, **loss aversion** there, **incentive-caused bias** in its own tidy box. That is exactly how psychology textbooks present them, and it is exactly how they *don't* occur in the wild. In a real bidding war, a real market bubble, a real cult recruitment, a real high-pressure sales close, the biases don't line up politely and take turns. They arrive together, all shoving in the same direction at once — and something happens that studying them one at a time completely hides.

They **multiply.** Charlie Munger, Warren Buffett's partner and the great popular champion of the latticework of mental models, gave this pile-up a deliberately silly name so it would stick: the **Lollapalooza effect**. His claim is precise and a little frightening. When two, three, four or more psychological tendencies all operate in the same direction on the same person at the same time, the result is not the *sum* of their separate pulls. It is far larger — a nonlinear, runaway outcome that none of the biases could have produced alone. Each tendency lowers your resistance to the next; each makes the others bite harder; the whole stack feeds on itself like a reinforcing loop until judgement is simply swamped. That is how sensible people — not fools, *sensible* people — end up paying triple at an auction, buying the top of a bubble, or handing their savings to a charismatic stranger.

This is the **advanced psychology** rung of the site, and it is where the whole thesis of a *latticework* turns back on itself. Everywhere else we have said models are stronger in combination than alone; here that principle becomes the subject. The Lollapalooza effect is what happens when the *biases* combine — the dark mirror of the reader combining *models*. To study it you need the pieces already in hand: **confirmation bias** (so you know how a mind protects a belief once it forms), **incentives** (so you know how reward quietly bends perception), and **loss aversion** (so you feel why the fear of missing out punches so hard). We will add the other great social levers — social proof, commitment and consistency, reciprocation, authority, scarcity, envy, over-optimism — and watch them stack.

Across five teaching lessons you'll drive an interactive **bias stack** where flipping tendencies on one by one makes a pressure meter climb *faster than the sum of the parts* and vault over the line into a frenzy; you'll take three famous disasters apart bias by bias — the open-outcry bidding war, the market mania, the cult and the high-pressure close — and name every force pointing the same way; you'll learn why combination is *multiplicative not additive*, tied straight back to feedback loops and critical mass; and you'll build the only defence that actually works — a written **bias checklist** you run against your own decisions precisely because no single tendency ever announces itself. Then, because a model you can't criticise owns you, you'll learn where the idea *lies*: the hindsight trap of slapping "Lollapalooza!" on everything after the fact, and the fact that biases sometimes *cancel* instead of compounding. By the end you'll spot a stack forming while you're still inside it — the rarest and most valuable moment this whole discipline can give you.
