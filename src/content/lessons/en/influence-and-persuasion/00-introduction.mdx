Imagine a locksmith standing in front of a locked door. To the person watching, the door is a mystery — sealed, opaque, either it opens for you or it doesn't, and whether it does seems like luck or some private knack. The locksmith sees something else entirely. Behind that lock sit five or six little spring-loaded pins, each one a tiny mechanism, and the whole art is knowing that there are only a *handful* of them, that they each move the same way, and which order to press them in. No magic. No charisma. Just a small, finite set of picks and the discipline to use them.

Persuasion is that lock, and this course hands you the picks. The great secret — the one that Robert Cialdini spent three years going undercover in sales, fundraising and advertising training to confirm — is that talking a person into "yes" is not a mysterious gift some people are born with. It runs on a *small control panel* of about seven psychological levers. Every one of them is an ordinary mental shortcut that usually serves you well, and every one of them can be pulled on purpose by someone who knows it's there. A salesperson, a campaigner, a con artist and a genuinely good friend are all reaching for the same handful of switches. Once you can name them, the door stops being a mystery — and the levers stop working on you without your say-so.

## The one idea to take away

Before five lessons take them apart one at a time, here is the whole course compressed to a single line:

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
**Persuasion** is a small, nameable set of about seven psychological levers — **Robert Cialdini's principles of influence** — that reliably move a person to "yes"; each one is a normally-useful mental shortcut (an incentive plus a bias working together) that someone can reach out and pull on purpose.
</Callout>

Two claims are packed in there, and the rest of the course rests on both. The first: the set is **small and finite** — not a thousand tricks but roughly seven, each with a name you'll carry for life. The second, and the reason this is a real skill rather than a party fact: each lever is a **shortcut that normally serves you well**. The pull to repay a favour, to defer to a genuine expert, to copy what everyone around you is doing — these are usually the *smart* move, which is exactly why they're so easy to turn against you. A lever nobody could ever use on you would be a bad heuristic; a good heuristic is precisely the thing a skilled persuader borrows.

## Feel the levers — and feel them backfire

Reading "persuasion runs on a few levers" is one thing. *Working* the control panel is another. Below is a single sales close — one prospect, one deal — with a row of switches, one for each of the seven principles. Flip them on one at a time and watch the meter.

Two markers matter, and they tell the whole story. The **faint upright tick** is the *naive* prediction: what the chance of a yes would be if more tactics only ever helped — if every lever simply added its little share and stacking was pure upside. The **solid bar** is the *real* chance once **reactance** is modelled — the pushback a person feels when they notice they're being worked. With one or two levers on, the tick and the bar sit almost on top of each other: a couple of aligned nudges just quietly help, and the prospect barely notices. Flip on a third and a fourth and the bar climbs past the dashed **threshold** into a genuine yes. But keep going — pile on a fifth, sixth, seventh transparent tactic — and something surprising happens: the target feels *handled*. The machinery becomes obvious ("this is clearly a sales trick"), reactance kicks in, and the solid bar peels away *below* the naive tick and slides into the red. More tactics, less yes.

Carry two things out of that panel. First, **a few aligned levers genuinely persuade**. This is not a course in cynicism — reciprocity, social proof and authority, used honestly, are how trustworthy people actually earn a yes, and the meter rewards them. Second, and less obvious, **persuasion is not monotone in tactics**: the chance of a yes does *not* just keep climbing the more switches you throw. Past a handful, each extra transparent tactic makes the target notice the machinery and push back, so the real chance falls. The person who pulls every lever at once doesn't close harder — they close *worse*, because the target stops seeing an offer and starts seeing a manipulation. The sweet spot lives well short of the far wall.

## Why naming a lever defuses it

Here is the quiet superpower this whole course is really building toward, and it's worth previewing now: **a lever you can name loses most of its grip.** Persuasion levers work best in the dark. Reciprocity does its job when you don't consciously register "I was handed a free sample, so now I feel a pull to buy"; scarcity works when the ticking clock feels like *your* urgency rather than a tactic aimed at you. The instant you can say the lever's name out loud — *ah, that's the scarcity lever* — you've dragged it into the light, and it fights much less well from there.

This gives you a single, portable defence we'll call **inoculation**: name the lever, then ask the one honest question — *"would I still want this if the tactic were removed?"* And it comes with a beautiful built-in test of the offer itself. A **genuine signal survives being named**; a **counterfeit one doesn't**. If the expert really is an expert, noticing that authority is in play changes nothing — you should still listen. If the scarcity is real, seeing the lever doesn't make the last two seats reappear. But if the "expert" is an actor in a lab coat, or the countdown resets the moment it hits zero, naming the lever is exactly what collapses it. The defence and the diagnosis are the same move — which is why lesson 05 spends its whole length on it.

## The map of the course

Five teaching lessons build the control panel one section at a time, then one exam you can't undo. The route:

1. **Reciprocity** — the pull to repay. The free sample, the unexpected favour, the "door-in-the-face" concession — why a gift creates a debt, and how the lever is turned into a tool.
2. **Commitment & Consistency** — the pull to stay true to what we've already said. The "foot-in-the-door", the written pledge, and why a string of small yeses is the runway to a big one.
3. **Social Proof & Authority** — the two levers of *whom we copy and whom we defer to*: canned laughter, "best-seller", the visible queue, plus titles, uniforms and the long shadow of the Milgram experiments.
4. **Liking, Scarcity & Unity** — the closer's favourites: the warmth of similarity and compliments (**liking**), "only 3 left" and the deadline (**scarcity**, loss aversion wearing a price tag), and the shared "we" of **unity**.
5. **Persuasion vs. Manipulation** — the lesson that matters most. Where the honest line sits, how to inoculate yourself by naming the lever, and the test of whether a signal is real or counterfeit.

Then a **Final Exam** — graded, one question at a time, one-way: once you answer, it locks. No back button, no retries, 70% to pass.

### How to use this course

One habit does most of the work: **guess before you peek.** Commit to an answer on every exercise *before* you reveal anything — the small sting of being wrong is what burns the idea in. And play with the compliance meter above until the shape is obvious in your hands: a couple of levers help, a few aligned ones persuade, and too many transparent ones backfire. A model you've *watched* rise, cross the line, and then collapse into the red sticks far better than one you've only read about.
