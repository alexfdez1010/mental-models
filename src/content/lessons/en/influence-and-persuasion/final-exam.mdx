This is the final exam for **Influence & Persuasion**. It pulls the whole course together: the core thesis that persuasion is not charisma or magic but a small, nameable set of psychological **levers** — Robert Cialdini's seven — that reliably move a person from "no" to "yes"; the deeper claim that every lever is really an **incentive and a bias working together**, a shortcut that normally serves us well turned into a tool; and the pile-up you get when several fire at once — the **lollapalooza effect**. You'll be asked to define each principle (**reciprocity**, **commitment and consistency**, **social proof**, **authority**, **liking**, **scarcity**, **unity**), to name its mechanism, to take real scenarios apart lever by lever, and to spot the trap when a tactic is quietly weaponised. You'll be tested on the ethical line that runs through the course — the difference between **persuasion** (the lever points at a genuine reason) and **manipulation** (a counterfeit signal faked to trigger the shortcut) — and on the defence toolkit that inoculates you: name the lever out loud, ask "would I still want this if the tactic were removed?", separate the signal's source from the deal, remember a sales tactic is not a gift, and run a written check before you commit. And, because a model you can't criticise owns you, you'll be tested on where the model **lies** — single-lever over-attribution, the backfire of transparent or over-stacked tactics (reactance), and the line between influence and coercion. Take your time; several questions look easy until you spot the trap — the option that treats a faked signal as a real one, the one that blames a single lever for a whole stack, the one that confuses influence with force, and the one that mistakes "tactic present" for "offer is bad."

<Callout variant="success" title="Key takeaways">
Persuasion is not charisma or magic — it is a small, nameable set of psychological **levers** that reliably move a person toward "yes", and Robert **Cialdini** distilled them into seven: **reciprocity** (repay a gift or concession — the free sample, the door-in-the-face), **commitment and consistency** (the foot-in-the-door, the written or public pledge, low-balling), **social proof** (copy the crowd under uncertainty and similarity — canned laughter, queues, the best-seller label, and its dark side the bystander effect), **authority** (defer to real expertise *and its symbols* — titles, uniforms, the long shadow of **Milgram**), **liking** (similarity, compliments, the halo effect, association), **scarcity** ("only 3 left", deadlines — *loss aversion wearing a price tag*, with **reactance** in the wings), and **unity** (the shared "we" of a common identity). The deep idea is that every lever is an **incentive and a bias working together** — a shortcut that normally serves us well, which is exactly why it pulls and why it is hard to resist — and when several fire at once they don't add, they **multiply**: the **lollapalooza effect**, persuasion's most powerful and most dangerous form. The ethical line is the truth of the signal: **persuasion** points the lever at a genuine reason, while **manipulation** counterfeits the signal (the actor in the lab coat, the fake countdown, the "gift" that is really bait). Because the levers work below awareness, the **defence toolkit** must be deliberate — **name the lever out loud**, ask **"would I still want this if the tactic were removed?"**, separate the signal's source from the deal, remember a sales tactic is not a gift, and run a written check before committing. And keep the model honest about its limits: don't over-attribute a whole stack to one lever, watch tactics **backfire** through reactance when they're transparent or over-stacked, and never confuse **influence with coercion** — the levers persuade but leave the choice with you, so an aligned stack flags *danger*, it does not deliver a *verdict*. Name the lever, and it stops working on you.
</Callout>
