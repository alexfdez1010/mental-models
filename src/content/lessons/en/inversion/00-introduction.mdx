A mathematician named Carl Jacobi had a habit that drove his colleagues a little crazy. Faced with a hard problem, he wouldn't push straight at it. He'd turn it around and attack it from behind. His advice, in three words that have outlived almost everything else he wrote: ***man muss immer umkehren*** — "**invert, always invert**."

A century later the investor Charlie Munger made the same three words his personal motto, and built a reputation for unusual good judgment on a deceptively simple trick: *most problems are easier to solve backward than forward.* That trick is the entire subject of this course.

## The flip, in one breath

Here's the whole idea before we spend five lessons unpacking it. **Inversion** means: instead of asking *"how do I achieve what I want?"*, you ask *"how would I guarantee the opposite — how could I make absolutely sure this fails?"* You list those failure-paths plainly, and then you do the obvious thing: **avoid them**. That's it. You solved the problem by walking in the back door.

Why would that ever beat just thinking forward? Because forward and backward thinking *see different things*. Asking "how do I succeed?" tends to produce a fog of vague, optimistic ideas. Asking "how do I fail?" produces a short, vivid, specific list — because failure modes are concrete and we're weirdly good at imagining disaster. Dodging a clear list of landmines is often far easier than charting a clear path to a hazy summit.

## A tiny example you can feel

Suppose you want to **give a memorable presentation**. Think forward and you get platitudes: "be engaging," "know your material," "tell a story." True, but slippery — you can't *act* on "be engaging."

Now invert. *How would I guarantee a forgettable, painful presentation?* The list writes itself: read every slide word-for-word, cram 200 words onto each slide, run 15 minutes over time, mumble at the floor, open with a five-minute apology for being nervous, use a font nobody past row three can read. That list is **actionable** in a way the forward list never was. Don't do those six things and you've already cleared the bar most presenters trip over. The forward question gave you a vibe; the backward question gave you a checklist.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
Inversion = **ask how you'd guarantee failure, then refuse to do it.** If you remember nothing else from this course, that sentence already makes you better at most decisions. Everything ahead is just learning to pull the move on purpose.
</Callout>

## Why this is a *mental model*, not a party trick

Recall what a mental model is: a compact, reusable way of seeing that you can carry from one situation to another. Inversion qualifies because the flip works *everywhere* — marriages, presentations, engineering, investing, project plans, your own bad habits. It isn't tied to a subject. It's a **question you can ask about anything**, and it reliably surfaces things forward thinking walks straight past. That portability is exactly what makes a thinking tool worth keeping in the kit.

It's also a tool with a *specific job*. Inversion is brilliant at telling you what to **avoid** — and noticeably worse at telling you what to **do**. (A presentation with zero mistakes can still be boring; not-failing isn't the same as winning.) Knowing that boundary is what separates someone who *has heard of* inversion from someone who can actually use it. We'll spend the final lesson there.

## The map of the course

Five short teaching lessons, then one exam you can't undo. The route:

1. **Forward vs. Backward** — the core distinction made precise: what each direction sees, why the backward list comes out sharper, and how to actually perform the flip on any goal.
2. **The Pre-Mortem** — inversion as a planning tool. Before you start, you *imagine the project has already failed* and work out why — surfacing risks while you can still prevent them.
3. **Avoiding Stupidity Beats Seeking Brilliance** — Munger's big claim: in most fields you win more by eliminating dumb mistakes than by chasing genius moves. Plus the famous "how to make a team miserable" flip.
4. **The Anti-Goal Toolkit** — running inversion on yourself and your habits, and stacking it with forward thinking so you both dodge failure *and* aim at something.
5. **When NOT to Invert** — the limits: inversion finds what to avoid, not always what to do, and a pure failure-dodger never actually wins. When to lead with forward thinking instead.

Then a **Final Exam** — graded, one question at a time, one-way: once you answer, it locks. No back button, no retries. You'll be ready.

### How to use this course

One rule does most of the work: **guess before you peek.** When you hit an exercise, commit to an answer in your head before revealing anything. The small sting of being wrong is what makes the idea stick — a smooth, nodding read-through teaches you almost nothing. The exercises *are* the lesson; the prose just sets them up.
