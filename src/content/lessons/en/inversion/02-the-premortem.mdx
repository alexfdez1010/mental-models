You know what a **post-mortem** is: after a project crashes, the team gathers to ask *what killed it?* Useful — but it's an autopsy. The patient is already on the table. Everything you learn arrives too late to save *this* project.

Now apply the flip you learned last lesson to the timing itself. What if you ran the autopsy *before the project started* — while the patient is still healthy and every cause of death is still preventable? That's the **pre-mortem**, and it's the single highest-leverage way to use inversion in real life.

## What a pre-mortem is

A **pre-mortem** is a planning technique, popularised by psychologist Gary Klein, that works like this: at the *start* of a project, before any work begins, you gather the team and say —

> *"Imagine it's a year from now. The project has failed. It was a complete, embarrassing disaster. Now: write down exactly why."*

That's it. You assume failure as a settled fact and reason backward to its causes. Instead of asking the optimistic forward question — "how will this go well?" — you ask the inverted one — "it went terribly; what did it?" And because you're now *imagining a failure that has already happened*, people produce reasons that are startlingly specific and honest.

<Callout variant="info" title="Pre-mortem vs. post-mortem">
A **post-mortem** dissects a failure *after* it happens — too late to help the deceased. A **pre-mortem** dissects an *imagined* failure *before* you start — early enough to prevent every cause it surfaces. Same autopsy, moved to where it can still save a life.
</Callout>

## Why imagining failure beats listing risks

"But we already do a risk assessment," you might say. Here's the subtle, powerful difference. A normal risk meeting asks: *"what could go wrong?"* — a hypothetical, and our optimism quietly files most answers under "unlikely, won't happen to us." The pre-mortem asks something psychologically different: *"it HAS gone wrong — why?"* By treating failure as a certainty that already occurred, it gives people *permission to be pessimistic* and defeats two biases at once:

- **Optimism bias** — our tendency to assume things will go well. Hard to sustain when the prompt insists the project is already a smoking ruin.
- **Groupthink / the silent doubter** — in a hopeful kickoff, the person with a nagging worry stays quiet to avoid being "the negative one." The pre-mortem makes voicing doom the *assignment*, so the quiet doubt finally gets said out loud — often the doubt that would have sunk the thing.

This second effect is the secret weapon. Klein's insight was that somebody on the team usually *already senses* the fatal flaw — they just have no socially safe way to raise it. The pre-mortem is that safe way.

## How to run one — the five steps

The pre-mortem is a repeatable drill, not a mood:

1. **Set the scene.** Gather the people who'll do the work. State the plan, then declare: *"It's [future date]. This project has failed completely."*
2. **Everyone writes failures silently, first.** Each person independently lists why it failed — *silent and solo* so the loudest voice or the boss's opinion can't anchor the room.
3. **Collect every cause out loud.** Go around and gather them all, no debating yet. You want the widest net, including the awkward ones.
4. **Cluster and rank.** Group similar causes and sort by *likelihood × damage*. A failure that's both probable and fatal jumps the queue.
5. **Turn the top causes into safeguards.** For each serious cause, decide a concrete action now: a check, an owner, a change to the plan. This is the negate-into-a-safeguard step from lesson 2, applied to a whole project.

<Reveal label="Worked pre-mortem: launching a mobile app" buttonLabel="Run the worked pre-mortem">

**The plan:** Ship a new mobile app in three months.

**The scene:** "It's a year later. The app launched and flopped. Why?"

**Causes the team writes down (silent, then collected):**
- Onboarding was so confusing that most users quit on the first screen.
- A key feature depended on a third-party service that throttled us at launch.
- We built features nobody asked for and skipped the one thing reviewers wanted.
- The app crashed on older phones we never tested.
- Marketing peaked a week *before* the app was actually in the stores.

**Rank by likelihood × damage, then convert the top ones to safeguards now:**
- *Confusing onboarding* (likely + fatal) → run five usability tests on the first-run flow before launch; assign an owner.
- *Third-party throttling* (medium + fatal) → confirm rate limits in writing and build a fallback; owner + deadline.
- *Crashes on old phones* (likely + damaging) → add the three most common old devices to the test matrix.

You haven't built anything yet — and you've already defused the three things most likely to kill the launch. *That* is inversion paying rent.

</Reveal>

## The same flip, scaled up

Notice the pre-mortem is *literally* the lesson-2 recipe applied to a whole project across time:

| Flip recipe (lesson 2) | Pre-mortem (this lesson) |
|---|---|
| State the goal | State the project plan |
| Invert to the opposite | "Imagine it has completely failed" |
| List the failure paths | Everyone writes why it failed |
| Negate each into a safeguard | Convert top causes into actions now |
| Act on the avoid-list | Build the project with mines pre-cleared |

Same engine — *ask how it fails, then prevent that* — just aimed at a plan instead of a single decision. That's what makes inversion a *model* rather than a one-off trick: the identical move rescales from "how do I word this email" to "how do we run a year-long programme."

## A pitfall: the pre-mortem that becomes a panic attack

Inversion has a shadow side, and the pre-mortem is where it first shows up. If you list failure modes and then *keep listing them forever*, you don't get a safer project — you get **paralysis**. A team that surfaces forty doom scenarios and treats all forty as equally urgent will never start, drowning in defensive busywork against risks that were never going to happen.

The fix is the **rank** step, and it's not optional. You are not trying to prevent *every* conceivable failure — you're trying to prevent the few that are both *likely* and *damaging*. A meteor could hit the office; you do not need a meteor contingency. Triage by likelihood × impact, fund the top few, and consciously accept the long tail of trivial risks. Inversion surfaces the list; *judgment* decides which items earn a response.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Surface, then triage — don't drown">
A pre-mortem that ends with “here are 40 ways we could fail” and no ranking has failed at its own job. The point isn’t to fear everything; it’s to find the two or three failure modes worth real effort and let the rest go. Inversion finds the candidates; triage picks the targets.
</Callout>
