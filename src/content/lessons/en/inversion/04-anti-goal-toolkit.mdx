You can now flip a goal, run a pre-mortem, and build an avoid-list for a whole field. This lesson points the tool at the most stubborn target there is — *your own decisions* — and then assembles everything into a repeatable two-direction routine. The goal is to leave you not with a clever idea about inversion, but with a habit you actually run.

## Anti-goals: deciding what you refuse to do

Forward goal-setting is everywhere: "I want to grow the business," "I want to get fit." Useful, but soft — you can drift for years feeling like you're pursuing a goal while quietly walking into the exact traps that kill it. An **anti-goal** is the inverted twin of a goal: an explicit statement of the outcomes you will *refuse to allow*, written sharply enough to act on.

The trick is that anti-goals are usually **more concrete and more enforceable** than goals. "Have a balanced life" is a wish. "Never schedule a meeting before 9am or after 6pm, and never work a third consecutive weekend" is an anti-goal — you can *check* whether you broke it. Goals describe a hazy destination; anti-goals draw bright lines you'll notice yourself crossing.

<Callout variant="info" title="Goal vs. anti-goal">
A **goal** states the outcome you want ("build a profitable business"). An **anti-goal** states the outcomes you'll refuse to tolerate on the way ("no clients who email at midnight, no work that needs me on holiday, no month where I can't make payroll"). Anti-goals are bright lines — easy to monitor, hard to rationalise past.
</Callout>

A well-known version comes from entrepreneurs who design a business *backward from the life they don't want*. Instead of "build a successful agency," they write the anti-goals first: no exhausting commute, no rude clients, no back-to-back calls all day, no dependence on one big customer. Then they build a business that structurally *cannot* produce those outcomes. The anti-goals shape the design more usefully than any forward vision board could.

## Inverting your own decisions

The same flip defuses individual choices. Before a meaningful decision, instead of only asking "what's the upside if this goes well?", ask the inverted question: **"how could this decision blow up, and what would *I* have done to cause it?"** That last clause matters — it points at the failure modes *within your control*, which are the ones you can actually prevent.

Run it as a personal pre-mortem:

1. **Name the decision.** "I'm about to take this new job / sign this lease / start this relationship."
2. **Assume it went badly.** "It's two years later and this was a clear mistake. Why?"
3. **List your contributing errors honestly.** "I ignored the red flags in the interview / overstretched my budget / dismissed my friends' warnings."
4. **Set safeguards or change the decision.** Add a check, negotiate a term, or walk away — *before* you're committed.

<Reveal label="Worked example: should I take this exciting job offer?" buttonLabel="Run the personal pre-mortem">

**Forward view (seductive):** Bigger title, more money, impressive logo. All upside.

**Inverted view:** "It's two years on and taking this job was a clear mistake. What did I miss?"
- I never asked *why the last person left* — turns out it's a meat-grinder role.
- The 'more money' barely covers a brutal commute I didn't price in.
- I'd be reporting to a manager I got a bad feeling about and ignored.
- The impressive logo hides a team in quiet crisis.

**Safeguards before deciding:** Ask why the role is open. Compute the *real* hourly pay including commute. Talk to someone who left. Push for one more conversation with the manager. None of these kill the opportunity — they stop a forward glow from hiding a backward-obvious disaster.

</Reveal>

## Now stack the two directions

Here's where the whole course clicks together. Inversion is powerful, but you've heard the warning three times now: **it tells you what to avoid, not what to chase.** A life or project built *only* on anti-goals is a beautifully defended fortress with nothing inside. The mature move is to run *both directions* on the same goal:

| Step | Direction | Question |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Aim | Forward | "What do I actually want here? What does success look like?" |
| 2. Invert | Backward | "How would I guarantee the opposite? What are the failure modes?" |
| 3. Safeguard | Backward → action | "Negate each failure into something I'll avoid or check." |
| 4. Advance | Forward | "With the mines cleared, what's my best move *toward* the goal?" |

Forward picks the destination; backward removes the ways you'd never arrive; forward again chooses the route. Skip step 1 or 4 and you're a flawless failure-dodger going nowhere. Skip step 2 or 3 and you're an optimist marching cheerfully onto a landmine. You need the *round trip*.

## The pitfall in this lesson: turning inversion into anxiety

Pointed at yourself, inversion has a specific failure mode: it can curdle into **chronic pessimism and self-criticism**. If every decision becomes a catalogue of everything that could go wrong with *you*, you don't get wiser — you get anxious and paralysed, too busy rehearsing disaster to act.

The discipline is the same as the pre-mortem's triage, plus a hard stop: invert to *surface* the real, controllable failure modes, convert the serious ones into safeguards, and then **close the inversion and switch to forward motion**. Inversion is a phase you pass *through*, not a place you live. You flip, you fix, you act. A thinker who only ever inverts has mistaken a stress-test for a way of life.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Invert, then close the loop">
Inversion is a tool you pick up and put down. Surface the failure modes, build the safeguards, and then *consciously stop* and aim forward. If you’re still listing doom after the safeguards are set, you’ve stopped thinking and started worrying — flip back to forward and move.
</Callout>

## Where this goes next

You can now run inversion on yourself — anti-goals, personal pre-mortems — and, crucially, stack it with forward thinking in a round trip so you aim *and* defend.

One lesson remains, and it's the most important guardrail in the whole course. We've said repeatedly that inversion finds what to avoid, not what to do — and that some games genuinely reward brilliance over caution. The final teaching lesson makes that boundary precise: exactly **when NOT to invert**, where the flip misleads, and how to tell a problem that wants backward thinking from one that demands you charge forward.
