This is the final exam for **Preference Falsification**. It pulls together the whole course: the gap that opens between what people privately believe and what they publicly profess when honesty is costly; the three payoffs that break revealed preference; the cost of dissent as the engine, the dictator's paradox, and the pluralistic ignorance that lets a private majority feel like a lonely minority; the preference cascade that flips a rock-solid consensus overnight (and why 1989 blindsided everyone); the persistence trap that freezes unloved norms for years and rots the very knowledge leaders reason from; and finally the model's limits, its self-serving misuses, and the cure — institutions that lower the price of speaking honestly. Several questions look easy until you notice a trap: that a quiet room proves a hidden majority, or that measured consensus equals real consensus. Reason each one through.

<Callout variant="success" title="Key takeaways">
Preference falsification is the wedge between what people **privately believe** and
what they **publicly profess** when honesty is costly — and it is a *gap*, not a
conversion, so it quietly breaks the assumption that behavior reveals preference.
The **cost of dissent** is the engine: crank it up and you get the **dictator's
paradox** (loud, hollow, untrustworthy agreement) and **pluralistic ignorance** (a
private majority each member mistakes for a lonely minority, because the objection is
mutual knowledge but never common knowledge). Hidden preferences stack up behind
personal thresholds, which is why a small drop in the cost — or a few brave first
movers — can trigger a **cascade** that flips an "eternal" consensus in weeks, as it
did across the **Eastern Bloc in 1989**, invisibly, right up until it happened. The
same silence that makes collapse sudden is what makes bad norms **persist** for
years, and worse, it **distorts the shared knowledge** everyone — leaders
included — reasons from. But hold the model honestly: not every silence hides a
majority, "everyone secretly agrees with me" is a seductive excuse, falsification runs
both ways, and measured opinion is noisy. The cure is always the same move — build
**institutions that lower the price of honesty**: secret ballots, anonymity, red
teams, premortems, and leaders who speak last. *Watch the cost of dissent, and never
mistake a quiet room for a settled one.*
</Callout>
