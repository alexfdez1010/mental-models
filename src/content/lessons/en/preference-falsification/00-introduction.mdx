Picture a team meeting. The lead unveils a plan. You think it's a mistake — and,
glancing around, you're pretty sure Priya thinks so too, and maybe Marcus. But
nobody says anything. Priya is watching *you* stay quiet and reading it as
agreement. Marcus is watching Priya. Everyone privately doubts the plan, and
everyone publicly nods, and the plan sails through 9–0. Not one of those nine
votes is a lie exactly — each person just said the thing that felt *safe* to say
out loud.

That gap — between the opinion you **hold** and the opinion you **voice** — is the
whole subject of this course. When speaking your mind is costly, you tend to state
the view you think is rewarded, not the one you actually believe. Do that at scale
and something strange happens: the group's *public* consensus can drift miles away
from its *private* one. A room, a company, or an entire country can loudly agree
on something that, behind everyone's eyes, almost nobody actually thinks.

That is **preference falsification** — a term coined by the economist **Timur
Kuran** — and it is one of the highest-leverage models you can carry, because once
you see it you stop trusting the *surface* of agreement and start asking the only
question that matters: *how much of this consensus is real, and how much is
everyone copying everyone else's silence?*

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
Preference falsification is what happens when it's costly to say what you think —
so people voice the safe opinion instead of the true one, and the public consensus
becomes a mirror that almost nobody privately believes.
</Callout>

## See it in one picture

Here is the entire model on one board. Every square is a person. Most of this
crowd *privately* disagrees with the status quo — but each person will only speak
up once they see enough *others* already dissenting. Because almost nobody wants to
be first, the public face of the crowd is a near-unanimous grey: silence read as
agreement.

The trick is the **Reveal** button. In the public view, a silent dissenter and a
true believer look *identical* — both just a quiet grey square. Reveal the private
preferences and the illusion breaks: most of that calm grey was opposition, hiding.
That invisible gap between the public face and the private truth is what the rest
of this course is about — how it opens, why it's so stable, and how it can vanish
in an afternoon.

## What you'll walk away with

By the end you'll be able to look at any loud consensus and gauge how *load-bearing*
it really is — whether it's built on genuine shared belief or on everyone
performing a safe opinion. Here's the map:

1. **Two preferences, one voice** — the exact split between your private and public
   preference, why the gap opens, and the difference between falsifying (hiding a
   belief) and genuinely being persuaded.
2. **Why silence has a price** — how the *cost of dissent* is the engine, why
   repression can *raise* measured agreement while gutting real support, and the
   trap of **pluralistic ignorance**, where everyone believes they're the lonely
   dissenter.
3. **The dam breaks** — the cascade: how a small drop in the cost, or a few brave
   first movers, can flip a rock-solid consensus overnight (with the 1989 collapse
   of the Eastern Bloc as the classic case).
4. **The persistence trap** — the model's dark side: how falsification freezes
   unloved norms, censorship, and bad policies in place for years, and how it
   quietly corrupts what everyone — including the people in charge — can even know.
5. **Where the model lies** — its limits and misuses: not every silence hides a
   majority, and "everyone secretly agrees with me" is the most seductive abuse of
   the whole idea.

## How to use this course

Every lesson opens with a quick guess, teaches the idea through a concrete story,
and checks that it stuck. Don't skip the guesses — trying to answer *before* you
know is one of the most reliable ways to actually remember. You'll drive the crowd
simulator a few more times, from different angles, and meet a couple of sorting and
matching exercises along the way.

This is an **expert-tier** course, so it leans on a few models you've ideally met
already. You'll get far more from it if you're comfortable with **incentives** (the
rewards and punishments that make honesty costly in the first place), **information
cascades** (people reading each other's *actions* as evidence and copying them),
and **common knowledge** (the difference between everyone privately knowing
something and everyone knowing that everyone knows). When you've finished the five
teaching lessons, a graded final exam pulls it all together — it's one-way, so once
you submit an answer it's locked.

<Callout variant="warning" title="One habit to build as you go">
Whenever you meet a striking unanimity — a poll at 95%, a room in total agreement,
a norm "everyone" supports — train yourself to ask not *"what do they all believe?"*
but *"how expensive would it be to say the opposite here?"* The costlier dissent is,
the less that agreement can be trusted. That single question is what this course
teaches you to ask.
</Callout>
