There is a particular kind of disaster that needs no villain. No greed beyond the
ordinary, no malice, no stupidity — just a group of perfectly reasonable people,
each doing the sensible thing for themselves, who together walk straight off a
cliff that every one of them could see coming. Overfished seas. Drained
groundwater. A warming sky. A shared kitchen where the sink is always full of
someone else's dishes. These look like failures of character, and we reliably
explain them that way — *people are selfish, people are short-sighted, people just
don't care.* But there is a far better explanation, and it sits in the **structure**
of the situation, not in anyone's heart. It's called the **tragedy of the commons**,
and once you see it, you'll see it everywhere.

Here's the idea in one breath. A **commons** is any resource that's shared and open
— a pasture anyone can graze, an ocean anyone can fish, an atmosphere anyone can
dump carbon into. When you take from a commons, you get the **whole benefit** of
what you took, but you pay only a **small fraction** of the cost, because the damage
is spread across everyone who shares it. That lopsided arithmetic — *private gain,
shared cost* — quietly rewards everyone for taking more, and taking more, until the
resource collapses. The tragedy isn't that people are bad. It's that they're
responding, rationally, to a structure that makes ruin the sum of sensible choices.

## The one sentence to take away

Before we spend four lessons unpacking it, here's the whole model in a line:

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
**When a shared resource is free to take but its cost is split among everyone,
each person is rewarded for overusing it — so individually rational choices add up
to collective ruin.** The pasture isn't destroyed by villains; it's destroyed by
arithmetic. To fix it, don't preach against selfishness — *change who bears the
cost of taking.*
</Callout>

The seductive wrong move is to reach for character. If the village pasture is
stripped bare, blame the greedy herders; if the fishery collapses, blame the
greedy trawlers. Sometimes there's genuine greed in the mix. But character is a
weak explanation here, because the trap catches *ordinary, decent people too* —
and a worse lever, because you can't fix a structural problem by asking everyone to
be nicer. Change the structure — who owns the resource, who pays for the damage,
who's allowed to take how much — and the "greed" quietly evaporates, the same
people grazing the same cows on a pasture that now stays green.

## Why this is a real model, not just "people are selfish"

"People overuse shared things" sounds like plain common sense — until you notice
how badly the common-sense version explains the world. If it were really about
selfishness, then nicer, more cooperative people in nicer cultures would never
overgraze, overfish, or pollute. But they do, reliably, whenever the structure is a
commons — and the *same* people will carefully conserve a resource they privately
own or jointly govern. That's the tell that we're looking at a structural force,
not a moral one. The tragedy is produced by the **shape of the incentives**, which
you met in the Incentives course: each actor's payoff rewards taking, because the
gain lands on them and the cost lands on everyone.

And like every good mental model, it's **portable**. The same question — *who keeps
the benefit, and who pays the cost?* — explains a medieval pasture, a collapsing
cod fishery, a traffic jam, a draining aquifer, a course of antibiotics that breeds
resistant bacteria, and a spam-clogged inbox. None of these need villains. They
need a resource that's shared, a benefit that's private, and a cost that's smeared
thin enough that no one feels it — and ordinary people walking, sensibly, toward
the cliff. Once you can see that structure, the disaster stops being mysterious and
starts being *predictable* — which is the whole point of a model.

## The map of the course

Four short teaching lessons, then one exam you can't undo. The route:

1. **The Parable & the Structure** — the original story (herders sharing a pasture,
   each adding one more cow), pulled apart into its general engine: a shared
   resource, a *private benefit* to taking, and a *socialized cost*. Why the
   arithmetic guarantees overuse, and how to recognize the structure stripped of
   the cows.
2. **A Trap, Not a Vice** — why each actor is behaving *rationally*, which is
   exactly what makes it a trap. The precise idea of an **externality** (a cost the
   decider doesn't pay) at its heart, the tight link to the **prisoner's dilemma**,
   and the crucial limit: when a shared resource is *not* a tragedy.
3. **The Commons Around Us** — the same machine in five disguises: overfishing,
   antibiotic resistance, traffic congestion, groundwater, the atmosphere, and
   spam. You'll drive an interactive simulator that collapses a shared fishery as
   you add boats — then watch a governance rule save it — and work the real numbers
   behind a fishery that takes more than it regrows.
4. **Escaping the Tragedy** — the ways out, and where each works: Elinor Ostrom's
   Nobel-winning rules for how communities actually govern commons, **property
   rights**, **regulation and quotas**, **Pigouvian taxes** that price the cost back
   in, and **social norms** — plus an honest look at where each one fails.

Then a **Final Exam** — graded, one question at a time, one-way: once you answer,
it locks. No back button, no retries. You'll be ready.

### How to use this course

One rule does most of the work: **guess before you peek.** When you hit an
exercise, commit to an answer before revealing anything. The small sting of being
wrong is what makes the idea stick — a smooth, nodding read-through teaches almost
nothing. The exercises *are* the lesson; the prose just sets them up.
