In the last lesson you watched a switch flip and a rival back off. Satisfying — but *magic* until you can explain exactly why it works. This lesson is the machinery. By the end you'll be able to say, precisely, why a rational opponent is *more* afraid of you when you have *fewer* choices, and you'll have a repeatable method for spotting these situations in the wild.

The engine has a name: **backward induction**. When one player moves and then another responds, a smart player doesn't reason forwards ("here's what I'd like to happen"). They reason *backwards*: "If I make this move, what will they do in response? And knowing that, what should I do now?" You look ahead to the end of the game and reason back to the start. It's the single most important habit in sequential strategy, and commitment is what happens when you use it against yourself — on purpose.

## The game, one move at a time

Here's our situation again, laid out as a sequence. There are two players and two decision points:

1. **First, the Rival chooses:** *Challenge* you, or *Stay away*.
2. **Then, if challenged, You choose:** *Fight* (a bruising war that's costly for both) or *Yield* (fold, accommodate, cut your losses).

And here are the payoffs that make it interesting (higher is better for that player):

| Outcome | Your payoff | Rival's payoff |
|---|---|---|
| Rival stays away | **10** | 4 |
| Rival challenges, you **yield** | 5 | 7 |
| Rival challenges, you **fight** | 2 | 1 |

Read the story in those numbers. You do best when the rival never shows up (10) — you keep the whole prize. If they challenge and you *fight*, it's a disaster for both of you (you 2, them 1) — war is expensive. If they challenge and you *yield*, you've lost ground but avoided the costly fight (you 5), while they've grabbed a nice chunk of your prize (7).

Now let's reason backwards, the way the rival does.

<Reveal client:visible>
**Step 1 — What will you do if challenged?** (Reason about the last move first.) Look only at the second decision point, where you've *already* been challenged. Your two options are Fight (payoff **2**) or Yield (payoff **5**). Five beats two. So a rational you, once challenged, will **yield** — a costly fight leaves you worse off than folding. Your fists are up, but everyone can do the arithmetic: you'd rather not swing.
</Reveal>

<Reveal client:visible>
**Step 2 — So what will the rival do?** (Reason back to the first move.) The rival looks ahead to Step 1 and sees it clearly: "If I challenge, they'll yield, and I'll get **7**. If I stay away, I get **4**." Seven beats four. So the rival **challenges** — confident you'll fold. The game lands on *(challenge, yield)*: you get **5**, they get **7**. You've been pushed around, and it was perfectly rational for them to push, because your own best response gave them the green light.
</Reveal>

That's the ugly resting point when you keep all your options. Your *freedom to yield* is precisely what the rival exploits. They're not gambling — they've read your incentives and know you'll fold. Now watch what one act of self-sabotage does.

## Burning the option

Suppose that *before the game starts*, you do something drastic and public: you **destroy your own ability to yield**. You sign a contract that fines you a fortune if you back down. You stake your public reputation on fighting. You hand the decision to a lieutenant under strict orders to fight no matter what. However you do it, the *Yield* branch is now gone — if challenged, you *will* fight, whether you like it or not.

Run backward induction again on this new game:

<Reveal client:visible>
**Redo Step 1 — What will you do if challenged now?** You have only one option left: **Fight** (payoff 2). The comfortable Yield-for-5 is gone — you burned it. So if challenged, the game reaches the fight outcome: you 2, rival **1**.
</Reveal>

<Reveal client:visible>
**Redo Step 2 — So what does the rival do now?** The rival looks ahead again: "If I challenge, they can only fight, and I get **1**. If I stay away, I get **4**." Now four beats one. So the rival **stays away** — challenging has become a losing move. The game lands on *stay away*: you get **10**, they get 4.
</Reveal>

<Callout variant="tip" title="The whole paradox in two numbers">
By keeping your options open, you land on **5**. By destroying an option — the very thing that looks like self-harm — you land on **10**. And notice the beautiful part: at the final outcome, *you never fight*. The threat of fighting was enough. A weapon that works is a weapon you never have to fire.
</Callout>

Your payoff **doubled** because you made yourself *less* free. That's not a trick of these particular numbers — it's the deep structure. When your soft option (yield) is what tempts the rival to attack, removing it removes the temptation. You didn't change your strength, your resources, or your luck. You changed *what the rival could predict about you*, and that was enough to move the equilibrium.

Three discoveries are hiding in those sliders. Go find each one:

- **When commitment is genius.** At the default settings (your fight payoff below your yield payoff, the rival's fight payoff below their stay-away), burning your retreat converts a challenge into a stay-away and your payoff leaps. This is the sweet spot: you'd *otherwise* be tempted to fold, and the fight genuinely hurts the rival.
- **When commitment is pointless.** Drag *your* fight payoff *above* 5 (above your yield payoff). Now fighting is already your best response — you'd fight even with the yield option available — so your threat is credible *without* committing. The rival stays away either way. Tying your hands adds nothing; you never needed to.
- **When commitment is a trap.** Put your fight payoff back low, then drag the *rival's* fight payoff *above* 4 (above their stay-away option). Now the fight doesn't hurt them enough to deter — they challenge even when you're committed. Uncommitted, you could have yielded and salvaged 5; committed, you're locked into a fight for a miserable 2. You've handcuffed yourself to a disaster.

That third case is the one to remember, and we'll return to it at the end of the course. **A commitment is only as good as the threat behind it, and a threat only deters if carrying it out genuinely hurts the other side.** Burning a bridge when the enemy can't be stopped anyway doesn't scare them — it just guarantees you can't escape.

## The subtle engine: it's about *their* beliefs, not your strength

Step back and notice what actually moved. You didn't get stronger. Your fight payoff was 2 before and 2 after. Your resources, your army, your bank balance — unchanged. The *only* thing that changed was that the rival could no longer count on you folding. **Commitment operates entirely through the other side's expectations.** It's a psychological weapon aimed at a rational mind: you rearrange your own incentives so that the other player, reasoning correctly about you, reaches a conclusion that's good for you.

This is why a commitment must be **visible** and **believed** to work at all. Cortés burning his ships in secret would have deterred no one — the whole point was that everyone *saw* the smoke. A binding contract locked in a drawer that the rival never learns about changes nothing. The commitment has to reach into the rival's head and change what they predict. Which raises the question that makes or breaks every single commitment, and the subject of the entire next lesson: **how do you make the other side actually believe you?**

<Callout variant="success" title="Key takeaways">
Sequential strategy runs on **backward induction**: a rational player looks ahead to what you'll actually do and reasons back to what they should do now. When you keep your options open, your **soft option** (yield) is exactly what a rival exploits — they predict you'll fold and challenge you. **Removing** that soft option, in advance and in public, rewires their best response: now they foresee a fight they'd lose and stay away, so your payoff rises and *you never have to fight*. But the sliders taught the boundaries: commitment is **genius** when you'd otherwise fold and the fight stings the rival, **pointless** when your threat is already credible, and a **trap** when the threat can't hurt the rival enough to deter them — then you've only handcuffed yourself. Commitment works through the rival's **expectations**, not your strength, which is why it must be visible and believed.
</Callout>
