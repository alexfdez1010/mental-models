In 1519, Hernán Cortés landed on the coast of Mexico with a few hundred men, and before marching inland he reportedly gave an order that sounds like the ravings of a lunatic: *scuttle the ships.* Sink his own fleet. Destroy the only way home. From a certain angle this is the worst decision in the history of decisions — he has just annihilated his own options, thrown away the lifeline, guaranteed that if things go badly there is no escape. Every management consultant in history would have fainted.

And yet it may have been the single most calculated move he made. Think about what it did to the *minds of his own men*, and to anyone watching. Retreat was now physically impossible. There was no "let's fall back and regroup," no quiet option to slink home if the going got hard. Win or die. A soldier who *cannot* run fights differently from one who can, and an enemy that knows this reckons differently too. Cortés didn't burn the ships to hurt himself. He burned them to **change what everyone else expected him to do** — and expectations, it turns out, are the whole game.

## The paradox, named

Here is the strange shape you just watched, stated plainly:

> **Deliberately destroying your own options can make you better off — because it changes what a rival expects you to do, and therefore what they do.**

That is the paradox of **commitment**, and it runs against every instinct you have. We spend our lives collecting options, keeping our choices open, avoiding anything irreversible. Flexibility is treasure. And *usually* it is. But in a strategic situation — one where your best move depends on someone else's move, and theirs on yours — flexibility can be a *liability*. The freedom to back down is exactly what invites the other side to push. Take that freedom away, visibly, and you can stop the push before it starts.

Three words carry this whole course, so let's define them once, cleanly:

- A **commitment** is a move that deliberately restricts your own future freedom — you close a door, give something up, or bind yourself to a course of action *in advance* so you can no longer freely change your mind.
- A commitment is **credible** when the other side genuinely believes you'll follow through — because you've arranged matters so that following through is now your *only* option, or plainly your *best* one. An unbelievable commitment is just talk.
- **Deterrence** is the payoff: a rival, reckoning with what you're now committed to do, decides *not* to do the thing you didn't want — the attack, the price war, the challenge — and so it never happens.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
A **credible commitment** is a move that ties your own hands *on purpose* — and it wins not by giving you more power to act, but by changing what a rival believes you will do, so that they change what *they* do. Fewer options, better outcome.
</Callout>

## Feel it in your hands

Reading about this paradox is one thing; watching the equilibrium move is another. Below is the whole logic on a two-move game. A **Rival** decides whether to challenge you — invade your market, contest your turf. If they challenge, **You** decide whether to *fight* (costly for both) or *yield* (fold and cut your losses). Crucially, the rival is not stupid: they *look ahead* to what you'll actually do before they decide.

Play it first with the switch **off** — your retreat intact. Then flip it **on** to burn your retreat, throwing away the option to yield. Watch what happens to who challenges, and to your payoff.

## Why commitment earns a place in the latticework

- **It inverts the value of freedom.** Almost every other model treats options as good and irreversibility as bad. Commitment is the sharp exception: in the right strategic spot, *fewer* options make you stronger, because a rival can no longer count on you backing down. Knowing when freedom flips from asset to liability is a rare and valuable instinct.
- **It's a lever on other people's choices.** You can't directly control what a rival does. But you *can* control your own options — and by visibly changing them, you change the rival's best response. Commitment is one of the few clean handles you have on someone else's behaviour.
- **It reveals that talk is cheap.** Once you see strategy through this lens, you stop taking threats and promises at face value and start asking the only question that matters: *is it credible — has the other side actually made following through their best or only move?* That single question cuts through an enormous amount of bluster, in negotiations, politics, and your own life.

## Where this sits on the ladder

This is an **expert** course, and it leans on two you should have already:

- From **Nash equilibrium**, the idea of a stable resting point where no one gains by moving alone — and the master move of *changing the game* rather than lecturing the players. A commitment is exactly a way to relocate the equilibrium by changing what your rival's best response has to reckon with.
- From **mechanism design**, the idea that a cleverly chosen *rule* steers self-interested behaviour to where you want it. A commitment is a rule you impose *on yourself*, in advance, to steer the person across the table.

If those feel shaky, they're worth a refresher — everything here is built on top of them.

## The map of the course

Six teaching lessons build the model floor to ceiling, then one exam locks it in:

1. **The Paradox of Fewer Options** — the core mechanism in slow motion: how tying your hands changes a rival's best response, worked through the game you just played.
2. **Credibility Is Everything** — the make-or-break ingredient, and the full toolkit for manufacturing it: contracts, penalties, public promises, delegation, and the automatic "doomsday machine".
3. **Threats and Promises** — sticks and carrots, and the crucial split between **deterrence** (stopping an action) and **compellence** (forcing one) — and why a promise needs credibility just as badly as a threat.
4. **The Threat That Leaves Something to Chance** — brinkmanship: deliberately raising a shared risk of disaster to make the other side blink, from the game of chicken to the Cuban Missile Crisis.
5. **The Balance of Terror** — deterrence at its most extreme: mutual assured destruction, second-strike capability, and the unsettling logic by which being *vulnerable* can be the destabilising thing.
6. **Where the Bridge Burns You** — commitment devices in everyday life, and an honest reckoning with the failure modes: bluffs that get called, and the **commitment trap** of locking yourself into a disaster you can no longer escape.

Then a **Final Exam** — graded, one question at a time, one-way: once you answer, it locks. No back button, no retries, 70% to pass.
