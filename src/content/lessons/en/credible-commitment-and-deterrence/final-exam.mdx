This is the whole course in one sitting. Every idea you've met — the paradox that destroying your own options can strengthen you, the backward induction a rival uses to look ahead at what you'll actually do, the credibility test that separates a real commitment from cheap talk, the toolkit that manufactures belief (contracts, burned bridges, reputation, delegation, automaticity), the split between threats and promises and between deterrence and compellence, the brinkmanship that leaves something to chance, the balance of terror that makes mutual vulnerability stabilising, and the failure modes where a burned bridge burns *you* — shows up here as questions. Hold the through-line in your head: a commitment works by changing what a rival believes you will do, so it only works when it is **credible**, and only helps when the influence it buys is worth the freedom it costs. Take a breath. There is no going back once you commit — a fitting test for a course about the choices no one can unmake.

<Callout variant="success" title="Key takeaways">
You now hold the whole model. The **paradox**: destroying your own options can make you stronger, because a commitment changes what a rival *expects* — it works through their **backward induction**, not your strength, so it must be **visible and credible**. The **credibility test** asks whether following through is your best move when the moment comes; **cheap talk** fails it, and you manufacture belief with **contracts and penalties, burning the option, reputation, delegation, and automaticity**. Commitments come as **threats** (sticks) and **promises** (carrots) — both need credibility, since a promise invites the temptation to **renege**. **Deterrence** stops an action (keep the status quo); **compellence** forces one (change it), and is harder because it demands visible, deadlined, face-losing compliance. When a threat is too big to believe, **brinkmanship** substitutes a *risk* of catastrophe you keep ratcheting — "the threat that leaves something to chance," from **chicken** to the **Cuban Missile Crisis**. At the extreme, **mutual assured destruction** deters a first strike so long as both keep a survivable **second-strike capability** — and the **paradox of vulnerability** means mutual exposure is *stabilising* while perfect defences and disarming weapons destabilise. Finally, stay honest about where a burned bridge **burns you**: bluffs get called, the **commitment trap** and **sunk-cost escalation** lock you into disasters, doomsday devices fire by accident, and rigid commitments cage you when the world changes. Commit only when the influence gained beats the flexibility lost, the threat genuinely bites, and the world can see it.
</Callout>
