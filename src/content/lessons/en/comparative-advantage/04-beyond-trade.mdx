So far we've watched **comparative advantage** play out on a big stage: countries, factories, two goods, gains from trade that grow the pie. Nadia the \$400/hr lawyer who still lets Owen do the typing. Maya and Sam splitting the work. That's the headline act.

But here's the secret the economists buried in the footnotes: **the model scales down.** The exact same engine that tells nations who should make the wine and who should make the cloth also tells *you* whether to mow your own lawn. Same logic. Smaller machine.

The core question never changes: **of all the things I could spend this hour on, what do I give up the *least* by doing this one?** That's your **opportunity cost** — the value of the best thing you *didn't* do. Comparative advantage says specialize where that cost is lowest, then hand off the rest. It applies whether "I" is a country, a team, a company, or a person staring at a to-do list at 9pm. Let's shrink the machine all the way down.

## On a team

The most expensive management mistake in the world sounds like good sense: *"Give the task to whoever's best at it."*

That's **absolute advantage** thinking, and we already know it's a trap. The right question isn't "who's best?" — it's "who gives up the least to do this?"

Picture a software team. **Priya** is a senior engineer. She is, annoyingly, the best person on the team at *both* designing system architecture *and* writing tests. Pure absolute advantage, both tasks. The naive rule says: Priya does everything she's best at. The naive rule is wrong.

Here's why. Priya is the *only* person who can do the architecture — it needs her experience, her context, her judgment. **Devin**, a capable junior, can write perfectly good tests; he just can't (yet) design the system. So look at what each hour actually *costs*:

| Person | Task | What an hour costs them (opportunity cost) | Specialize in |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Priya** (senior) | Architecture | Almost nothing — no one else *can* do it | ✅ Architecture |
| **Priya** (senior) | Writing tests | An hour of architecture only she can do — huge | ❌ |
| **Devin** (junior) | Writing tests | An hour of… other junior work. Low | ✅ Tests |
| **Devin** (junior) | Architecture | He can't do it yet | ❌ |

Priya is *better* at writing tests than Devin. Doesn't matter. Every hour she spends on tests is an hour of irreplaceable architecture lost — her opportunity cost is enormous. Devin's opportunity cost for the same hour is tiny. So **Devin specializes in tests**, Priya specializes in architecture, and the team ships more — even though you just handed a task to the person who's *worse* at it.

That last sentence is the whole course in miniature. Comparative advantage routinely tells you to give work to the person who is *not* the best at it, because the best person's time is worth more elsewhere.

## Running a company

Now zoom out to the founder. This case is so common it has its own startup folklore.

**Sofia** founded her company. She is — genuinely — a better programmer than every engineer she has hired. She could out-code all of them. So how much code should Sofia write?

Almost none.

Because Sofia is *also* the only person who can do the things a CEO does: set the vision, raise the next round, recruit the people who are better than her at *their* jobs, close the strategic partnership. Nobody else in the building can do those. Her opportunity cost for an hour of coding is an hour of fundraising-or-hiring-or-vision that *only she* can do. That's the highest opportunity cost in the company. So her **comparative advantage** is the CEO work, even though her **absolute advantage** is in coding.

You've heard the advice "hire people better than you." Good advice — but it's only half the rule, and it makes comparative advantage sound like it's only about hiring superstars. The fuller truth is sharper: **even people who are *worse* than you should take the work you give up the most to do.** Sofia's engineers may be worse coders than she is, and they should *still* write the code, because the alternative is Sofia *not* raising the money that keeps the lights on. Specialization isn't a reward for being the best. It's a routing decision based on opportunity cost.

## Your own calendar

Here's the version with no employees at all: just you, one person, twenty-four hours, and a list of things that all want doing. This is comparative advantage as a **one-person problem.**

You are simultaneously the CEO, the engineer, the janitor, and the intern of your own life. So the same routing question applies: which tasks have the *highest opportunity cost* — the ones that, by doing them yourself, cost you the most valuable thing you could've done instead? Those are the ones to **delegate, outsource, or drop.**

Concretely:

- You bill \$80/hr (or your evening with your kids is worth more than money). Cleaning the house takes you 3 hours. Paying someone \$120 to clean buys back 3 hours of \$80/hr work — or 3 hours of rest you actually needed. Cleaning is *not* your comparative advantage; you give up a lot to do it.
- That fifth low-stakes committee, the "quick favor" that eats a Saturday, the meeting that could've been an email — these are low-**edge** commitments (you bring no special advantage) with high opportunity cost (they crowd out your real work). Saying **no** to them *is* specialization.
- The deep, high-value work that only you can do — the thing you're uniquely good at, the relationship only you can maintain — that's where you keep your hours.

But one honest caveat, because the model has a sharp edge: **delegating only pays off if the freed time actually goes to higher-value use.** If you pay someone to clean so you can… doom-scroll, you didn't exploit a comparative advantage; you just spent money. The math works only when the bought-back hour goes to something genuinely worth more. (We'll push hard on this limit — and others — in the next lesson.)

## Comparative advantage meets your circle of competence

There's a sister model on this site worth bolting on here: your **circle of competence** — the set of things you genuinely understand well enough to have a real edge. The first rule of that model is *know where your circle ends and don't stray outside it.*

Stack the two models and you get a complete operating procedure:

1. **Circle of competence** finds *where you have an edge at all* — the domains where you're genuinely good.
2. **Comparative advantage** then sorts *within* your plate: even among things you're good at, keep only the ones you'd give up the most by *not* doing — and delegate the rest.

The twist worth remembering: comparative advantage tells you to hand off tasks *even inside your circle of competence.* You might be excellent at writing tests, or formatting decks, or bookkeeping — squarely inside your circle — and still be wrong to do them, because something else inside your circle costs you more to skip.

<Callout variant="info" title="The two-step routing rule">
First ask **circle of competence**: where do I have a real edge? Then ask **comparative advantage**: of the work on my plate, what would I sacrifice the most by *not* doing personally? Keep that. Delegate the lower-opportunity-cost work — yes, even the parts you're good at.
</Callout>

### When this breaks down

This isn't magic, and the next lesson collects the fine print. Briefly: delegating has **transaction costs** (finding, training, and trusting someone isn't free), some things genuinely *can't* be handed off, and the whole argument collapses if the time you free up doesn't actually go to higher-value use. Comparative advantage is a powerful default — not an unconditional law.

The big idea to carry out of here: **comparative advantage is not an international-trade rule that happens to be interesting.** It's a general law of scarce time and limited people. Nations, teams, companies, and your own Tuesday all obey it. Do only what only you can do — and what you'd sacrifice the most by skipping. Route the rest to whoever gives up the least.
