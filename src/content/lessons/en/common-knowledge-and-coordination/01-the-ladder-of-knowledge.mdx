Picture a lecture hall five minutes after the scheduled end. The speaker has long
since finished, the slides are back on the title card, and every single person in
the room privately wishes they could leave. Nobody moves. Each person is thinking
the same thing — *is it over? can we go?* — but nobody is sure everyone else thinks
it too, so nobody wants to be the one who stands up, gathers their coat, and
discovers they've misread the room. Everybody wants the same thing, everybody knows
what they want, and still the room sits frozen.

That frozen room is the puzzle this whole course is built on. The people aren't
confused about the *facts* — they're confused about each other's *minds*. What
unlocks the room isn't new information about the world; it's a specific kind of
shared certainty about what everyone else knows. This lesson builds the ladder that
tells those situations apart, one rung at a time.

## Rung one: private knowledge — "I know X"

Start at the bottom. **Private knowledge** is something *you* know that lives only
in your own head — you have no assurance that anybody else knows it too. It's the
rawest, most solitary form of knowing: a fact you hold, full stop.

In the lecture hall, "the talk is over and I'd like to leave" is private knowledge
for each person. You know it about yourself with total certainty. But that
certainty is sealed inside you. For all you can tell, the person beside you is
riveted, taking notes, hoping for a second hour. Private knowledge is a fact you're
sure of and everyone else's mind is a locked box.

Consider a cleaner example. You glance at your phone and read that your train is
cancelled. Right now, that's private knowledge: *you* know the train is cancelled,
and you have no idea whether the other forty people on the platform have seen the
same alert. You could act on it alone — walk to the bus stop — but you can't yet
count on anyone else doing the same.

<Callout variant="info" title="The test for rung one">
Ask only: *do I, personally, know X?* If yes, you have private knowledge — and
nothing more is guaranteed. Everyone else's head is, so far, a black box.
</Callout>

## Rung two: mutual knowledge — "we each know X (but I'm not sure you know I know)"

Now suppose the train cancellation flashes up on the big departures board and you
watch several people glance at it. Something has changed: now *you* know the train
is cancelled, and you have good reason to believe *they* know too. That's the
second rung.

**Mutual knowledge** (also called *shared* knowledge) means everyone in the group
individually knows X. Each person has the fact. What's still missing is the *next
layer up*: you don't necessarily know that *they* know that *you* know. The knowing
is spread around, but nobody has confirmation that the knowing itself is shared.

Here's the trap that makes mutual knowledge feel like more than it is. Imagine two
colleagues, Ana and Ben, sitting in a meeting that has clearly run out of purpose.
Ana thinks, *this is over, someone should call it.* So does Ben. Both know it's
over — that's mutual knowledge. But Ana is thinking, *I know it's over, but what if
Ben still has a point to make? I don't want to look rude.* And Ben is thinking the
exact same thing about Ana. Each knows the fact; neither is sure the other knows
that they know it. So both sit tight. Mutual knowledge, all by itself, is **not
enough to coordinate.**

<Callout variant="warning" title="Mutual knowledge is a floor with no ceiling">
"We each privately know X" sounds like plenty. It isn't. As long as I'm unsure
whether you *know that I know*, I can't safely bet that we'll both move together —
and a lot of worthwhile joint moves only pay off if we move together.
</Callout>

## Rung three: common knowledge — the knowing goes all the way up

Back to the lecture hall — but now the host walks to the microphone and announces,
loudly, to the whole room: "Thank you all, that concludes today's session." Watch
what happens. People stand up at once, without hesitation, without checking their
neighbours. The frozen room thaws instantly.

Nothing about the *facts* changed. Everyone already knew the talk was over. What
the announcement added was the top rung.

**Common knowledge** of X means: everyone knows X; *and* everyone knows that
everyone knows X; *and* everyone knows that everyone knows that everyone knows
X — and so on, without end. It is the fact plus the entire infinite tower of "and
we all know that we all know that we all know…" stacked on top of it. When the host
announced the end out loud, in front of everyone, each rung of that tower was
delivered in a single instant: you heard it, you saw everyone else hear it, you saw
everyone see everyone hear it. There's no level at which someone might have missed
it.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The definition, boiled down">
**Common knowledge** of X = everyone knows X, *and* everyone knows that everyone
knows X, *and* everyone knows that everyone knows that everyone knows X, forever
up the tower — with no top rung where the chain quietly breaks.
</Callout>

## The recursion isn't mystical — a public event delivers every rung at once

That infinite tower — "I know that you know that I know that you know…" — can sound
like it demands superhuman mental gymnastics, as though you'd have to consciously
think a thousand nested thoughts before standing up. You don't, and this is the
part worth getting right.

The tower is a **logical closure**, not a checklist you run through in your head.
Nobody actually thinks level 47. What a public event does is make every level *true
at once*, so that if you ever needed to check any rung, it would already hold.
Hearing an announcement *in a room where you can see everyone else hearing it* is a
single act that satisfies the whole infinite stack — the way turning on the lights
reveals the whole room at once instead of one object at a time.

Let's walk the frozen room through the rungs explicitly, and watch exactly where
the lower rungs fail and the top one succeeds.

- **Level 1 (private / mutual only).** Ana knows the talk is over. Ben knows the
  talk is over. But Ana reasons: "Maybe Ben didn't realise it's finished — if I
  stand and he stays, I look like the rude one who bolted." So Ana waits. Ben,
  thinking the same, waits too. Knowing the fact wasn't enough.
- **Level 2 (I know you know).** Now suppose Ana catches Ben's eye and sees the
  same *this-is-over* look. Ana now knows Ben knows. Better — but Ana's next worry
  arrives instantly: "Does Ben know that *I* know? If he thinks I'm still engaged,
  he might wait for me out of politeness." The doubt just moved up a rung. Still
  frozen.
- **Level 3 and up (common knowledge).** The host announces it out loud to the
  whole room. Now Ana knows, knows Ben knows, knows Ben knows she knows, and so on
  — there's no rung left for the doubt to hide on. The excuse to wait has nowhere
  to live. Everyone stands.

The lesson of the walk-through: doubt is like water — block it on one level and it
just seeps up to the next. Only a rung that goes *all the way up* leaves it nowhere
to go. A public event is the thing that closes every level in one stroke.

## Why the mutual → common jump is the one that matters

Here's the crux of the entire course, so slow down for it. The gap between rung one
and rung two is real, but the gap that decides whether a group can *act together on
something risky* is the jump from **mutual** to **common** knowledge.

Why risky action specifically? Because the cost of moving alone is what freezes
people. Standing up first, walking out first, pulling your money out of a wobbly
bank first, being the first to object at a rigged meeting — each of these is safe
*only if others move with you*, and painful if you move alone. Mutual knowledge
leaves you exposed: you know the fact, but you can't rule out that someone thinks
someone else hasn't caught on, so you can't be sure the crowd will move. Common
knowledge removes the last hiding place for that doubt. Once the knowing goes all
the way up, "but maybe they won't move" has no rung to stand on, and coordinated,
risky action becomes safe to attempt.

## The three rungs, side by side

| Rung | Do I know X? | Do I know *you* know X? | Do I know you know I know…​ (all the way up)? | Enough to coordinate on a risky joint move? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Private** | Yes | No | No | No |
| **Mutual** | Yes | Yes | No | No — the doubt just climbs a rung |
| **Common** | Yes | Yes | Yes, endlessly | **Yes** — no rung left for doubt to hide on |

Notice that the first column is identical down every row: at all three rungs *you
personally know the fact*. The whole difference — and it's the difference between a
frozen room and a room that empties in one motion — lives in the higher columns,
which are about knowledge of other minds, not knowledge of the world.

## Pitfall: mistaking "we all know" for common knowledge

The single most common slip with this model is treating mutual knowledge — "come
on, we *all* obviously know this" — as if it were common knowledge, and then being
baffled when the group doesn't act.

Work through a concrete failure. A manager sends each team member a *separate*
private message: "Between us, the project is being cancelled next week." Now every
single person on the team knows the project is dead — that's rock-solid mutual
knowledge. The manager assumes that's the same as telling everyone, and expects the
team to gracefully wind things down together. Instead, everybody keeps grinding on
their tasks, nobody raises the obvious question in the team meeting, and the
awkwardness drags on for days.

What went wrong? Because the message was *private*, each person is unsure what the
others were told. Priya knows it's cancelled, but wonders: "Did Sam get the same
message? If I bring it up and Sam hasn't heard, I've leaked something and I look
disloyal." Everyone reasons this way, so nobody speaks. The manager had mutual
knowledge and *needed* common knowledge — a single announcement to the whole team
at once — and the two are not interchangeable. The fix isn't more information;
everyone already had the fact. The fix is making the fact *public*.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The error, in one line">
❌ "Everyone already knows it, so of course they'll act on it together."
Knowing-it-separately (mutual) is *not* knowing-it-together (common). Coordinated
action needs the top rung, and only a public signal supplies it.
</Callout>

## When to reach for this ladder

Pull out this three-rung ladder whenever a group is stuck even though the *facts*
seem obvious to everyone in it — the frozen meeting, the silence after a bad
decision, the bank nobody wants to be first to flee, the emperor whose nakedness
everyone can see. In those moments, stop asking "does everyone know?" (they usually
do) and start asking "does everyone know that everyone knows — all the way up?"
That reframing is the diagnostic. If the honest answer is "no, it only arrived
privately," you've found the blockage, and you've found the fix: make it public.

The reason this course exists is that an astonishing range of human behaviour —
protests that erupt overnight, prices that everyone secretly distrusts but nobody
challenges, meetings that never end — turns on the difference between the middle and
the top rung. Get the ladder solid now, and the rest of the course is watching it
do its work in the wild.
