You now have the machinery: the ladder from private to mutual to **common
knowledge** (the infinite regress — I know, you know, I know you know, forever),
and the coordination game where the right move depends entirely on what you
believe others will do. This lesson is where that machinery earns its keep. We're
going to watch it explain some of the strangest, fastest, most consequential
events humans produce: banks that die in a weekend, regimes that look eternal
right up until they evaporate, and why a soft-drink company will pay a fortune to
reach you specifically while you're watching the big game.

The thread tying all of them together is a single sentence. **A stable-but-bad
outcome can flip the instant a public signal turns a private truth into common
knowledge.** Nobody's beliefs about the *facts* have to change — only their
beliefs about *each other's* beliefs. Hold that sentence; every case below is a
variation on it.

## Bank runs: the solvent bank that dies anyway

A bank takes your deposits, promises them back on demand, then lends most of them
out for years — mortgages, business loans. That mismatch is the entire business
model, and it hides a trap. The bank can be **solvent** (its loans are good and
worth more than it owes) yet **illiquid** (it cannot turn those loans back into
cash fast enough to pay every depositor at once). Those two words are the whole
lesson, so let's nail them down.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Two words that run the whole show">
**Solvent** means assets exceed liabilities — given time, the bank can pay
everyone. **Illiquid** means it can't do so *right now*, because the money is tied
up in loans that won't mature for years. A healthy bank is normally solvent but
always illiquid. That gap is where runs live.
</Callout>

Here's the coordination game, stripped to its bones (this is the intuition behind
the **Diamond–Dybvig** model of bank runs). If you believe other depositors will
stay calm, your best move is to leave your money in and collect interest. If you
believe others will rush to withdraw, your best move is to rush *first* — because
the bank pays out until the cash runs dry, and latecomers get nothing. So there
are **two equilibria**: everyone-stays (good, stable) and everyone-runs (bad,
also stable). Both are self-fulfilling. You run if you think others will run, and
that belief alone can drain a perfectly healthy bank.

Notice what does the killing. The loans didn't go bad. The bank didn't become
insolvent. What changed is that "the bank is in trouble" became **common
knowledge** — usually via a *public signal* everyone sees and everyone knows
everyone else saw: a news report, a queue photographed outside a branch, a viral
post. The signal doesn't have to be true. It only has to make each depositor
think *everyone else is about to run.*

<Callout variant="info" title="Worked example: the Friday-afternoon run">
Northbridge Savings holds \$100 in deposits. Its loans are sound and will be worth
\$110 next year — genuinely solvent. But it keeps only \$20 in cash on hand.

Monday, all is calm; withdrawals are tiny and routine. Then Friday at noon a
popular finance account posts "is Northbridge about to fail?" — seen by thousands,
and crucially, *everyone knows thousands saw it.* By 1pm a queue forms. The queue
is itself a new public signal: now depositors don't just fear the bank, they can
*see* others acting on the fear. Each person reasons, correctly, "only the first
\$20 gets paid — I'd better be in it." The \$20 evaporates by 2pm. A solvent bank
is dead by close of business, killed not by bad loans but by a self-fulfilling
belief about everyone else.
</Callout>

This isn't a museum piece. Britain's Northern Rock in 2007 drew literal queues
around the block; Silicon Valley Bank in 2023 died in roughly 48 hours as
depositors — coordinated by news and group chats — pulled funds faster than any
bank could survive. In both, the accelerant was a public signal that made the fear
common knowledge.

## Revolutions: the discontent nobody could see

Now take the same shape and point it at a dictatorship. Under a repressive regime,
opposing the government is dangerous, so people who privately despise it *pretend*
to support it — they clap at the right moments, they don't say what they think.
This hiding has a name.

**Preference falsification** (Timur Kuran's term) is the act of misrepresenting
your true preferences under social or political pressure. Its poisonous
side-effect is **pluralistic ignorance**: because everyone is hiding the same
opposition, *everyone underestimates how much opposition exists.* You hate the
regime, but you look around, see everyone clapping, and conclude you're nearly
alone — dangerously outnumbered. So you keep clapping. So does the person next to
you, for exactly the same reason. The opposition can be a silent majority and
*still* feel, to each of its members, like a lonely minority.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Two failures, stacked">
**Preference falsification** is the *hiding* — I mask my real view. **Pluralistic
ignorance** is the *result at the group level* — since we're all hiding, none of
us knows the truth about the others. Discontent is now *private* knowledge held by
millions, but not *common* knowledge held by anyone.
</Callout>

Then something makes the shared discontent common knowledge — and the dam breaks.
Someone shouts the forbidden thing and isn't arrested. A crowd stands and doesn't
scatter. A dictator is booed on his own balcony, on live television, in front of
everyone. Suddenly each person learns not just "I hate this" but "*everyone* hates
this, and everyone now knows everyone hates this." The private truth becomes
common knowledge, the cost of speaking collapses (they can't arrest all of us),
and the regime that looked eternal falls in days.

<Callout variant="info" title="Worked narrative: the balcony that broke a regime">
For decades a regime holds power partly through preference falsification: people
attend the rallies, wave the flags, and privately loathe it — each believing the
loathing is theirs almost alone.

Then a mass rally is called, staged for the cameras as a show of loyalty. Standing
in the enormous crowd, the leader begins his speech — and from somewhere the
jeering starts. It spreads. The cameras, broadcasting live, capture the leader's
face freezing. In that instant every viewer learns three things at once: *I'm not
alone, they're not alone, and everyone can see that everyone can see it.* The
falsification that held the whole system up depended on nobody knowing the truth
about everyone else. The broadcast destroyed that in seconds. Days later the regime
is gone. Romania's Ceaușescu in 1989 was booed on exactly such a balcony; across
Eastern Europe that year, and again in the 2011 Arab Spring, regimes that looked
immovable dissolved once the shared discontent became common knowledge.
</Callout>

This is why revolutions blindside everyone — including the experts, the secret
police, and often the revolutionaries themselves. The discontent was there all
along, invisible, because everyone was busy hiding it from everyone else. What
changed wasn't how much people hated the regime. It was that they suddenly *knew*
they all did.

## Crowds: the number is the message

Why do protesters risk turnout in the streets rather than, say, a private petition?
Because a **visible crowd is itself the common-knowledge signal.** When you stand in
a square with a hundred thousand people, you don't just learn "many oppose this" —
you learn "many oppose this *and everyone here can see that many do*." The size of
the crowd, seen by the crowd, is the message. "We saw how many we were" changes
everything, because it converts a private hope ("surely others feel this too") into
common knowledge in one glance.

This is also why *broadcasting* a crowd amplifies it, and why regimes fight so hard
over the footage and the headcount. A protest of a hundred thousand seen only by
its participants is powerful. The same protest on every screen in the country —
where now everyone knows everyone saw it — is an order of magnitude more powerful,
because it manufactures common knowledge among people who were never in the square
at all.

## Advertising: common knowledge you can buy

The political scientist **Michael Chwe** noticed that this same logic quietly runs
a chunk of the advertising industry. Some ads run in the most expensive slots money
can buy — the big game, the season finale — at a wild premium *per viewer.* Reaching
one person during the big broadcast can cost many times what reaching that same
person privately would. Why would any rational buyer overpay so grotesquely?

Because those slots don't just deliver eyeballs — they deliver **common knowledge.**
When an ad airs during the big game, you don't only see it; you know that millions of
others saw it, and that they know you saw it, and so on. For most products that's
worthless. But for a specific and important class of goods, it's the entire point.

<Callout variant="tip" title="When you need everyone to know that everyone knows">
A **coordination good** is something you want *because others will want it too* —
its value to you depends on other people also adopting it. Three big families:
**network products** (a chat app, a payment platform, a video-call tool — useless
if your friends aren't on it), **status and social goods** (a fashion label, a
luxury brand — worthless as a signal if others don't recognize it), and **standards**
(the format everyone must pick together). For these, private information isn't
enough; you need common knowledge that *others* are adopting too.
</Callout>

<Callout variant="info" title="Worked contrast: the same ad, delivered two ways">
Imagine one million people, and a new video-call app whose whole value is that your
contacts use it too. You have one ad to spend.

**Privately** — a million individually targeted views, each person seeing it alone.
Everyone now knows the app exists. But no one knows whether *anyone else* is
adopting it, so each waits for others to move first. The classic coordination
stall: great product, private knowledge, nobody jumps.

**Publicly** — one slot in the big game, a million people watching *together.* Same
one million impressions. But now each viewer knows the other million saw it too, and
knows they know. Adopting suddenly looks safe — "my contacts must have seen this and
be signing up." The public buy created common knowledge; the private buy only created
private knowledge. Identical reach, opposite result — which is exactly why the public
slot is worth the premium for a coordination good, and *not* worth it for, say,
painkillers, where I don't care whether you take the same brand.
</Callout>

## The Keynesian beauty contest

Economist John Maynard Keynes described a newspaper game: readers pick the six
prettiest faces from a hundred photos, and whoever's picks match the *most popular*
choices wins a prize. The clever player doesn't pick the faces *she* finds prettiest.
She picks the faces she thinks *others* will find prettiest. But everyone reasons
that way — so really she must pick the faces others think others will find prettiest,
and so on up the ladder of beliefs-about-beliefs you already know.

The **Keynesian beauty contest** is any situation where the winning move is to
predict the *average opinion about the average opinion*, not to hold the correct
view yourself. Being right about the object is beside the point; being right about
everyone else's guesses is the game.

<Callout variant="info" title="Where the beauty contest bites">
This is a startling amount of modern life. **Markets:** a stock can be worth buying
purely because you expect *others* to buy it (find the meme before the crowd does),
which is why prices can float free of fundamentals for a long time. **Fashion and
memes:** a style spreads because people expect it to spread, not because it's
independently best. **Elections covered as horse-races:** voters and donors flock to
the candidate they think *others* think is electable — "viability" becomes
self-fulfilling, a beauty contest wearing a ballot. In each, everyone is trying to
sit where the crowd will sit, which is precisely a coordination game solved by
guessing others' guesses.
</Callout>

## Why tyrants fear the town square more than the kitchen table

Put all of this together and a puzzle resolves itself. Authoritarian regimes are
often oddly relaxed about private grumbling — you can complain about them at your
own kitchen table — yet they come down with astonishing force on *public assembly*,
even tiny gatherings. Why fear a small square more than millions of angry kitchens?

Because private grumbling stays private knowledge, and private knowledge doesn't
coordinate anyone. A regime survives on pluralistic ignorance: as long as each
discontented person believes they're alone, no one dares move. **Public assembly is
the common-knowledge-generating channel** — the one thing that can convert a nation
of isolated grumblers into a crowd that knows it's a crowd. So regimes attack the
channel, not the opinion. Ban gatherings. Jam broadcasts and cut the internet during
protests. Arrest the single person who says the forbidden thing out loud — not
because one voice is dangerous, but because the *audience learning that the voice
went unpunished* is. Even punishing jokes and mocking memes makes sense here: a
shared joke about the emperor is a tiny act of common-knowledge creation, everyone
laughing and everyone seeing everyone laugh.

<Callout variant="info" title="The channel is the target">
China's Tiananmen Square in 1989 was, above all, a fight over a *public space* where
common knowledge could form — and its later erasure from the record was a fight to
keep it from re-forming. Under Soviet censorship, dissidents circulated banned texts
hand-to-hand as **samizdat** ("self-published"), a low-tech workaround precisely
because the *public* channels of common knowledge were sealed shut. In every case the
regime's instinct is identical: control not what people privately think, but what
they can all *know that they all* think.
</Callout>

## The map: latent truth, public spark, coordinated outcome

Every case in this lesson has the same three-part skeleton. There's a **latent
private truth** (something many people know or feel, but privately). There's a
**public spark or channel** that converts it into common knowledge. And there's the
**coordinated outcome** that follows, often overnight.

| Case | Latent private truth | Public spark / channel | Coordinated outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bank run | "The bank might not survive a rush" | Viral post, news report, visible queue | Everyone withdraws; solvent bank collapses |
| Revolution | Millions privately loathe the regime | Booing on live TV, a defiant act unpunished | Falsification breaks; regime falls in days |
| Protest | "Surely others are angry too" | The visible size of the crowd, broadcast | Isolated grievance becomes a mass movement |
| Coordination-good ad | "I'd adopt this if others did" | Big-game broadcast everyone knows all saw | Simultaneous adoption; the network tips |
| Beauty-contest market | "Others may pile into this" | Prices and headlines everyone watches | Self-fulfilling rush that ignores fundamentals |

## The crucial caveat: sparks ignite, they don't create

Before you start seeing common knowledge as a magic wand, one essential limit — and
it's the seam where this model can be misused. **The spark only ignites a fire that
was already laid.** A public signal converts *latent* discontent into *kinetic*
action; it does not manufacture the underlying grievance out of nothing.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The signal converts; it does not create">
If the private dispositions aren't there, the spark fizzles. A viral "the bank is
failing" post does nothing to a bank nobody had reason to doubt and everyone knows is
flush. A dictator booed by a genuinely content population just looks briefly
embarrassed. The big-game ad can't make people coordinate on a product none of them
privately wanted. Common knowledge is the *detonator* — it releases stored energy
fast — but it is not the *fuel.* Confusing the two makes you think you can spark a
revolution or a bank run at will, or that every collapse was engineered by whoever lit
the match. The grievance, the illiquidity, the latent demand had to be there first.
</Callout>

That's also the honest limit on how far you can *engineer* these effects, and the
next lesson digs into exactly where common-knowledge reasoning breaks down and
misleads — the cases where the ladder of beliefs snaps, the signal is misread, or
the "everyone knows" turns out to be a fiction. For now, hold the caveat: the signal
is a converter of latent truth, never a creator of it.

### When to reach for this model

Pull out common-knowledge-and-coordination thinking whenever an outcome (a) depends
on what people expect *each other* to do, (b) has a self-fulfilling quality, and (c)
can flip fast without the underlying facts changing much. Bank runs, panics, protest
waves, fashion cascades, speculative bubbles, network-product tipping points,
platform migrations, viral norms — all fit. The diagnostic question is always the
same: *what's the latent private truth, and what public signal would make it common
knowledge?* If you can answer both, you can often see the flip coming before it does.
