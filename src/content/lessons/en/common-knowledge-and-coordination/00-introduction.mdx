Picture a packed commuter train, stalled between stations for the third time
this month. Every single passenger is fed up. Every single passenger knows the
service is a disgrace. And every single passenger sits there in silence, staring
at their phone, doing absolutely nothing. The grievance is total — and the
platform stays quiet.

Now imagine one person stands up and says, loudly, *"This is unacceptable — who's
with me?"* — and heads turn, and murmurs spread, and suddenly a carriage that was
a hundred lonely complaints is one annoyed crowd. Nothing about the trains
changed. What changed is that a private truth everyone was carrying alone became
a **public** one: not just *everyone knows*, but *everyone knows that everyone
knows.*

That second, recursive layer of knowing is the whole subject of this course. It
has a precise name — **common knowledge** — and once you can see it, you'll spot
the same hidden switch behind bank runs, revolutions, viral trends, Super Bowl
ads, and why dictators lose sleep over public squares.

## What you'll actually walk away with

By the end you'll be able to tell three kinds of knowing apart — what *I* know,
what *we each* know, and what *everyone knows that everyone knows* — and use the
difference to explain why crowds freeze, why they suddenly move, and why the
*signal* that makes knowledge public often matters more than the underlying
truth. Here's the map:

1. **The ladder of knowledge** — private, mutual, and common knowledge, and the
   "everyone knows that everyone knows…" recursion made concrete.
2. **The emperor's new clothes** — why a single *public* signal coordinates a
   crowd that a thousand *private* signals never could, plus focal points. This
   lesson has an interactive crowd you can freeze and unfreeze yourself.
3. **Coordination games** — how beliefs about other people's beliefs, not
   payoffs alone, pick which outcome a group lands on (with the Stag Hunt).
4. **Bank runs, revolutions & crowds** — the model's real faces: self-fulfilling
   panics, hidden dissent that erupts overnight, advertising, and the beauty
   contest.
5. **Where common knowledge breaks** — the idealisation's cracks: almost-common
   knowledge, the two-generals problem, and manufactured *false* common
   knowledge.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
Coordinated action doesn't hinge on what everyone privately knows — it hinges on
what everyone knows that everyone knows. Make a shared truth *public* and a frozen
crowd can move as one.
</Callout>

## A quick taste of the idea

Back to the silent train. Why does the grievance sit there doing nothing?
Because each passenger is thinking the same nervous thought: *I'm furious — but
am I the only one? If I stand up and no one joins me, I just look like the unhinged
person shouting on the 8:15.* Each person *privately* knows the trains are awful,
but nobody is sure the others feel the same, or would act. The knowledge is real
but **trapped** — everybody has it, nobody knows that everybody has it.

The person who stands up doesn't add a single new fact. Everyone already knew the
trains were late. What the shout does is make that shared feeling **common
knowledge**: now you know the others are angry, *and* you know they can see that
you're angry, *and* you know they know you can see it. The fear of being alone
evaporates, because being first is no longer a risk anyone is taking by
themselves.

## How to use this course

Every lesson opens with a quick guess, teaches the idea through a concrete story,
and checks that it stuck. Don't skip the guesses — trying to answer *before* you
know is one of the most reliable ways to actually remember. There's no heavy maths
here: one lesson has payoff tables with numbers you can add up by hand, and the
emperor lesson hands you an interactive crowd whose behaviour you control with a
slider and a switch.

This is an **expert-tier** course, so it assumes you've met a few earlier models —
you'll get more from it if you're comfortable with *game-theory basics*,
*Nash equilibrium*, and *critical mass*. When you've finished the five teaching
lessons, a graded final exam pulls it all together. It's one-way — once you submit
an answer it's locked — so treat the practice questions along the way as exactly
that: practice.

<Callout variant="warning" title="One habit to build as you go">
Whenever you see a crowd do something dramatic — a run, a protest, a stampede
into a stock — train yourself to ask not *"what did they learn?"* but *"what did
they suddenly all know that everyone else knew?"* That second question is the one
this course teaches you to answer.
</Callout>

Ready? The next lesson builds the ladder carefully — private, mutual, common —
and shows exactly why that top rung is the one that moves the world.
