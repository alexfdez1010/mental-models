<Callout variant="success" title="Key takeaways">
Coordinated, risky action needs more than everyone privately knowing a fact — it
needs common knowledge, where everyone knows that everyone knows, without end. A
pile of private signals never gets you there; a single public signal, seen by all
at once, does — which is exactly what the child's shout, a Super Bowl advert, a
visible crowd, and a free press all manufacture. That is why coordination games
have multiple equilibria (a bad, everyone-plays-safe outcome can be perfectly
stable), why bank runs and revolutions are self-fulfilling and surprise everyone,
and why regimes attack the common-knowledge channel first. But it is an
idealisation: no finite string of confirmations ever truly closes the tower, false
common knowledge coordinates a crowd just as well as true, and a focal point or a
little critical mass can do the job without everyone knowing everything. Read for
what is common, not merely shared — and you will see the hidden signal behind the
crowd.
</Callout>
