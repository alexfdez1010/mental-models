Everyone knows the story. Two swindlers sell a vain emperor a suit of clothes so
fine, they say, that only the clever and the worthy can see it — invisible to
fools. The emperor, seeing nothing, dares not admit it. Nor do his ministers. Nor
do the crowds lining the street as he parades past, gloriously naked, because who
wants to be the one fool who can't see the magnificent robes? Then a small child
pipes up: *"But he hasn't got anything on!"* — and the whole crowd, as one, bursts
out laughing.

Here's the puzzle that makes this story the perfect gateway to our model: the
child told nobody anything they didn't already know. Every adult on that street
could see perfectly well that the emperor was naked. The information was already
sitting in every single head. So why did one obvious sentence from a child change
everything?

## The fact was already mutual knowledge

Before the child speaks, the emperor's nakedness is **mutual knowledge**: every
person in the crowd knows it. But — and this is the trap from the last lesson —
each person is *unsure whether the others know it too*, or at least unsure whether
the others will *admit* it. Maybe everyone else really can see the magic cloth.
Maybe I'm the only fool. So each person keeps quiet, and their silence becomes
evidence to everyone else that maybe the robes are real after all. A whole street
of people who can see the truth, each trapped by not knowing what the others see.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Mutual knowledge, one more time">
**Mutual (shared) knowledge:** everyone knows the fact, but nobody is sure that
everyone else knows it. It sits in every head *individually* and coordinates
*nobody*. This is the state the crowd is stuck in while the emperor parades.
</Callout>

## The shout makes it common knowledge

The child's sentence doesn't add a fact — it changes the *type* of knowledge.
Said out loud, in front of everyone, it becomes **common knowledge**: now everyone
knows the emperor is naked, *and* everyone can see that everyone else just heard
it too, *and* everyone knows that everyone knows that everyone heard it. The
private fear — *am I the only one?* — is gone in an instant, because the answer is
now standing in plain sight. Once nobody has to be the first to say it, everybody
can laugh.

That is the engine of this entire course: **a public signal converts mutual
knowledge into common knowledge, and that conversion — not any new fact — is what
unlocks coordinated action.**

## Public signal vs. a pile of private signals

Now the crucial contrast, because it's where people's intuition usually fails. It
is *not* enough for everyone to receive the information. Everyone already *had* the
information privately. What matters is whether the signal is **public** — seen by
all, *and seen to be seen by all*.

Imagine the swindlers' scam is exposed differently: a discreet royal aide walks
the parade route and whispers to each person, one at a time, *"Between us — he's
naked."* Now every single person has been told, explicitly. Have you created
common knowledge? **No.** Each person knows the emperor is naked, and knows that
*they* were told — but has no idea the aide whispered the same thing to everyone
else. For all I know, I'm the only one who was let in on it. The crowd stays
frozen. A thousand private whispers do not equal one public shout.

<Callout variant="info" title="The defining feature of a public signal">
A signal is **public** when everyone receives it *and* everyone can see that
everyone else received it — a shout in the square, a broadcast everyone knows is
airing, a crowd visibly gathered. That "seen to be seen" quality is the whole
difference. Private information, however widespread, coordinates nobody; a public
signal coordinates everybody.
</Callout>

## Focal points: the signal doesn't just say "go" — it says "go *here*"

A public signal does more than break the freeze. It also tells everyone *which*
option to converge on, when several are possible. Thomas Schelling called these
salient options **focal points** (or "Schelling points"): the choice that stands
out as the obvious one *everyone expects everyone else to expect.*

His famous puzzle: you must meet a stranger in New York City tomorrow, but you
never agreed where or when. No communication. Where do you go? Most people
answer *Grand Central, at noon* — not because it's objectively best, but because
it's the option that leaps out as the one the other person will guess *you'll*
guess. A focal point is a natural piece of common knowledge: its whole power is
that it's obvious *to everyone that it's obvious to everyone.*

<Callout variant="tip" title="Why focal points are a common-knowledge device">
A focal point works only if its salience is itself common knowledge — everyone
must expect everyone to expect it. "Noon" beats "3:47 p.m." not because noon is
better, but because everyone knows that everyone knows noon is the obvious
default. Publicness manufactures focal points: a shared landmark, a round number,
a tradition, a leader everyone can see.
</Callout>

Everyday focal points are everywhere once you look. Which side of the road to
drive on (either works, but a country needs *one*, publicly known). Who speaks
first in an awkward silence — often whoever the group visibly looks toward. Which
brand becomes "the" default. The standing ovation is a lovely case: nobody wants
to be the first on their feet *or* the last one sitting, so people wait for a
visible cue — a few prominent people rising — and then the whole hall stands in
seconds. The cue is the focal signal that everyone was waiting to share.

## The pitfall: publicness works regardless of what's true

Here is the double edge you must carry forward. A public signal manufactures
common knowledge about *whatever it carries* — true or false, wise or foolish. The
child happened to be right. But the very same mechanism works if the shout is a
lie: "the bank has failed!" screamed in a crowded hall creates common knowledge of
a panic whether or not the bank is actually failing. Publicness coordinates; it
does not verify.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Don't confuse 'everyone's acting on it' with 'it's true'">
The power of a public signal is entirely in the coordination it creates, not in
the accuracy of its content. A crowd moving as one is strong evidence that
something became common knowledge — and *no* evidence at all that the something is
true. We'll return to this "false common knowledge" trap in the final lesson; for
now, just flag it.
</Callout>

### When to reach for this model

Reach for *public signal → common knowledge* whenever you're trying to explain (or
engineer) a sudden, coordinated shift that no new *fact* seems to justify: a crowd
that erupts over something everyone already knew, a market that turns on an
announcement of old news, a norm that flips the moment someone says the quiet part
out loud. Ask: *what was already mutual knowledge, and what just made it common?*
