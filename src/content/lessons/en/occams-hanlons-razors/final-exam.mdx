<Callout variant="success" title="Key takeaways">
A **razor** is a rule of thumb that shaves off unlikely explanations to give you a
smart first bet — a *prior*, never a *proof*. **Occam's razor** prefers the
explanation that fits the facts with the fewest assumptions, and the arithmetic
backs it up: independent assumptions multiply, so every extra one can only drag the
joint probability down. **Hanlon's razor** is Occam pointed at people — reach for
incompetence before malice, because a screw-up needs no plan while a plot needs
intent, competence, and coordination all at once. But both razors *lie* when you
trust them blindly: Occam over-trims genuinely complex situations (remember
Hickam's dictum), and Hanlon excuses patterned, one-directional, self-serving harm.
Hold each as a starting bet you actively test, update on evidence, and lean on less
as the stakes rise. Used that way, the razors make you faster and less wrong; used
as verdicts, they're just a blindfold you sharpened yourself.
</Callout>
