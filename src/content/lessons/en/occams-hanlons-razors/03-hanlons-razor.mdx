Picture a four-way stop where a car cuts in front of you. Your gut already has a story: *"That entitled jerk did it on purpose."* You feel wronged. Now flip it — last week **you** cut someone off at that same intersection. Were you a jerk? No. You were distracted, the sun was in your eyes, you had a meeting in six minutes. Same action, two completely different explanations: when it's *them*, it's character; when it's *you*, it's circumstance.

That asymmetry is a glitch in human reasoning, and this lesson hands you a one-line tool to correct it. It's a razor — a rule for choosing between explanations — but a very specific one: it only applies to **other people's bad behavior**. When someone harms or annoys you, it tells you which explanation to reach for first.

## The razor itself: incompetence before malice

**Hanlon's razor** says: *never attribute to malice that which is adequately explained by incompetence.* Swap in "stupidity," "carelessness," "neglect," or "an honest mistake" — they all point the same way. When someone does something that hurts or irritates you, the leaner default is **error**, not **enmity**.

A handy analogy: a razor *shaves off* unnecessary stuff. Occam's razor (lesson 3) shaves off unnecessary *assumptions* from any explanation. Hanlon's razor is Occam's razor pointed at one specific target — **explanations of other people's motives** — and it shaves off the most expensive assumption of all: that the person *meant* to harm you.

Notice the scope. This is not a theory of physics or a tool for diagnosing your car. It's for the social moment when you're about to decide *why a human did the annoying thing*. "Malice" means they intended the harm. "Incompetence" means they didn't — they were sloppy, rushed, forgetful, or just bad at the task.

<Callout variant="info" title="The folk version">
You may have heard the blunter British saying: <strong>"cock-up before conspiracy."</strong> A "cock-up" is a bungle, a screw-up. The point is identical — when something goes wrong, a bungle is almost always a better bet than a plot. Hanlon's razor is the polite, generalizable version of the same instinct.
</Callout>

## The bias it fixes: the fundamental attribution error

Here's the formal name for that four-way-stop glitch. The **fundamental attribution error** is our tendency to explain *other people's* behavior by their **character** ("she's rude," "he's lazy," "they don't respect me") while explaining our *own* identical behavior by the **situation** ("I was slammed," "I didn't see it," "the alarm didn't go off").

It's an asymmetry: same action, two different lenses, depending on whose action it is. You see your own pressures from the inside — the traffic, the deadline, the bad night's sleep — so you give yourself a situational pass. You can't see anyone else's pressures, so you fill the gap with a story about *who they are*.

Hanlon's razor is the manual correction. It forces you to extend to other people the same situational benefit of the doubt you automatically give yourself. You already know that *you* aren't a villain when you mess up. The razor says: assume the same of them, by default, until the evidence says otherwise.

<Reveal client:visible summary="Why does the brain make this error in the first place?">
Because you have a front-row seat to your own situation and a nosebleed seat to everyone else's. From inside your head, the *situation* is loud and obvious — you feel the stress, the rush, the headache. From outside someone else's head, the situation is invisible, so the only thing left to explain their behavior is the **person**. The error isn't stupidity; it's a side effect of where you're standing. Hanlon's razor is a deliberate nudge to imagine the situation you can't see.
</Reveal>

## Conspiracy thinking and the Occam link

The second thing Hanlon's razor guards against is **conspiracy thinking** — explaining a bad outcome as a coordinated, intentional, secret plan.

Walk through what a malicious-plot explanation actually *requires*, and stack the assumptions:

1. **Intent** — someone wanted this specific harm.
2. **Competence** — they were skilled enough to pull it off.
3. **Coordination** — multiple people acted together toward the goal.
4. **Secrecy** — and nobody leaked, bragged, or bungled the cover-up.

That's a tall stack. Recall the conjunction math from lesson 3: each extra assumption an explanation needs *multiplies* its improbability, because the whole story is only as likely as all its parts being true at once. Four shaky assumptions multiplied together is a very small number.

Now price the alternative. "A bunch of people were independently careless" requires **none** of those four. No plan, no coordination, no secret to keep. Just ordinary sloppiness, scaled up. That's why the saying goes: most apparent conspiracies are not a plot — they're many people, separately, dropping the ball.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Hanlon is Occam, applied to humans">
If lesson 3 was "prefer the explanation with fewer assumptions," this lesson is the same rule wearing a social hat. <strong>Malice is the assumption-heavy explanation</strong> (intent + competence + coordination + secrecy). <strong>Incompetence is the lean one</strong> (just... a mistake). Hanlon's razor is Occam's razor pointed at other people's motives.
</Callout>

## Why it works: base rates plus fewer assumptions

Two forces make incompetence the better bet, and they're the same two forces from the previous lessons.

**Force one: base rates.** A "base rate" is just how common something is in general, before you look at the specific case. Carelessness, forgetfulness, fatigue, and being overwhelmed are *enormously* common — every human does them daily. Cold, calculated malice aimed precisely at you is rare. When you reach for the common explanation over the rare one, you'll be right more often. That's not optimism; it's arithmetic.

**Force two: fewer assumptions.** As we just saw, malice carries a heavy load of assumptions (intent, coordination, secrecy), while a mistake carries almost none. Fewer assumptions, by Occam's razor, means a leaner and more probable explanation.

Higher base rate **plus** fewer assumptions both point the same direction: incompetence wins the default. Here's the contrast laid out side by side.

| | **Malice (enmity)** | **Incompetence (error)** |
|---|---|---|
| What it claims | They *meant* to harm you | They messed up, no intent |
| Assumptions required | Intent + competence + coordination + secrecy | Basically none — just a slip |
| How common (base rate) | Rare | Extremely common |
| Needs coordination? | Often yes | No |
| Default verdict | Only when evidence forces it | Reach for this first |

## Three worked examples

Theory's done. Here's the razor at work on three everyday scenes.

### The friend who didn't show

Your friend was supposed to meet you at 7. It's 7:40 and they're not here, no text.

- **Malice / disrespect reading:** "They're blowing me off. They don't value my time. They don't respect me."
- **Incompetence reading:** "They lost track of time, hit traffic, or forgot to check the calendar."

Count the assumptions. The first story assumes they *decided* you weren't worth showing up for — an intent, plus the cruelty to act on it. The second assumes... a human was a human. The base rate of ordinary flakiness towers over the base rate of someone plotting to slight you. Reach for incompetence, send a friendly "everything okay?" text, and you'll be right almost every time.

### The email that went unanswered

You sent something important. Crickets, all day.

- **Malice reading:** "They're ignoring me on purpose to make a point."
- **Incompetence reading:** "It's buried under the other 400 emails in their inbox."

Ignoring-you-on-purpose requires that they (a) saw it, (b) decided to punish you with silence, and (c) are willing to look unprofessional doing it. "Buried inbox" requires nothing but a busy person. Lean explanation wins.

### The company's baffling decision

A business makes a confusing, customer-unfriendly move, and the internet decides it's a deliberate scheme to deceive everyone.

- **Malice reading:** "They're intentionally lying to customers as a master plan."
- **Incompetence reading:** "A committee, a deadline, conflicting goals, and nobody clearly owning the decision produced a mess."

Big organizations are *incompetence factories* — not because the people are dumb, but because coordination is hard and no single person sees the whole picture. A confusing outcome from a large org is the *expected* result of ordinary organizational friction, not evidence of a villain. Scale produces sloppiness for free.

## The guardrail: "adequately explained"

Read the rule again and don't skip the middle words: never attribute to malice what is **adequately explained** by incompetence. That phrase is the whole safety mechanism. Hanlon's razor is **not** "malice never exists" and it is **not** "always assume the best." It's a default, and defaults are made to be overridden.

When incompetence *stops* adequately explaining the facts, you **drop the default**. This is the same through-line from the earlier lessons: a razor gives you a starting **prior**, not a permanent **proof**. You begin assuming error, and you *update* the instant the evidence points elsewhere.

What "doesn't adequately explain" looks like:

- a clear **pattern** rather than a one-off,
- a plausible **motive** for the actor,
- a real **benefit** to the actor from the "mistake."

When all three line up, the innocent reading is straining to hold the weight, and a deliberate explanation becomes the lean one. (Lesson 5 — *When the Razors Lie to You* — gives this its full treatment. Here, just plant the flag: the default has limits.)

## The pitfall: weaponized innocence

Here's how the razor gets abused — usually against the person holding it. The failure mode is using Hanlon's razor to **excuse repeated, patterned, self-serving harm.**

The tell is in the example from the sorting exercise: a vendor that "accidentally" shortchanges only the customers who never check their statements — every month, for a year. Each instance, *in isolation*, looks like a harmless error you'd happily forgive. Strung together, the pattern screams design.

Three signals tell you the incompetence default has **expired**:

1. **Repetition** — it keeps happening, not just once.
2. **Benefit to the actor** — the "mistake" reliably profits the person making it.
3. **A pattern** — the errors aren't random; they consistently fall in one direction.

A genuine mistake is roughly random: sometimes it costs *them*, sometimes it costs *you*. When the "errors" only ever cut one way — toward the actor's advantage — that's not carelessness wearing a mask. Real incompetence is sloppy *both* ways.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Charitable, not naive">
Hanlon's razor pairs naturally with <strong>charity</strong> (assuming good faith) and <strong>steelmanning</strong> (engaging the strongest version of someone's case). But it is <strong>not</strong> the same as being naive or a pushover. You're not vowing to believe the best forever — you're choosing the <em>leanest</em> explanation as your starting point and updating the moment evidence arrives. Charity is a default, not a blindfold.
</Callout>
