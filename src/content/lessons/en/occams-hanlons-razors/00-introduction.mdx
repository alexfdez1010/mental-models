Your boss didn't reply to your message. It's been six hours. Your brain, helpful
as ever, has already written three endings: *they're furious with me*, *I'm about
to be fired*, *the whole team is talking about me behind my back.* None of these
has any evidence. All of them feel true. And the boring fourth option — *they're
busy and haven't opened it* — barely got a vote.

This happens because the world hands you events, not explanations. The message
goes unanswered; the website goes down; the friend doesn't show. Each event fans
out into a crowd of possible causes, and you can't chase all of them. You need a
fast, cheap way to decide which explanation to bet on **first** — before the
evidence is in, before you've spent an afternoon investigating the wrong thing.

## What a "razor" actually is

A **razor** is a rule of thumb that "shaves off" unlikely explanations so you can
focus on the promising ones. The name is a metaphor: you're trimming away the
explanations that probably don't belong, the way a razor trims away what you don't
want to keep.

Here is the single most important thing about a razor, and we're going to repeat it
until it's annoying: **a razor gives you a prior, not a proof.** A *prior* is a
smart starting bet — your best guess *before* the full evidence arrives. A *proof*
is a settled conclusion. A razor only ever does the first job. It tells you where
to point your attention first; it never tells you that you're right. Forget this,
and a razor stops being a tool and becomes a blindfold — a story you defend instead
of a guess you test.

## The two razors you're here to learn

This course teaches the two most useful razors ever invented. They trim different
kinds of clutter.

**Occam's razor** trims *complexity*. Among explanations that fit the facts equally
well, it says prefer the one that requires the **fewest assumptions**. The website
went down right after you pushed a deploy? "The deploy broke it" needs one
assumption; "a hacker timed a zero-day attack to your deploy window" needs three or
four. Bet on the deploy first.

**Hanlon's razor** trims *paranoia*. It says never attribute to **malice** what is
adequately explained by **incompetence** or plain carelessness. Your boss's
silence? "They're plotting against me" assumes intent, coordination, and a reason.
"They're swamped" assumes a busy person. Bet on busy first.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version of each">
**Occam:** prefer the explanation with the fewest moving parts. **Hanlon:** prefer
"someone messed up" over "someone's out to get me." Both are bets you make *first*
and then update — never hills you die on.
</Callout>

Notice they're cousins. Hanlon's razor is really just Occam's razor pointed at
human behavior: a malicious plot is an *assumption-heavy* explanation (it needs
intent, competence, secrecy, and coordination all at once), while a simple screw-up
needs almost nothing. We'll make that link explicit — and quantitative — as we go.

## The map of the course

Four short teaching lessons, then an exam you can't undo. The route:

1. **Occam's Razor** — what it really says (and the two things everyone gets
   wrong about it), how to count the assumptions an explanation smuggles in, and
   why "simpler" never means "simplest at all costs."
2. **Why Fewer Assumptions Win** — the arithmetic under the razor. Every
   assumption is another thing that has to be true, and probabilities multiply
   *downward*, so more moving parts means more ways to be wrong. This is where the
   razor stops being a vibe and becomes math.
3. **Hanlon's Razor** — incompetence before malice, why it's the antidote to two
   specific thinking errors, and the guardrail that stops it from excusing real
   bad actors.
4. **When the Razors Lie to You** — the most important lesson: exactly where each
   razor fails, so you can use them without being used by them.

Then a **Final Exam** — graded, one question at a time, and one-way: once you
answer, it locks. No back button, no retries.

### How to use this course

One rule does most of the work: **guess before you peek.** When you hit an
exercise, commit to an answer in your head before revealing anything. The little
sting of being wrong is what makes an idea stick — a smooth, agreeable read teaches
you almost nothing. The exercises aren't a quiz *on* the lesson; they *are* the
lesson.
