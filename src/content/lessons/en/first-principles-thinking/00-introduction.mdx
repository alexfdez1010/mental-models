Two people are stuck on the exact same problem. Same facts, same deadline, same coffee going cold beside them.

The first one asks: *how is this normally done? What did the last team do? What does everyone in this industry do?* Reasonable questions. They find the standard answer, ship it, and go home. This is reasoning **by analogy** — solving a new problem by matching it to old ones and copying what worked there.

The second one asks something stranger: *what do we actually know is true here — really, for-certain true — and what must follow from that?* They ignore the standard answer entirely and rebuild from the ground up. Most days this is slower and they land roughly where the first person did. But every so often, the rebuilt answer is one nobody else in the room could see. This is reasoning **from first principles** — and that occasional, others-can't-find-it answer is the entire promise of this course.

## What first-principles thinking is

Here's the one-paragraph teaser (the full, careful definition gets its own lesson). **First-principles thinking** means boiling a problem down to the bedrock facts you're genuinely sure are true, throwing out everything you merely *assumed* or *inherited*, and then building an answer back up from that bedrock — instead of copying whatever answer is lying around. The phrase is older than any startup founder who's borrowed it: Aristotle called a first principle "the first basis from which a thing is known" — the irreducible starting point you can't break down any further. That's all a first principle is. The ground floor of what you know.

## Why copying usually works (and why that's the trap)

Now, before this turns into a sermon against reasoning by analogy — copying the usual answer is *good*. Most of the time. It's fast, it's cheap, and it's right often enough that defaulting to it isn't lazy, it's wise. You don't re-derive the laws of cooking every time you make toast. You copy "toaster, two minutes," because the standard answer is correct and re-thinking it would be a waste of a perfectly good morning.

The trap is that analogy works *quietly* and *most* of the time — so we reach for it even in the cases where it fails. And it fails in three situations: when the standard answer is flat-out **wrong**, when there *is* no standard answer (genuinely new problem), or when the answer is merely **inherited** — "we've always done it this way" — and nobody alive remembers why. First principles is the tool you pull out exactly then, and ideally only then. Knowing when *not* to bother is its own skill — we give it a whole lesson near the end.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
First-principles thinking = **reason up from what's actually true, not from what's usually done.** If you remember nothing else from this course, remember that sentence — everything ahead is just learning to do it on purpose.
</Callout>

## The map of the course

Four short teaching lessons, then one exam you can't undo. Here's the route:

1. **Analogy vs. First Principles** — the core distinction, made precise. When each one is the right tool, and how to feel the difference between "copying" and "rebuilding."
2. **The "Why" Ladder** — the actual technique: how to decompose a problem down to bedrock by asking *why* until you hit something you can't break further, and how to tell an assumption from a fact (harder than it sounds).
3. **The Battery Pack** — a famous worked example you rebuild yourself, step by step, so the method stops being abstract and becomes a thing your hands can do.
4. **When NOT to Use First Principles** — the cost, the judgment call, and why a first-principles thinker who *never* copies the standard answer is just an exhausting person who reinvents toast.

Then a **Final Exam** — graded, one question at a time, and one-way: once you answer, it locks. No back button, no retries. You'll be ready for it.

### How to use this course

One rule, and it does most of the work: **guess before you peek.** When you hit an exercise, commit to an answer in your head before you reveal anything. The little jolt of being wrong is what makes the idea stick — a smooth, agreeable read-through teaches you almost nothing. The exercises aren't a quiz on the lesson; they *are* the lesson. The prose is just there to set them up.
