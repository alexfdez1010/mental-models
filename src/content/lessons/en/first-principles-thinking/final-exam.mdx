first principles at all. Take your time and reason each question through; a few
look easy until you spot the trap.

<Callout variant="success" title="Key takeaways">
Reasoning by analogy copies a known answer — fast, cheap, and right most of the
time, but it quietly inherits the example's assumptions. First-principles thinking
decomposes a problem to the truths you can verify and rebuilds from there; it
doesn't mean forgetting what you know, only refusing to let assumptions pose as
facts. Use the why-ladder to drill to bedrock, the "how do I know this?" test to
separate facts from inherited beliefs, and remember the battery pack: a rebuilt
floor is a *lower bound*, not a price tag. Finally, know when not to bother —
first principles is slow, so save it for high-stakes problems, failing standard
answers, and assumptions that deserve questioning, and check Chesterton's fence
before you tear anything down.
</Callout>
