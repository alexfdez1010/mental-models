Picture two ways your brain could answer the question *"how common is this?"* The
first is slow and honest: go find the numbers, count, compare. The second is
instant and effortless: notice how quickly an example comes to mind, and let that
feeling stand in for the answer. A flood of easy examples feels like "common"; a
struggle to think of even one feels like "rare." Your brain runs the second method
almost every time, because it's free and usually close enough — and it never
mentions that it switched. This course is about the moments when that swap quietly
hands you the wrong answer.

That mental shortcut has a name: the **availability heuristic** — judging how
likely or how frequent something is by how *easily* relevant examples come to
mind. (A **heuristic** is just a mental rule of thumb: a fast, good-enough
shortcut your mind uses instead of a full calculation.) It was identified by
psychologists **Daniel Kahneman** and **Amos Tversky** in the early 1970s, and it
is one of the most reliable, repeatable quirks in all of human judgment. Most of
the time it works, because things you bump into often genuinely are easier to
recall. The danger lives in everything *other* than frequency that also makes a
memory easy to summon — and those things distort your sense of the odds without
you ever noticing the distortion.

## The one sentence to take away

Before the detail, here's the whole model compressed to a line:

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
**The availability heuristic makes you estimate how *likely* something is by how *easily* you can picture it — so whatever is vivid, recent, emotional, or endlessly repeated feels common, even when it's rare.** If you remember nothing else, keep the question: *"Am I judging the real frequency, or just how fast an example came to mind?"*
</Callout>

Notice the trap built into it: ease of recall *feels* like evidence. When examples
flood in, the sheer fluency of it lands as a little signal of *this happens a lot*
— and that signal is completely blind to *why* the examples came easily. A single
horrifying news story you saw last night can summon faster than a statistic you
read a year ago about something a thousand times more common. The story wins the
recall race, so it wins your sense of the odds — and you walk away genuinely
believing the rare thing is the frequent one.

## Why the easiest memories mislead

The availability heuristic isn't a malfunction — it's a clever bet that usually
pays off. In the world our minds evolved for, the things you encountered most
often really were the things easiest to remember, so "easy to recall" was a
decent proxy for "happens a lot." The bet only fails when something *other* than
frequency inflates how easily a memory comes — and in a modern world of news
feeds, viral video, and blockbuster movies, that happens constantly. Four forces
do most of the damage, and you'll meet each in detail later: **vividness** (a
gory, concrete image sticks; a dry number slides off), **recency** (what happened
this week feels more typical than what happened for the last decade), **emotion**
(fear and outrage burn memories in deep), and **repetition** (the more often the
media replays a story, the more available — and therefore "common" — it feels).

Here's the consequence that should unsettle you: a newsroom doing its job
*perfectly* will still leave you miscalibrated. News is, by definition, the rare
and the dramatic — "plane lands safely" is not a headline, "plane crashes" is. So
the events that get the most coverage are precisely the ones that happen *least*,
and the slow, common killers (heart disease, diabetes) get almost no airtime at
all. Feed your sense of risk off the news and you'll end up afraid of the rare
spectacular thing while ignoring the mundane thing far more likely to actually get
you. Your fears will be a near-perfect *inverse* of the real danger.

## The map of the course

Four short teaching lessons, then one exam you can't undo. The route:

1. **What the Availability Heuristic Is** — the precise definition from Kahneman
   and Tversky, the deeper idea of **attribute substitution** (your mind swapping
   the hard question "how frequent?" for the easy one "how easily recalled?"), and
   the classic experiments that pinned it down — including why people are sure
   more English words start with *K* than have *K* in the third position (they're
   wrong).
2. **What Cranks Availability Up** — the four engines that make a memory easy to
   summon independent of how often it really happens: **vividness, recency,
   emotion, and repetition**. Each one, worked through a real misjudged risk.
3. **How the News Rewrites Your Risk Map** — the headline machine up close: why
   coverage runs *inverse* to frequency, the shark-attack-versus-heart-disease
   gap, and an interactive chart where you watch a population's fears drift away
   from what actually kills it.
4. **Cascades, Base Rates, and the Fix** — how one story repeated becomes an
   **availability cascade** that sweeps a whole society; why availability is the
   exact mechanism behind **base-rate neglect** (the link back to thinking in
   probabilities); and the practical toolkit for catching the heuristic in the
   act.

Then a **Final Exam** — graded, one question at a time, one-way: once you answer,
it locks. No back button, no retries.

### How to use this course

One rule does most of the work: **guess before you peek.** When you hit an
exercise, commit to an answer before revealing anything. The small sting of being
wrong is exactly what makes the idea stick — and on this particular subject,
notice the irony: a smooth, nodding read-through gives you a vivid *memory* of
having learned availability bias, which your brain will then misread as actually
*understanding* it. Don't let the feeling of fluency stand in for the real thing.
The exercises are where the learning happens.
