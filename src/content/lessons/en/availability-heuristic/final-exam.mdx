## Key takeaways

<Callout variant="success" title="Key takeaways">
The **availability heuristic** is the mind’s habit of judging how **likely or frequent**
something is by how **easily an example comes to mind** — identified by **Kahneman and
Tversky** in the early 1970s. Underneath it runs **attribute substitution**: the mind
quietly swaps the hard question ("how frequent?") for the easy one ("how easily
recalled?") and never tells you, and it tracks the **felt ease** of recall rather than
the amount of evidence (the **K-position**, **famous-names**, and 12-vs-6 results all
show this). Four **engines** crank availability up independent of real frequency —
**vividness, recency, emotion, and repetition** — and they **stack**, which is why
terrorism, sharks, and plane crashes feel far deadlier than the mundane killers (heart
disease, the drive to the airport) that actually get you; the heuristic fears the
**footage, not the frequency**. **News inverts your risk map**: coverage runs roughly
**inverse to frequency** (a **selection** effect, not dishonesty), so heavy coverage
flags **rare** events — and even perfect reporting miscalibrates you. Repeated stories
swell into **availability cascades** that sweep whole societies, and availability is the
exact **mechanism behind base-rate neglect**: the vivid case shouts louder than the
boring statistic. You can’t switch the heuristic off — it fires before you’re aware — but
you can catch it: when a probability *feels* obvious, ask **"am I measuring real
frequency, or just how fast an example came to mind?"**, reach for the **base rate**,
discount **vividness, recency, emotion, and repetition**, and stay suspicious of the feed
engineered to manufacture availability.
</Callout>
