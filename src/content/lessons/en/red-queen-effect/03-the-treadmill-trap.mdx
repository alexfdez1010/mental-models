You've watched two rivals sprint up the treadmill and go nowhere, and you've met the great biological arms races. Now we slow down and stare at the single mistake that all of this is really about — the one your own brain makes about your own life, constantly, without ever announcing it. It's the trap at the heart of the Red Queen, and it has fooled people far smarter than the both of us.

Here it is, stated as a confession you've probably made: *"I got better this year, so I must be getting ahead."* That sentence feels airtight. It is, in fact, only true half the time — and the half where it's false is exactly the half where it matters most. This lesson is the cleanup crew. We're going to name the illusion precisely, hand you a single diagnostic that tells you which world you're standing in, and then teach you the move almost nobody makes: knowing when to stop running and *change the race* instead of just running faster in it. As always, guess before you peek.

## The treadmill, named

**The analogy.** Imagine you're at a packed stadium and the people in front of you stand up to see the game better. So you stand up too — and now you *can* see, briefly. But everyone behind you stands as well, and within seconds the whole stadium is on its feet. Look around: nobody can see any better than when everyone was sitting. You're all just standing now, legs aching, view identical. Here's the cruel part — you can't fix it by sitting back down, because the moment *you* sit, the wall of standing people in front blocks you completely. Standing bought you nothing; sitting would cost you everything. Welcome to the treadmill.

**The precise definition.** The **treadmill trap** is the error of measuring your improvement against your *past self* — an absolute yardstick — and concluding you're winning, when the scoreboard that actually decides the outcome is your *position relative to rivals who are improving too*. You feel the burn of genuine effort, you see real gains over last year, and your intuition files that under "progress." But if everyone you're competing with made the same gains, your *position* hasn't moved — and position was the whole game. The trap isn't that the improvement is fake. The improvement is real. The trap is mistaking it for an advantage.

The hinge, one more time, is the distinction this whole course is built on: **absolute** improvement is "I am better than I was." **Relative** position is "I am ahead of my rivals." In a fixed world — paying off a debt, learning to swim, fixing a leaky roof — those two are the same thing, so the confusion never bites. But the instant your competitors improve *in response to you*, the two come apart, and the treadmill trap is what happens when you keep using the absolute yardstick to score a relative game.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The central trap, in one sentence">
**"I improved, therefore I got ahead" is only true when nobody you're competing with improved too.** The moment your rivals are running the same race — matching your gains, copying your moves, adapting right back — your absolute improvement can be enormous and your relative position can sit dead still. Measuring progress against your past self feels honest and is sometimes a lie. Always ask the second question: *did everyone else improve too?* If they did, you ran flat out to stay in the same place.
</Callout>

### When to use it

Reach for this any time you catch yourself — or a company, or a country — celebrating an improvement and concluding you've pulled ahead. Stop and ask the two-part question: *better than my past self, yes — but did everyone I'm competing with improve too?* If the answer to the second is "yes," your gain may have bought you a standstill, and you should price your effort accordingly. The pitfall in the other direction (we'll get to it) is concluding the running is therefore *pointless*. It usually isn't. But the first job is simply to stop confusing "I improved" with "I got ahead."

## Positional vs. absolute goods — the diagnostic that does all the work

The whole skill of escaping the treadmill comes down to one classification you can learn in a minute and use for the rest of your life. Economists drew this line, and it's the sharpest tool in this lesson.

**Absolute goods.** An **absolute good** is something whose value is *intrinsic* — it doesn't depend on how much anyone else has. Your health is worth the same to you whether or not your neighbour is healthy. A vaccine protects you regardless of who else is vaccinated (in fact others being vaccinated helps you more). Clean water, literacy, a warm house, the ability to read, a cured disease, knowing how to swim — these are absolute. When you improve an absolute good, the improvement *sticks*, because nobody can erase it by improving alongside you. Two people both learning to read doesn't make either of them less literate. Absolute games are the ones where "I improved, therefore I'm better off" is just *true*.

**Positional goods.** A **positional good** is something whose value depends *entirely on how much you have relative to others* — on your rank, not your amount. Status is the purest example: being "high status" means being *above* other people, which is impossible for everyone at once. So are the best house in the neighbourhood, the top job in a field with few openings, being the fastest gun, a military edge in an arms race, or a place in a fixed number of university slots. The defining feature: **if everyone gets more, nobody is better off**, because the good is the *ranking*, and ranking is zero-sum. Positional goods are exactly where the Red Queen lives — improve all you like, but if your rivals match you, your position is unmoved and your effort cancels out.

**Worked example — the neighbourhood that renovated together.** Suppose "having the nicest house on the street" is what you actually want (a positional good: it's about being *nicer than the neighbours*). You spend 40,000 dollars on a stunning kitchen. For one glorious month, yours is the best house. Then, seeing your renovation, every neighbour renovates too. Now every house on the street is gorgeous — and yours is once again merely average for the street. Collectively the street spent half a million dollars, every kitchen genuinely improved (absolute gain!), and *nobody's relative standing changed* (zero positional gain). That half-million bought a standstill. Compare that to spending those same 40,000 dollars on, say, getting a chronic illness treated — an absolute good. The neighbours treating *their* illnesses too takes nothing away from your recovered health. One game is a treadmill; the other isn't. Telling them apart is the entire skill.

| | Positional good | Absolute good |
|---|---|---|
| **Value depends on** | Your *rank* vs. others | The amount you have, intrinsically |
| **If everyone gets more** | Nobody gains — rank is unchanged | Everyone genuinely gains |
| **Red Queen dynamics?** | Yes — rivals erase your gain | No — your gain sticks |
| **Examples** | Status, best house, top-N job slots, military edge, fame | Health, literacy, clean water, a cured disease, a skill |
| **"I improved, so I'm ahead"** | Often false | True |

<Callout variant="info" title="Most real situations are mixed — and that's the interesting part">
Don't expect every good to sit cleanly in one bucket. Most real things are *mixed*. A university education is partly absolute (you genuinely learn things — knowledge nobody can un-teach you) and partly positional (the *credential* is worth more when fewer people have it, and floods in value as everyone gets one). A high salary is absolute in what it buys (food, shelter, travel) and positional in the status it signals. The skill isn't forcing things into one box — it's learning to *see both components* and ask which one is driving your effort. When you're pouring money into the positional half of a mixed good (the credential, the status, the rank), the Red Queen is waiting. When you're building the absolute half (the actual skill, the real health), your gains will stick.
</Callout>

## Watch what standing still costs

In the intro you used the coevolution race to *feel* the trap: both lines climb, the meter stays near centre, all that running keeps you in the same place. Now use the same island to learn the other half of the lesson — the half that stops you from drawing exactly the wrong conclusion.

Run the race a few generations and watch the familiar pattern: both rivals improve hugely (absolute gains soaring), the relative-advantage meter barely twitches off centre (no positional gain). It looks like wasted effort, doesn't it? *Then press "Stop running (opt out)."* Watch your line go flat while the rival keeps climbing — and watch the meter slam over against you as the gap yawns open. That's the whole point of this section, made visible.

This is the counter-intuitive heart of the trap. Your first instinct, once you realize the running buys no relative gain, is to conclude: *then I should stop — why waste the effort?* The race just showed you why. On a genuine treadmill, **the running isn't offensive, it's defensive.** You can't win by running, but you can absolutely *lose* by stopping — because while you stand still, the rival keeps climbing, and the gap that decides survival opens against you. The gazelle that stops getting faster doesn't get to keep its current safety; it gets eaten, because the cheetahs didn't stop. The company that stops innovating doesn't hold its current market share; it loses it to rivals who kept going.

So both naive readings are wrong. *"I improved, therefore I progressed"* is wrong (the rivals matched you; your position didn't move). And *"the running is therefore wasted, so I'll quit"* is **even more** wrong (quitting on a real treadmill is fatal). The running is the price of staying in the game. The *right* question isn't "should I run harder?" or "should I quit?" — it's the deeper one we turn to now: **should I be in this race at all?**

## When the running is worth it — and when to step off

So you've diagnosed a treadmill. You know running won't get you ahead, but stopping cold will get you killed. What are your actual options? There are really only three sane moves, and knowing which one you're in is the payoff of the whole model.

### Keep running — when stopping costs more than you can afford

Running is the rational choice when the thing you'd lose by stopping is something you genuinely can't afford to lose: survival, staying in business, staying solvent. The gazelle keeps running because the alternative is being eaten. The pharmaceutical company keeps developing new antibiotics because bacteria keep evolving resistance and the alternative is patients dying. The software company keeps shipping security patches because attackers keep finding new holes and the alternative is being breached. In these races the prize for running is *not falling out of existence*, and that prize is worth almost any cost. Here, "all the running you can do to stay in the same place" isn't a tragedy — it's the rent you pay to keep playing a game you must stay in.

### Step off — when the prize is purely positional and the cost keeps escalating

Now the opposite case. Sometimes the prize is *purely* positional and *nobody actually profits from the running* — the whole field just spends more and more to stay in the same relative place. This is a **collective-action trap**: each player rationally escalates because they can't unilaterally stop, but the universal escalation cancels out and just raises everyone's costs. The tutoring arms race from the top of the lesson is exactly this — every family spends more, the kids' relative ranks barely move, and the *only* net effect is that everyone is poorer and more stressed. An advertising war where two rivals spend ever more on ads just to neutralize each other's spending, ending with the same market split at twice the cost, is the same trap. When you spot a purely positional race whose escalating cost buys no one anything, the move is to find a way *out* of it.

### Change the game — the deepest move

Here's the one almost nobody makes, and it's the most powerful thing this model teaches: **the way to win a relative race is often to stop running it and find an absolute game instead.** Instead of sprinting faster on someone else's treadmill, you step onto different ground entirely — a **niche**, a differentiated position, a race where you're not competing head-to-head with the adapting rival at all. The restaurant that can't win the price war stops competing on price and becomes the only place in town serving one beloved specialty. The company that can't out-feature its giant rival stops trying and serves a customer the giant ignores. You didn't run faster; you changed which race you were in — and the rival's adaptations no longer point at you.

And there's a fourth, rarer escape worth flagging because the next lesson develops it: sometimes the runners can **collectively agree to stop**. If everyone's just paying escalating costs to stay even, all of them would be better off if they *all* sat back down at once. That's what arms-control treaties are (nations agreeing to cap weapons), what some regulations do (capping an arms race nobody profits from), and what cartels attempt (firms agreeing not to compete a destructive dimension into the ground). The catch — and it's the whole drama of lesson 5 — is that the incentive to *defect* and stand back up is brutal, so these agreements are fragile. We'll meet the game theory that explains why next.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The deepest move: trade the relative race for an absolute one">
When you find yourself on a treadmill — pouring effort into a positional good your rivals keep erasing — the instinct is to run harder. The master move is to ask whether you can leave the race entirely. **You usually can't *win* a purely relative race by running; you win it by refusing to play it and finding an absolute game instead** — a niche, a differentiated position, a place where your improvement *sticks* because no adapting rival is matching you stride for stride. Don't ask only "how do I run faster?" Ask "is this even a race I should be in?"
</Callout>

## Your treadmill checklist — when to use the model

This is the part that pays rent in your own life. Before you pour serious effort, money, or worry into *getting better* at anything competitive, run this short diagnostic. It's the whole lesson compressed into four questions.

1. **Absolute or positional?** Is the value of this prize intrinsic (it's worth the same regardless of others), or does it come from out-*ranking* people? Status, scarce slots, "the best ___," and any edge over a rival are positional. Health, skill, knowledge, and safety are absolute. (Most things are a mix — ask which half your effort is feeding.)
2. **Will rivals erase this?** If you improve, will the people you're competing against improve right back and cancel your gain? If yes, you're on a treadmill and your improvement won't move your position — price it accordingly. If no, your gain sticks; pour effort in freely.
3. **Is the treadmill worth running?** If it *is* a treadmill, what does stopping cost? If stopping means losing something you can't afford (survival, your livelihood), keep running — the running is defensive rent, not waste. If stopping just means forgoing a positional trinket whose cost keeps climbing, that's a signal to look for the exit.
4. **Can I change games?** Before resigning yourself to running forever, ask: is there a different race — a niche, a differentiated position, an absolute game — where my improvement would actually *stick*? The deepest win in a relative race is usually to stop playing it. (And occasionally: can all of us agree to stop running at once? — the fragile fourth option lesson 5 explores.)

<Callout variant="warning" title="The three errors this lesson exists to kill">
Carry these out the door. **(1)** *"I improved, therefore I progressed"* — true only in absolute games; in a positional race where rivals matched you, your improvement is real and your position didn't move. **(2)** *"The running is wasted, so I'll quit"* — dangerous: on a genuine treadmill, stopping doesn't freeze your position, it collapses it. The right response isn't to quit, it's to ask whether to *change games*. **(3)** *"More effort always pays"* — in positional/Red Queen games, *universal* extra effort cancels out and just raises everyone's costs (a collective-action trap). Effort pays in absolute games; in positional ones it can buy a very expensive standstill.
</Callout>

## Recap

You came in able to watch a treadmill go nowhere. You're leaving able to *diagnose* one in your own life and decide what to do about it:

1. **The treadmill trap** is mistaking absolute improvement (better than my past self) for relative progress (ahead of my rivals) when the baseline is moving — when rivals improve right alongside you and cancel your gain. The stadium stands up, and nobody sees better.
2. **Positional vs. absolute goods** is the diagnostic that does all the work. Positional goods (status, scarce slots, a military edge, the best house) get their value from *rank*, so universal improvement cancels out — pure Red Queen. Absolute goods (health, literacy, clean water, a skill) have intrinsic value, so improvement *sticks*. Most real things are a mix; find which half your effort feeds.
3. **The running is defensive, not offensive.** On a genuine treadmill you can't win by running but you *can* lose by stopping, because rivals keep climbing while you stand still. So "the effort is wasted, I'll quit" is the more dangerous of the two errors.
4. **Three moves:** keep running when stopping costs more than you can afford (survival, your business); step off when the prize is purely positional and the escalating cost buys no one anything (a collective-action trap); and — the deepest move — *change the game*: trade the relative race for an absolute one, a niche where your gains stick because no rival is matching you. (Plus the fragile fourth: everyone agreeing to stop at once.)
