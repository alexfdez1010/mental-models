This is the whole course in one sitting. Hold the through-line in your head: whenever your type is hidden behind a verification wall — good worker or bad, healthy peacock or sickly one, reliable seller or fly-by-night — **talk is cheap** because words cost every type the same, so they prove nothing. The only thing that separates the good type from the bad is an **action whose cost differs by type**: a costly signal. Honesty doesn't live in the message; it lives entirely in the **cost gap**. That single move — do something too expensive for the wrong type to fake — is the peacock's tail, the gazelle's stott, the hard degree, the long warranty, the burned bridge, the Veblen watch, the initiation rite. And when the cost gap narrows, mimics flood in and the whole separating equilibrium erodes. Take a breath. There is no going back once you commit.

<Callout variant="success" title="Key takeaways">
You now hold the whole model. Behind a **verification wall**, your **type** is private and **cheap talk** proves nothing — words cost every type the same, so the bad type says them just as freely as the good. Information demands an **action whose cost differs by type**: a **costly signal**. The **Spence single-crossing / separating condition** is the engine of everything here — a signal separates the good type from the bad **exactly when it is cheaper for the good type to send** — so **honesty lives entirely in the cost gap**. In biology this is Zahavi's **handicap principle**: the peacock's tail and the gazelle's **stott** are honest *because* they are costly in a currency only high quality can spare, an **index** (impossible to fake) or a **handicap** (too costly to fake), selected at the level of the **individual**, not "the good of the species." In markets it is Spence's **job-market signalling** (a degree sorts by ability even if it teaches nothing, hence the **sheepskin effect**), and the family of lemons-fixes — **warranties, brands, skin-in-the-game, costly advertising** — plus the **screening** (uninformed builds the test) vs **signalling** (informed pays to reveal) distinction and the **credential-inflation** arms race. In strategy and society it is deterrence by **sinking costs** (pay up front) or **tying hands** (**burn the bridge** so backing down hurts), **conspicuous consumption / Veblen goods**, virtue signalling, and costly **initiation rites** — all one move: an action too costly for the wrong type. The **equilibria** come in three flavours — **pooling** (all the same, receiver keeps the prior), **separating** (types split, needs the cost gap), **semi-separating** (probabilistic, partial reveal) — with **countersignalling** as the twist where the secure top skip the signal (the billionaire in a hoodie). And the model **lies** at its edges: when the **cost gap narrows**, **dishonest mimicry** (diploma mills, counterfeits, **Batesian mimics**) erodes the separating equilibrium toward pooling; positional races burn **deadweight waste**; **revelation vs causation** is a genuine confound (does the degree reveal or build ability?); over-reading a **noisy one-shot** signal over-fits to luck; and **cheap talk works fine when interests are aligned** — the whole apparatus is for adversarial, conflicting-interest settings. Never ask "what did they say?" Ask: *what did it cost them to say it, and could the wrong type have afforded the same?*
