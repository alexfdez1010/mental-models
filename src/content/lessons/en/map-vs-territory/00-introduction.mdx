Hold a subway map next to a satellite photo of the same city and they barely look
related. The map's lines are straight; the real tracks curl. Its stations sit at
tidy intervals; the real ones are bunched downtown and sparse in the suburbs. The
river is a smooth blue ribbon instead of a muddy, branching mess. By every
literal measure, the subway map is *wrong*.

And yet you'd never navigate the underground with the satellite photo. The photo
has *too much* truth in it — every rooftop, every car, every tree — and somewhere
buried in all that detail is the one fact you actually need: *the blue line gets
me to the museum.* The map is a deliberate, useful lie. It threw away the city so
you could find the train.

That is the whole course in one picture. Every model you think with — every
theory, rule of thumb, stereotype, formula, and mental shortcut — is a map. It
buys its usefulness by leaving things out. The danger is never that maps simplify;
maps *must* simplify. The danger is *forgetting that yours did*, and walking
confidently off the edge of what it shows.

## Why this is the root model

In the very first course you met the line "the map is not the territory" as *one*
idea among several. This whole course exists because it isn't really one idea
among several — it's the soil the others grow in. First-principles thinking is
"rebuild the map from the territory." Circle of competence is "know which maps you
actually hold." Inversion, base rates, second-order thinking — every one of them
is a map you're choosing to lay over a messy world. If you don't deeply
understand what a map *is* and where it lies, you'll misuse all the others.

So we're going to slow all the way down and take this idea apart properly: what it
means, why simplification is a feature and not a bug, the famous companion line
"all models are wrong, but some are useful," the exact situations where the gap
bites, the sneaky error of mistaking the model for the world, and the handful of
habits that keep your maps honest.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
**Every model is a map: a useful, deliberately incomplete stand-in for reality.**
Plan with it — you have no choice — but never stop watching for the places it and
the territory disagree.
</Callout>

## The map of this course

Four short teaching lessons, then one exam you can't undo. Here's the route:

1. **The Map Is Not the Territory** — the core claim, made precise. Why the value
   of a map *is* what it omits, why a perfect 1:1 map would be useless, and three
   different ways of saying the same warning so it sticks.
2. **All Models Are Wrong, but Some Are Useful** — George Box's companion line,
   read correctly. We tour maps across wildly different domains (finance,
   stereotypes, GDP, org charts) and see the same pattern every time.
3. **When the Map Drifts** — the three situations where the gap actually bites:
   near the model's edges, when the stakes are high, and when the territory has
   moved but the map hasn't. With an interactive stale-map you can scrub through.
4. **Reification, and How to Stay Honest** — the deepest trap (mistaking the
   model for the thing) and the habits that defuse it: hold maps loosely, check
   them against reality, and keep several maps of the same ground.

Then a **Final Exam** — graded, one question at a time, one-way. Once you answer,
it locks. You'll be ready.

### How to use this course

One rule does most of the work: **guess before you peek.** When you hit an
exercise, commit to an answer in your head before you reveal anything. The little
jolt of being wrong is what makes an idea stick; a smooth, nodding read-through
teaches you almost nothing. The exercises aren't a test *of* the lesson — they
*are* the lesson. The prose just sets them up.
