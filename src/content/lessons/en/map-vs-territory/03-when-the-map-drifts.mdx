You now know every map omits detail and that's a feature. So why isn't this whole
course just "shrug, all maps are wrong, carry on"? Because the omission is harmless
right up until the precise moment it isn't — and the difference between a harmless
gap and a catastrophic one is *predictable.* The gap bites in three recognisable
situations. Learn to feel them coming and you've turned a vague humility into an
actual early-warning system.

## Danger 1: You're near the edge of the model

Every model has a range it was built and tested in — its comfortable middle — and
that's where it earns its keep. The **edges** are where the dropped detail lives,
because the edges are the situations nobody bothered to capture.

The classic example: *"water boils at 100°C."* A perfectly good map — at sea
level. Climb a mountain, where the air pressure is lower, and water boils at maybe
85°C. Your "100°C" map now leaves you with weirdly undercooked pasta, because
you're operating outside the conditions the model quietly assumed. The model didn't
*lie*; you walked off its edge, where the detail it dropped (air pressure) suddenly
matters.

This is everywhere. A diet that works for a sedentary office worker is an edge case
for an ultramarathoner. A parenting rule tuned for a toddler is off the edge for a
teenager. **The middle of a model's range is where it's most trustworthy; the
edges are where it secretly fails.**

<Callout variant="warning" title="'Validated' means 'validated somewhere'">
When someone says a model is "tested" or "validated," always ask: *tested on
what?* A model validated on normal conditions tells you almost nothing about how it
behaves at the extreme you're about to bet on. Validation is a statement about the
*middle*, not a promise about the *edges.*
</Callout>

## Danger 2: The stakes are high

The size of the gap between map and territory matters only in proportion to what's
riding on it. If you're guessing where to grab lunch, a rough mental map is plenty:
being wrong costs you a ten-minute walk. The omission is real and totally
harmless.

Crank the stakes and the same-sized omission becomes lethal. A pilot, a surgeon, or
someone betting their life savings is working with maps that drop *some* detail —
and now the dropped detail can kill. **The higher the stakes, the more a small
map–territory gap is worth checking,** because the cost of the gap scales with the
consequence, not with the gap's size.

This gives you a cheap rule: spend your scepticism where it pays. Don't audit your
mental map of the cereal aisle. Do audit it before the irreversible, expensive,
or dangerous decision — that's where the same crack in the same map suddenly
matters.

## Danger 3: The territory moved but the map didn't

The first two dangers are about *space* (the edges) and *weight* (the stakes). The
third is about *time.* Maps go stale. A road closes, a market shifts, a competitor
pivots, a friend changes — the territory quietly moves on, but the model frozen in
your head stays pinned to its last update. And here's the cruel part: **acting on a
stale map feels exactly like acting on a current one,** right up until you drive
into the road that closed last month.

The deadly thing about staleness is that there's no internal alarm. A fresh map and
a five-year-old map *feel* identically trustworthy from the inside — confidence is
a property of the map, remember, not of the territory. The only defence is to date
your maps: to ask *"when was this belief last checked against reality, and has the
world moved since?"*

<Reveal client:visible label="A worked case: the general fighting the last war" buttonLabel="Show the example">
<Callout variant="warning" title="The map that won the last war">
"Generals always fight the last war" is this danger in a sentence. At the start of
World War I, commanders carried mental maps drawn in earlier wars of cavalry charges
and quick, mobile battles. The territory had moved — machine guns and barbed wire
had arrived — but the maps hadn't, and men were sent charging across open ground
into fire as if it were 1870. The maps had been *correct.* That was the trap: they
felt as reliable as ever, because a stale map never feels stale. It just keeps
quietly pointing at a world that's gone.
</Callout>
</Reveal>

## Three dangers, one table

| Danger | What's drifting | Tell-tale sign | Cheap defence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Edges** | You're outside the model's tested range | "This case feels unusual / extreme" | Ask what conditions the model assumed |
| **High stakes** | The cost of being wrong is large | "This is expensive / irreversible" | Audit the map before you commit |
| **Stale map** | Time has moved the territory | "When did I last check this?" | Date your beliefs; re-verify |

Notice they often gang up. The 2008 financial crisis was all three at once: risk
models assumed house prices basically never fall everywhere at the same time (an
edge case nobody had stress-tested), the stakes were the global economy, and the
maps were years out of date relative to how the mortgage market had actually
changed. When all three bite together, you get a disaster that, in hindsight,
"the model should have seen" — but the model never could, because it was a map.

## When to use it

This lesson converts a fuzzy "stay humble" into a concrete checklist. Run it
exactly when a decision is unusual, expensive, or based on an old belief:

- **Feel an edge?** Ask what the model assumed and whether you've left that zone.
- **Feel weight?** Slow down and audit the map in proportion to the stakes.
- **Feel age?** Date the belief and re-check it against today's territory.
- **And don't run it on everything** — auditing every trivial map is its own
  failure mode (paralysis). Save the scrutiny for the edges, the big bets, and the
  beliefs that have had time to rot.

There's one last failure mode that's deeper and sneakier than all three — the one
where you stop noticing there's a map at all and start treating the model *as* the
world. It has a name, and defeating it is the final lesson.
