In 1976 the statistician George Box wrote a line that has become the perfect
companion to Korzybski's: **"All models are wrong, but some are useful."** Box was
talking about statistical models, but the sentence escaped the lab and now applies
to every map you think with.

It's a deceptively dangerous sentence, because there are two ways to read it and
beginners almost always grab the wrong one.

## The wrong half and the right half

**The wrong takeaway:** *"All models are wrong, so models are useless — just look
at reality directly."* This sounds tough-minded and is actually impossible. You
**can't** look at reality directly; reality is the territory, and it's far too big
and detailed to hold in a human head. The moment you try to think about anything
at all, you reach for a map. Refusing all models doesn't connect you to raw truth;
it just leaves you using maps you refuse to admit you're using.

**The right takeaway:** *Since every model is wrong somewhere, your job is not to
find the one perfect model. It's to (1) choose a model that's useful for the
question in front of you, and (2) stay awake to where that model is wrong.*
"Wrong but useful" isn't a verdict against models — it's the correct way to hold
every single one. A weather forecast is wrong (it won't nail the exact
temperature) and enormously useful (bring the umbrella). Both halves are true at
once, and that's fine.

"All models are wrong" → relax; you'll never find a flawless map, so stop demanding
one. "But some are useful" → that's not a problem, it's the job — pick the map fit
for *this* question and keep one eye on its edges.
</Callout>

## The same pattern, in four very different maps

The reason this idea is foundational is that *everything* you reason with turns out
to be a map — not just literal maps and weather forecasts. Watch the identical
pattern (useful structure preserved, crucial detail dropped) appear in four
domains that look nothing alike.

### 1. A financial model is not the business

A spreadsheet model of a company captures revenue, costs, and growth in tidy
cells. It's genuinely useful — you can test "what if we raise prices 10%?" without
risking real money. But it silently drops the founder's burnout, the key engineer
about to quit, the lawsuit nobody's mentioned, and the customer whose loyalty is
purely habit. The model says "the business"; it means "the parts of the business
that fit in cells." Mistake one for the other and you'll be blindsided by the
thing that was never in the spreadsheet.

### 2. A stereotype is not the person

A stereotype is a brutally compressed map of a group of people. Like all maps it's
a generalisation that's sometimes structurally useful (you do, sensibly, expect a
stranger in a pilot's uniform to know how to fly). But applied to *an individual*
it's wrong in the way that matters most: a person is a territory, full of detail
the category never recorded. The error isn't having categories — your brain can't
function without them — it's treating the category as the person. The map is not
the territory; the *group label* is not the human.

### 3. GDP is not wellbeing

Gross Domestic Product is a map of a country's economy: the money value of what it
produces. Hugely useful, and it does mirror real structure — richer countries
genuinely tend to have better health and schools. But GDP cheerfully counts a
traffic jam's extra fuel and a hurricane's rebuilding as "growth," while ignoring
unpaid care work, clean air, and whether anyone is actually happy. Govern as if
GDP *were* national wellbeing and you'll optimise the map while the territory —
people's actual lives — drifts somewhere the number never looked.

### 4. The org chart is not the organisation

A company's org chart maps who reports to whom. Useful for "whose budget is this?"
— and almost a lie about how work really happens. The real organisation runs on a
hidden territory of friendships, favours, the one veteran everyone secretly asks,
and the back-channel where decisions actually get made. New managers who trust the
org chart as the territory keep wondering why pulling the official levers does
nothing.

## Why "wrong" doesn't mean "useless"

Put the four cases together and a rule pops out: **a map being wrong tells you
nothing about whether it's useful.** Usefulness is a separate question — *does this
map's preserved structure help me with the question I'm actually asking?* A subway
map is wildly geometrically wrong and perfect for catching a train. A photorealistic
painting of the subway is far more "accurate" and useless for navigation. Accuracy
and usefulness are different axes.

| | Wrong (omits a lot) | "Accurate" (omits little) |
|---|---|---|
| **Useful for the job** | Subway map for catching a train; GDP for comparing economies | A surgeon's anatomical atlas for an operation |
| **Useless for the job** | A subway map for hiking the coast | A 1:1 map; a satellite photo for the underground |

The takeaway: stop grading maps on "how wrong is it?" and start grading them on
"is it useful *for this*, and do I know where it stops being so?" That second
clause is the whole next lesson.

There's a clever-sounding move where someone learns "all models are wrong" and
uses it to wave away any claim they dislike: *"well, that's just a model."* True —
and useless. Yes, the climate model is a model; yes, the epidemiologist's forecast
is a model; so is every alternative the sceptic prefers, including "it'll probably
be fine." "It's a model" is not a counterargument, because the only thing you can
ever compare a model to is *another model.* The honest question is never "is this a
model?" (everything is) but "**which** available map has the best track record for
*this* question?" Cynicism about all maps is just a sneaky way to smuggle in your
favourite map unexamined.

## When to use it

Keep Box's line handy in three moments:

- **When you're tempted to dismiss a model for being imperfect.** Imperfect is the
  baseline. The question is whether it's *useful here* and whether you know its
  edges — not whether it's flawless.
- **When you're tempted to trust a model completely because it's been "validated"
  or "data-driven."** Those are still maps. Ask what they were built for and what
  they therefore drop.
- **When you catch yourself saying "that's just a model" to win an argument.** Make
  yourself name the *better* map and its track record, or admit you're just
  defending your own.

So every map is wrong and some are useful — fine. But *when*, exactly, does the
wrongness stop being harmless and start costing you? That's not random. It bites in
three recognisable situations, and spotting them in advance is the real skill.
