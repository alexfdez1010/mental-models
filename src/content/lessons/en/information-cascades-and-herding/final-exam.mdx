<Callout variant="success" title="Key takeaways">
An information cascade forms when the visible actions of others outweigh your own
single private signal, so copying them is the rational move — and the instant you
copy against your own evidence, your action stops carrying information and the
crowd stops learning. That makes a cascade *rational for each person yet fragile
and often wrong for the group*: it rests on a handful of early signals, so a single
credible public signal or one well-informed contrarian can shatter and reverse it.
It wears many faces — queues, bestseller lists, bubbles, standing ovations,
citation and reputational herding — and it must be told apart from mere conformity
(social pressure) and from genuine consensus (independent evidence actually
combined). Never read "everyone is doing it" as "it is true." The cure is always
the same: keep independent signals alive — aggregate votes and prices that reveal
information rather than copyable actions, protect contrarians, and sequence or hide
early choices so the crowd can pool what it actually knows.
</Callout>
