Here is a result that should worry anyone who has studied this far. Psychologists
handed people a plain-language description of some cognitive bias — say, the tendency
to take more credit for success than for failure — and then asked two questions: *how
much does this bias affect the average person?* and *how much does it affect you?*
Almost everyone gave the same answer. The average person: quite a lot. Me personally:
much less. They had just read the definition. It changed nothing.

That gap — *everyone else is biased, I'm mostly fine* — is itself a bias, and it is one
of the most reliable findings in the field. It is called the **bias blind spot**, and
it is the reason this whole course exists. You have spent the psychology track
learning to name distortions: confirmation bias, anchoring, sunk cost, overconfidence.
Naming them is genuinely useful for spotting them *in an argument, in a forecast, in
someone else*. What it does **not** do — and this is the part almost nobody believes
until they measure it — is protect you from committing them yourself.

## Why "just know about it" fails

The intuitive model of debiasing is a mental smoke alarm: learn what the bias feels
like, and next time it flares up you will notice and stop. It is a lovely theory. It is
also mostly wrong, and for a specific reason — **biases operate below the level of
awareness.** The anchoring number pulls your estimate before you consciously weigh it.
Confirmation bias steers what you even *think to search for*. By the time the thought
reaches your inner narrator, the distortion has already happened, and your narrator's
job is to make the result feel reasonable. Introspection arrives too late and reports
back "looks fine to me."

That is why the blind spot is so sticky. When you look inward for a bias and find
nothing, you take the *absence of a felt bias* as evidence you are unbiased — but a
bias you could feel would not be much of a bias. The clean conclusion, "I'm objective,"
is exactly the error.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The trap in one sentence">
Knowing about a bias lets you catch it in **other people's** reasoning far better than
in your own — because your own biases don't announce themselves, and the confident
feeling of "but *I'm* being objective here" is itself the bias blind spot at work.
</Callout>

## So what actually works?

If willpower and awareness barely move the needle, what does? The rest of this course
answers that, and the answer sorts cleanly into two families — a distinction worth
fixing in your head right now:

- **Change the thinker.** Disciplines you run inside your own head to force a more
  honest look: the **outside view** (ask how similar cases actually turned out rather
  than reasoning from inside this one), **considering the opposite**, and the
  **pre-mortem** (assume the plan has already failed and explain why). Real, but they
  still depend on you remembering to run them at the moment it counts.
- **Change the environment.** Fixes built into the *process* so they don't depend on
  your willpower: **checklists**, **red teams** and structured dissent, **decision
  hygiene** (independent estimates before anyone talks), simple **rules and
  algorithms**, and **pre-commitment** — binding your future self before the distortion
  arrives.

The punchline of the whole course is a ranking: **structure beats incentives beats
training beats mere awareness.** The further a fix moves *away* from "just try harder to
be objective" and *toward* "build it into the process," the more reliably it works.

<Reveal client:visible buttonText="Why the strongest fixes don't rely on you">
A structural fix — a checklist you can't skip, a colleague assigned to argue the
opposite, everyone writing their estimate down before the meeting — works precisely
*because it doesn't require you to catch yourself*. It sidesteps the bias blind spot
instead of fighting it. The willpower-based fixes ask the biased organ (your own
in-the-moment judgment) to police itself; the structural ones take the policing out of
your hands. That is the entire reason the hierarchy runs the way it does — and why the
humblest move in this course, admitting you can't be trusted to notice, is also the
most powerful.
</Reveal>

## The map of the course

Six teaching lessons, then a final exam you can't undo:

1. **The Bias Blind Spot** — the core result: why you rate yourself less biased than
   average, the introspection illusion that powers it, and why it makes *you* the
   hardest case.
2. **Why Knowing Doesn't Help** — the evidence that education barely debiases, why
   biases run below awareness, and the difference between spotting a bias and
   correcting one.
3. **Change the Thinker** — the willpower-based tools that genuinely help: the outside
   view and reference classes, considering the opposite, the pre-mortem, and
   calibration training with feedback.
4. **Change the Environment** — the stronger, structural tools: checklists, red teams
   and structured dissent, decision hygiene, algorithms over clinical judgment, and
   pre-commitment. You'll drive the **debiasing bench** and watch which fixes actually
   move the error.
5. **The Debiasing Hierarchy** — putting it in order (structure > incentives > training
   > awareness), matching the right fix to the right bias and the right stakes, and a
   full recap.
6. **Where Debiasing Lies to You** — the pitfalls: using "you're just biased" as a
   weapon, over-correcting into useless hedging, paying for hygiene you don't need, and
   the humbling fact that no technique removes bias, it only shrinks it.

Then a **Final Exam** — graded, one question at a time, one-way: once you answer, it
locks. No back button, no retries.

### How to use this course

One habit does most of the work here: **when an exercise describes a judgment, make
your snap call first — out loud or in your head — before you read on.** The point of
this course is felt, not memorised: you have to watch your own confident first instinct
miss, and then watch a structural tool catch what your awareness didn't. The exercises
*are* the lesson.
