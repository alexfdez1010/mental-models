The tools in the last lesson all shared one weakness: they run *inside your head*, so
they depend on you remembering to use them at the exact moment your judgment is already
tilting. But the moment you most need the outside view is the moment you're most sure
you don't — the bias blind spot sees to that. Asking your in-the-moment judgment to
police itself is asking the fox to audit the henhouse.

So the strongest family of fixes stops asking. Instead of trying to make *you* less
biased, it builds the correction into the **environment** — the process, the rules, the
room — so the debiasing happens whether or not you happen to catch yourself. These are
harder to set up and they add friction, but they are far more reliable, because they
don't route through the one thing you can't trust: your own feeling of being objective.

## Checklists: offload the judgment

A **checklist** takes a judgment you'd otherwise make from memory and confidence, and
turns it into a fixed sequence you physically work through. Its power is counter-
intuitive precisely because the steps are usually *obvious* — and obvious steps are the
ones a confident expert skips. Atul Gawande's *Checklist Manifesto* documents the move
spreading from aviation (where pilots have run checklists for decades, no matter how
senior) into surgery, with the WHO checklist cutting major complications by roughly a
third in the trial hospitals.

The reason it debiases is exact: a checklist doesn't rely on you *feeling* like you
might have missed something. It assumes you will feel fine — overconfidence is the
default — and catches the miss anyway. It converts "trust the expert's gut" into
"verify against the list," which is the whole environment-side move in miniature.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Why obvious-looking steps are the dangerous ones">
The steps a checklist catches are the ones too basic to feel worth checking — which is
exactly why they get skipped under time pressure and confidence. A good checklist is
short, targets the killer items, and is run *out loud* so skipping is visible to
others. It works because it doesn't trust the very feeling ("I've got this") that
causes the error.
</Callout>

## Red teams and structured dissent

Confirmation bias means a group left to itself will hunt for reasons its favoured plan
is right. A **red team** fixes this structurally: you *assign* someone — or a whole
sub-team — the job of attacking the plan, finding how it fails, arguing the other side,
on the record. The disagreement no longer depends on a brave individual risking their
standing to speak up; it's someone's *official role*, so it happens every time.

This is the institutional version of "consider the opposite." Intelligence agencies run
red-cell analysis; some companies appoint a formal devil's advocate; the old Catholic
canonisation process literally employed an *advocatus diaboli* to argue against
sainthood. Worked example: a board about to approve a big acquisition assigns two
directors to build the strongest possible case that it's a mistake and present it
first. If the deal survives a genuine assault, it's stronger; if it only survived
because no one was willing to be the naysayer, the red team just saved the company.

## Decision hygiene: independent estimates before discussion

Here's a bias you create for free the moment a meeting starts: the first number spoken
becomes an **anchor**, and the first confident voice starts a **cascade** — everyone
adjusts toward it and quietly suppresses their own read. By the time the room "agrees,"
you don't have ten independent judgments; you have one judgment echoed ten times, which
*feels* like overwhelming consensus.

**Decision hygiene** (a term from Kahneman, Sibony & Sunstein's *Noise*) fixes this by
changing the order of operations: everyone writes their estimate down *privately, before
anyone speaks*. Then you reveal them together. Now the spread of opinions is real
information — a wide spread flags genuine uncertainty the cascade would have hidden — and
no one is anchored on the loudest colleague. It's the same logic as a secret ballot, and
it's one of the cheapest, highest-leverage structural fixes there is.

<Reveal client:visible buttonText="The subtle part: independence has to come first">
The magic is entirely in the *ordering*. Collect the judgments independently, THEN
discuss — not the other way round. Once people have heard each other, you can't
un-anchor them; the estimates have already collapsed toward the first mover. This is
why "let's go around the room" starting with the senior person is one of the worst
possible protocols: it maximises anchoring and cascade at exactly the moment you wanted
independent signal. Aggregate first, deliberate second. The order is the fix.
</Reveal>

## Algorithms and simple rules over gut

When you have data and a repeatable decision, a boringly simple rule often beats expert
judgment. Paul Meehl's famous review — and the sixty years of studies after it — found
that mechanical/statistical prediction matches or beats trained clinical judgment across
a huge range of tasks, from parole decisions to graduate-school admissions to medical
prognosis. The expert, adding "clinical intuition" on top of the formula, usually makes
it *worse*, because the intuition is where the noise and bias leak back in.

The structural move is to let a checklist-like rule or a model make the call where the
evidence supports one, and reserve human judgment for the genuinely novel. This is not
"computers are smarter"; it's that a consistent rule doesn't have moods, doesn't get
anchored, doesn't remember its hits and forget its misses. Its errors are at least
*consistent*, which makes them fixable.

## Pre-commitment: bind your future self

Some biases don't strike now — they strike *later*, when you're tired, tempted, or in
the grip of a hot emotion. Against those, the structural fix is **pre-commitment**:
while you're clear-headed, you remove your own future options. Ulysses had himself
lashed to the mast so the sirens couldn't tempt him overboard; the modern versions are a
stop-loss that sells automatically, an investment policy statement you write before the
market panics, a diet with no junk food *in the house*, a submitted-in-advance decision
rule your future self can't argue with.

A **pre-commitment device** is a debiasing tool aimed squarely at your own future
distortions — it's the credible-commitment idea turned inward. You can't trust
in-the-moment-you to be objective, so present-you takes the choice away. It's the
purest expression of this whole lesson's humility: the strongest move is admitting you
*will* be biased and building the cage in advance.

<Callout variant="success" title="The environment-side move, in one line">
Don't try to be less biased — **build a process that doesn't need you to be.** A
checklist you can't skip, a colleague assigned to attack the plan, estimates written
before anyone speaks, a rule where the data supports one, a commitment made before the
temptation arrives: each takes the policing out of your unreliable hands and puts it
into the structure, where the bias blind spot can't reach.
</Callout>
