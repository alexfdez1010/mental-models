<Callout variant="success" title="Key takeaways">
A **circle of competence** is the set of subjects where your mental maps are
accurate enough to trust your own judgment — and the whole model lives in its
**edge**, not its area. A small sharp circle beats a big fuzzy one, because the
real danger is the **band just past your boundary**, where things still feel
familiar and confidence keeps climbing after competence has stopped. You cannot
map that boundary by how competent something *feels* (the feeling is
systematically inflated); you map it with **instruments** — scorekeeping with real
probabilities, the "what would I need to know?" test, the teach test, and a wary
eye on lucky wins. Your circle **can** widen, but only the honest way: deliberate
study and real feedback over time, never name-dropping, borrowed conviction, or the
halo effect. And under pressure — FOMO, authority, ego, social proof — staying
inside is rational because the **asymmetry** is brutal: passing costs a bounded
forgone gain, while bluffing across your edge risks unbounded loss. Know where your
competence stops, act inside it, widen it slowly, and pass cleanly outside it —
that is a margin of safety you build for yourself.
</Callout>
