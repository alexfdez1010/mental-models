The last lesson handed you instruments to *locate* your edge — scorekeeping, the "what would I need to know?" test, the teach test, and a guard against the lucky win. But a located edge isn't a prison sentence. The circle isn't fixed at birth; Buffett and Munger spent their careers moving theirs outward. The only question that matters is *how* you move it — because there's an honest way to grow your circle and a counterfeit way that just *feels* like growth. This lesson is about telling them apart.

## The circle can grow — slowly

Here's the good news the title hides: **your circle is not fixed.** You can grow it. The bad news is the speed limit. **Genuine widening = deliberate practice + real feedback + scorekeeping + time** — and there's no shortcut around any of those four.

Think of it like clearing new ground by hand. Your circle is a patch of cleared land where you can actually farm — where your maps match the territory well enough to trust. You widen it the way you'd widen a real clearing: with a machete, stump by stump, *over seasons*. What you cannot do is take out a pen, draw a bigger circle on the map, and declare the new region cleared. The map says "cleared." The ground is still jungle. Faking range is exactly that — redrawing the boundary on the map while the actual terrain stays wild. (This is your prerequisite, **the map is not the territory**, coming back to bite: a bigger circle *drawn* is not a bigger circle *earned*.)

The textbook example of doing it right is Buffett and Munger themselves, and the key word is **decades.** They didn't wake up with a wide circle; they widened it deliberately, in earned stages:

| Era | Where the circle sat | How they got there |
|---|---|---|
| Early (Graham school) | "Cigar-butt" deep value — cheap, beaten-down assets bought below liquidation value | Mastered Benjamin Graham's quantitative method first |
| Middle | Great consumer brands with durable moats — **See's Candies**, **Coca-Cola** | Years of studying *why* certain brands keep their pricing power |
| Later | Technology, carefully — eventually a large **Apple** position | Long study of Apple as a consumer brand with a sticky ecosystem, not a tech gamble |

Each step was *earned*, never asserted. Buffett didn't buy Coca-Cola because consumer brands felt familiar; he bought it after building a real model of why a brand can defend its margins for decades. And the discipline cuts the other way too: through the **late-1990s dot-com boom**, Buffett sat almost entirely *out* of internet stocks. He took heat for it — "Buffett's lost his touch" — but he refused to act inside a circle he hadn't earned. Sitting out is the same muscle as widening: both come from honesty about where the boundary actually is.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The recipe for honest widening">
1. **Study the primary material** — not summaries, threads, or one expert's take. The actual sources, until you can build the mechanism yourself.
2. **Make predictions.** Commit to specific, falsifiable forecasts in the new domain *before* outcomes are known.
3. **Get graded by reality.** Track those forecasts (lesson 3's scorecard) and check your hit rate. Reality is the only examiner who can't be charmed.
4. **Only then claim the terrain.** A region is inside your circle when your model predicts and your scorecard agrees — not a moment before.

This is just **deliberate practice plus feedback loops** wearing work clothes. The feedback is what makes it practice instead of theater.
</Callout>

## Faking range

Now the counterfeit. **Faking range** is anything that produces the *appearance* of competence without the model underneath. It's dangerous precisely because it's convincing — to others, and worst of all, to yourself. Here are its four favorite disguises:

- **Name-dropping and jargon.** Reaching for the field's vocabulary as if the words were the understanding. "It's fundamentally a liquidity-mismatch problem" can be said by someone who couldn't define liquidity mismatch from first principles. Jargon is the *label*; competence is the *machine*.
- **Borrowing conviction from an authority.** "My pick is X because [famous investor] is in it." You've imported a conclusion you can't evaluate. If you can't say *why* the authority is right, you don't hold their competence — you hold their company's stock.
- **Surface pattern-matching.** "This looks just like that other situation I nailed." Matching surface features — the industry, the vibe, the chart shape — while the *causal mechanics* underneath are completely different. The danger zone runs on exactly this resemblance.
- **The halo effect.** The big one. The **halo effect** is letting excellence in one domain radiate into unearned authority in another. Remember the restaurateur from lesson 2 — brilliant at restaurants, who therefore "obviously" knows real estate, then crypto, then politics? That glow is the halo: *success here* gets quietly relabeled as *competence everywhere*. The more impressive you are inside your circle, the more your reputation (and your own ego) will try to extend the franchise rent-free.

What unites all four: they manufacture the *feeling* and the *social signal* of competence while the underlying model is missing. They're the map redrawn, not the ground cleared.

## The honesty test: would you bet on it?

There's one check that cuts through every disguise at once: **would you bet on it?** Put a probability or a wager on the line and faux competence evaporates instantly. It's easy to *sound* knowledgeable; it's impossible to fake a falsifiable forecast that reality is about to grade.

Watch how it dissolves the four disguises. The jargon artist who "fundamentally understands the liquidity mismatch" suddenly goes quiet when you ask, "So — 70% or 30% that this fund freezes withdrawals within a year? Want to put money on it?" The authority-borrower can't price the bet, because they were renting a conclusion, not holding a model. A real opinion is a *number you'd stake something on*. A fake one is a vibe that won't commit. This is **calibration** doing its job as a lie detector: it forces the fuzzy feeling of "I kind of know this" to resolve into a specific, checkable claim — and the fuzz can't survive the resolution.

## Competence doesn't transfer for free

Here's the hard truth that the halo effect bets against: **competence does not cross the boundary on its own.** Being world-class at X gives you two real things — sharper *study skills* (you know how to learn a field, how to spot a flimsy argument, how to keep score) and maybe an *adjacent foothold*. What it does **not** hand you is a working model of Y. And **adjacent is not inside** — that's lesson 2's whole danger, restated: the band right next to your circle is the most dangerous place on the map, because it feels closest to home.

So what *is* the right use of an adjacent strength? Not a license to act as if you're already inside — a **foothold to widen *from*.** Your engineering fluency is a fine place to *start* learning hardware manufacturing; it is not a substitute for having learned it. You begin from the foothold, run the recipe (study, predict, get graded), and let feedback tell you when you've actually arrived. The foothold shortens the climb; it doesn't skip it.

This is also where **comparative advantage** lives: your real edge isn't "everything near things I'm good at" — it's the narrower region where your competence genuinely *transfers and gets verified*. Find the spots where your existing model actually does predictive work in the new domain, confirm it with feedback, and build out from there. That verified-transfer zone is where you have an honest advantage; the rest of the "nearby" territory just *looks* like it.

<Reveal client:visible label="“I'm a fast learner, so I can opine on this now.”" buttonLabel="Show why">
Learning fast is real and valuable — but notice what it actually is: a skill for **closing the gap** between not-knowing and knowing *over time*. It is not a teleporter that drops you on the far side of the gap before you've crossed it. "I learn quickly" predicts that, with study and feedback, you'll get there *sooner than most* — it says nothing about where you are *right now*. Speed of learning and current competence are different quantities, and the fast-learner trap is quietly swapping one for the other: using your future, earned competence as a license to act inside your circle today, before you've put in a single rep. The fast learner's edge is that the climb is shorter. It is still a climb.
</Reveal>

## Recap

- **The circle can grow, but only slowly.** Genuine widening = deliberate study + real feedback + scorekeeping + time. You clear ground stump by stump; you don't redraw the map and call it cleared.
- **Faking range is the counterfeit.** Name-dropping, borrowing an authority's conviction, surface pattern-matching, and especially the **halo effect** all manufacture the *feeling* of competence with no model underneath.
- **Would you bet on it?** A real opinion resolves into a probability you'd stake something on. Faux competence can't make a falsifiable forecast — calibration is the lie detector.
- **Competence doesn't transfer for free.** Mastery of X gives you study skills and maybe a foothold, never a working model of Y. Adjacent ≠ inside; use the foothold to *widen from*, verified by feedback. Learning fast shortens the climb — it doesn't skip it.

## Where this goes next

You can now do three things with your circle: locate its edge, grow it honestly, and tell genuine widening from the convincing fake. That's most of the theory. But theory has a cruel weakness — **it all happens in your own calm head**, and decisions don't.

The next lesson, **"Staying Inside Under Pressure,"** is about the hard part: holding the line when the world is shoving. Knowing and growing your circle is useless if social pressure, FOMO, or a hot tip shoves you across the edge before you've earned the new ground — exactly what happened to everyone who *didn't* sit out the dot-com boom. We'll get into the discipline that protects you there: Munger's **"too hard" pile**, and the quiet power of saying "I don't know" out loud.
