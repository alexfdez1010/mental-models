In 1996, in a letter to Berkshire Hathaway shareholders, Warren Buffett dropped a
sentence that has launched a thousand business-school slides: *"The size of that
circle is not very important; knowing its boundaries, however, is vital."* He was
describing what he called a **circle of competence** — the area where you know
enough to actually trust your own judgment. And notice the move he just made. He
didn't brag about how *big* his circle was. He bragged about knowing where it
*ends*.

Buffett liked to quote Tom Watson Sr., the founder of IBM, on exactly this point:
*"I'm no genius. I'm smart in spots, and I stay around those spots."* That second
sentence is the whole game. Plenty of people are smart in spots. The rare,
valuable, slightly boring skill is *staying around them* — knowing where the spot
ends and the fog begins, and not wandering out there with the same confident
stride you used inside.

That's what this course is about. Not getting smarter (though you might). Mostly:
getting honest about the edges of what you already know.

## The one sentence to take away

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
**A circle of competence is the area where your mental maps are reliable enough
to trust your judgment — and what matters is not how big the circle is, but
knowing exactly where its edge runs.**
</Callout>

Here's the counter-intuitive part, and the reason this is a real model and not a
motivational poster: a *big fuzzy* circle is more dangerous than a *small sharp*
one. A small circle with a crisp edge tells you precisely when to stop trusting
yourself. A sprawling circle with a blurry edge lets your confidence keep talking
long after your competence has gone quiet — and that gap is where the expensive
mistakes live. This course will argue that the most valuable thing you own isn't
the inside of the circle at all. It's the line.

## Why a feeling isn't a boundary

If your circle's edge were clearly marked — a bright fence with a sign reading YOU
KNOW NOTHING PAST HERE — none of this would need to be a model. You'd just stop at
the fence. The problem is that the edge is invisible from the inside, because
competence *feels the same* just inside it and just outside it. A subject you've
half-absorbed from headlines and dinner-party chatter feels familiar. Familiar
feels like understanding. And so you stroll across the boundary without the
slightest sense that the ground has changed.

Psychologists have a name for this: the *illusion of explanatory depth*. In a 2002
Yale study, Rozenblit and Keil asked people how well they understood everyday
objects — zippers, flush toilets, bicycles. People rated their understanding high.
Then they were asked to actually explain the mechanism step by step, and they
crumbled. The classic party trick is to ask someone to draw a bicycle from memory:
nearly everyone produces a frame that couldn't possibly turn or hold together. You
*recognize* a bicycle perfectly. You'd swear you understand it. You can't draw it.
Recognition is not knowledge — and your brain charges you the same warm feeling of
"yeah, I've got this" for both.

That's why the danger isn't out in the far unknown. It's in the narrow band *just
past your edge*, where things still look familiar.

## The map of the course

Five lessons, then a final exam. Here's the route:

1. **The Edge, Not the Area** — what a circle of competence actually is, where
   Buffett got it, and why the boundary matters more than the size. The crucial
   split between *"I understand this"* and *"I'm familiar with this."*
2. **The Danger Zone** — why the most dangerous spot on the map is the band just
   past your edge, not the far outside. Overconfidence, the illusion of
   explanatory depth, and how this connects to calibration. (Has an interactive
   island to play with.)
3. **Mapping Your Circle** — practical methods for finding your *real* boundary:
   keeping score of your forecasts, asking "what would I need to know?", the
   teach-it-to-someone (Feynman) test, and separating familiarity from genuine
   understanding — while dodging outcome bias.
4. **Widening Without Faking** — how to grow the circle for real (study, feedback,
   reps) versus faking range (name-dropping, borrowed conviction, the halo
   effect). Why competence doesn't automatically carry across the boundary.
5. **Staying Inside Under Pressure** — the discipline to say "I don't know" and
   use Munger's *"too hard"* pile, even with FOMO, authority, and social proof all
   shoving you outward. It is completely fine to pass.

Then the **Final Exam**: a graded run that locks in each answer as you go — no
going back, no retries — and you need 70% to pass. It's one-way on purpose,
because the entire model is about committing honestly and living with where your
boundary actually is.

### How to use this course

You'll have noticed the questions above came *before* the explanations. That's
deliberate. **Guess before you peek.** Commit to a real answer first, then reveal
the reasoning. When your guess is wrong, that little sting is the most useful thing
on the page — it's the exact sensation of bumping into the edge of your own
circle, which is the one skill we're here to build. Don't rob yourself of it by
scrolling ahead.

Ready? Let's start where every good map starts — at the edge.
