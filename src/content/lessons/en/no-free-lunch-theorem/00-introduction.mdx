Open any bookshop, feed, or group chat and you'll find the same promise on
repeat: *this* is the best one. The best diet. The best morning routine. The best
way to learn a language. The best trading strategy. Each claims to win — and each
claim quietly assumes there's a single champion out there, waiting to be crowned.

The **No Free Lunch theorem** says the crown is empty. There is no method that
beats all the others across every problem. This course is about why that's true,
why it's *good* news rather than bad, and how it quietly rewires the questions you
ask.

## What you'll actually walk away with

By the end you'll be able to say — precisely — what "no free lunch" means, why it
follows almost inevitably once you think about *all possible problems*, and (most
importantly) the one thing people get wrong about it. Here's the map:

1. **No shortcut works everywhere** — the claim in plain words, and the free-lunch
   metaphor it's named after.
2. **Why it's true** — a tiny worked example you can count on your fingers, where
   two strategies genuinely tie once you average over every case.
3. **It does *not* mean all methods are equal** — the crucial misreading. Real
   problems have *structure*, and matching it is the whole game.
4. **The many faces** — the same idea in diets, investing, management, and study;
   why it's a cousin of *circle of competence* and the *latticework*.
5. **The lunch you *can* get** — what No Free Lunch permits, and why carrying many
   tools beats hunting for one magic one.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
No single method is best at everything; performance comes from matching a method
to the structure of the specific problem, not from finding a universal winner.
</Callout>

## A quick taste of the idea

Imagine two friends arguing about the best way to look for lost keys. One always
checks the kitchen first; the other always checks the bedroom first. Who's right?

It depends *entirely* on where the keys actually are. Over a lifetime of losing
keys in all sorts of places, neither habit finds them faster on average — every
head start in one room is paid back by a slow start in another. Neither friend has
a *free* lunch. What they *can* have is a **paid** lunch: if you know you always
drop your keys by the door, searching the door first genuinely wins. The knowledge
is what you pay with.

<Reveal client:visible>
<Callout variant="info" title="Where we're headed">
That little keys example is the whole theorem in miniature. "Averaged over
everywhere the keys could be, all search habits tie" is No Free Lunch. "But I know
where I usually drop them, so I search there first" is the paid lunch that makes
you fast in the real world. Keep both halves in mind.
</Callout>
</Reveal>

## How to use this course

Every lesson opens with a quick guess, explains the idea with a concrete example,
and checks that it stuck. Don't skip the guesses — trying to answer *before* you
know is one of the most reliable ways to actually remember. There's no maths to
fear here: the one worked example uses numbers you can add up by hand, and an
interactive board does the counting for you.

When you've finished the five teaching lessons, a graded final exam pulls it all
together. It's one-way — once you submit an answer it's locked — so treat the
practice questions along the way as exactly that: practice.

<Callout variant="warning" title="One thing to unlearn as you go">
You've probably spent years looking for the single best tool. This course won't
give you one — it'll give you something better: a reliable way to tell *which*
tool fits *which* job. That's a skill that never goes out of date.
</Callout>
