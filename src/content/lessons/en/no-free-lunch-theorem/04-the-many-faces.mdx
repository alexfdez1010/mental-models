## "It depends" — with a theorem behind it

When an expert answers "what's the best X?" with an infuriating *"it depends,"*
they're not dodging. They're stating No Free Lunch in plain English. "It depends"
means: *the winner changes with the structure of your specific situation, so
there's no context-free answer.* The follow-up questions — depends on *what?* — are
where the real expertise lives. Let's see the pattern wear four costumes.

### Diet

There is no single optimal diet for every human. A diet that controls one person's
blood sugar may leave another sluggish; an athlete in training and a desk worker
recovering from illness have different "problem structures" (activity, genetics,
goals, what they'll actually stick to). A diet is a *method*; a body-plus-goal is a
*problem*. The winner depends on the match. Anyone selling *the* diet for everyone
is selling a free lunch.

### Investing

No strategy beats the market in every regime. Value investing shines in some
decades and lags for years in others; momentum works until it violently doesn't;
holding cash is genius in a crash and a slow bleed in a boom. Each strategy has a
**prior** about how markets will behave, and gets paid when reality matches it.
"What's the best strategy?" has no answer without "in what kind of market, over
what horizon, for whose risk tolerance?"

### Management

There's no leadership style that's best for every team and moment. Tight,
directive management can rescue a team in crisis and suffocate a team of experts;
hands-off autonomy frees great people and abandons struggling ones. The style is a
method; the team-in-its-situation is the problem. Great managers don't have *a*
style — they read the situation and match.

### Study technique

No study method is universally best. Flashcards crush vocabulary and are hopeless
for essay structure; re-reading feels productive but fails for problem-solving,
where practice testing wins. The best technique depends on the *kind* of knowledge
you're after — another problem-structure in disguise.

<Callout variant="info" title="The tell in every case">
Whenever a claim reaches for "the best ___ for everyone," a free lunch is being
smuggled in. The honest version always names a condition: best *for this body,
this market, this team, this kind of material.* If there's no "for," be suspicious.
</Callout>

## How it locks into the latticework

No Free Lunch doesn't sit alone — it's the theorem underneath two foundational
models you already know.

**Circle of competence.** Your circle is the set of problems whose structure you
genuinely understand — where you can tell which method fits. No Free Lunch explains
*why* the circle matters: since no method is universally right, the crucial skill
is knowing *which problems you can correctly match a method to.* Straying outside
your circle isn't dangerous because the problems are hard; it's dangerous because
you can no longer tell whether your favourite tool's prior fits.

**The latticework of models.** Why carry *many* mental models instead of one
favourite? Because No Free Lunch guarantees your favourite has problems it's bad
at. A single model is a single prior; it wins on the problems that match it and
misleads you on the rest. A latticework is a *toolkit of priors* — so that
whatever structure a new problem has, you've got a model whose assumptions fit.
No Free Lunch is precisely why the man with one hammer gets in trouble.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The through-line">
One method = one prior = good on some problems, bad on others (No Free Lunch). So:
know which problems you can judge (**circle of competence**) and carry many tools
for the ones you can (**the latticework**). Three models, one spine.
</Callout>

## A worked case: the same person, two problems

Watch the matching happen. Sam is deciding how to tackle two very different tasks
this week.

- **Problem A: memorise 200 medical terms for an exam.** The structure is
  *arbitrary paired associations, tested by recall.* The method that matches:
  spaced flashcards. Re-reading the textbook would feel productive and fail.
- **Problem B: figure out why a patient's symptoms don't add up.** The structure is
  *diagnosis under uncertainty, needing many disciplines.* The method that matches:
  a latticework — run it past several models (base rates, physiology, incentives to
  malinger, measurement error). Flashcards are useless here.

Same person, same week, and the *best method flips completely* because the problem
structure flipped. That flip is No Free Lunch in one life.
