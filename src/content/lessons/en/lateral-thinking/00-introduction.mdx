There's an old riddle. A truck gets wedged under a low bridge — jammed tight,
can't go forward, can't back out. Engineers gather, argue about jacks, cranes,
cutting the bridge, cutting the truck. A kid watching from the pavement asks the
obvious question nobody in a suit thought to ask: *why don't you let some air out
of the tyres?* The truck drops a few centimetres and rolls free.

Every adult there was thinking *harder* about the same thing — how to move the
bridge or the truck. The kid moved *sideways*: don't change the bridge, change the
truck's height. That sidestep is the entire subject of this course, and it has a
name.

## The one-breath version

**Lateral thinking** means solving a problem by *changing where you attack it
from* — reframing the question, dropping a hidden assumption, or deliberately
jumping to a fresh starting point — instead of grinding forward from the obvious
one. The term was coined in 1967 by **Edward de Bono**, a Maltese psychologist who
spent his career arguing that creativity isn't a mystical gift you're born with;
it's a *technique* you can practise on purpose.

Here's the distinction that runs under everything. **Vertical thinking** is the
normal, logical kind: you take one line of reasoning and dig it deeper, each step
carefully justified by the last. It's how you do long division or debug code, and
it's magnificent — *when you're digging in the right place.* **Lateral thinking**
is what you reach for when you're not: instead of digging the same hole deeper,
you go and start a hole somewhere else entirely.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
Vertical thinking digs the *same hole deeper*. Lateral thinking digs *a new hole
somewhere else*. When more effort in the obvious direction stops paying off,
that's the signal to move sideways instead of pushing harder.
</Callout>

## Why your brain fights this

If lateral thinking is so useful, why doesn't it come naturally? Because of how
the mind is built. De Bono's key insight was that the brain is a
**self-organising pattern system**: it takes incoming information and files it
into grooves, and once a groove exists, new information rushes down it
automatically. That's a feature — it's why you can read this sentence without
sounding out every letter, why a doctor recognises a disease at a glance. Patterns
are mental shortcuts, and they're mostly a gift.

But there's a catch. Once your mind has a well-worn groove for a problem, the
"obvious" solution feels *inevitable* and every other path feels invisible — not
wrong, just literally unseen. You're not choosing to ignore the sideways answer;
your brain routed straight past it before you were even conscious of a choice.
That's why the truck-and-bridge answer feels so obvious *after* someone says it
and was so invisible *before*. Lateral thinking is the deliberate practice of
climbing out of the groove.

<Callout variant="info" title="A pattern system, in one image">
Imagine rain falling on a landscape. The first drops wander, but they carve tiny
channels — and every later drop follows those channels, deepening them, until all
the water runs down a few big rivers. Your mind does this with ideas: early
patterns become the rivers that all later thinking flows down. Useful, fast — and
exactly why the fresh, sideways idea can't get a look-in without a deliberate
nudge.
</Callout>

## Why this is a mental model, not a slogan

You've heard "think outside the box" a thousand times, and it's nearly useless —
it tells you *that* you should be more creative without telling you *how*. Lateral
thinking earns its place as a real **mental model** — a compact, reusable way of
seeing you can carry between domains — because it comes with actual *tools*: ways
to find the assumption you're trapped in, jolt yourself onto a new track, and
generate options on demand. "Be creative" is a wish. Lateral thinking is a set of
moves you can run on purpose, even on a grey Tuesday when inspiration is nowhere.

It's also a tool with honest limits, which we'll be strict about. Lateral thinking
*generates* new starting points; it doesn't *test* them. Moving sideways to a
clever idea is worthless if the idea doesn't actually work — and checking whether
it works is a job for patient vertical thinking. The two aren't rivals; they're a
team. Anyone who only ever "thinks outside the box" and never rigorously builds
anything is just being weird, not useful. Knowing when to move sideways *and* when
to dig straight down is the real skill.

## The map of the course

Five short teaching lessons, then one exam you can't undo. The route:

1. **Vertical vs. Lateral** — the core distinction made precise: the pattern-system
   brain, why obvious solutions feel inevitable, and how the two modes divide the
   labour of thinking.
2. **Reframing & Hidden Assumptions** — the heart of the skill: finding the
   unspoken "of course…" that's holding a problem shut, plus the famous puzzles
   (nine dots, the candle problem, functional fixedness) that prove an unquestioned
   assumption — not a lack of effort — is usually the wall.
3. **Provocation & Random Entry** — de Bono's tools rendered plainly: *PO* and
   provocation (say something deliberately "wrong" to jump the groove), random
   entry (feed in an unrelated word to force a new association), and *movement*
   versus judgment.
4. **Diverge, Then Converge** — when to generate wide with judgment switched off
   and when to switch it back on to evaluate — and why mixing the two kills good
   ideas in the cradle. Plus practical drills you can run today.
5. **Where Lateral Thinking Misleads** — the limits: it complements rigour rather
   than replacing it, "outside the box" as an empty slogan, contrarian ≠ useful,
   and the survivorship bias hiding in every famous creativity anecdote.

Then a **Final Exam** — graded, one question at a time, one-way: once you answer,
it locks. No back button, no retries.

### How to use this course

One rule does most of the work: **try the puzzle before you read the answer.**
This subject is full of little problems you can actually attempt, and the whole
point is the moment you feel stuck — because *feeling* the groove hold you shut is
what teaches you to climb out of it. A smooth, nodding read-through teaches almost
nothing. The puzzles *are* the lesson; the prose just sets them up.
