## What confirmation bias is

Picture a defense attorney handed a client and a verdict on day one: *not guilty.* From that moment, the attorney's job is not to discover the truth — it's to **build the strongest possible case for the conclusion already chosen.** Every witness gets interviewed with one question in mind ("does this help my client?"), every ambiguous detail gets read in the client's favor, and the inconvenient facts get quietly left out of the closing argument. The attorney is skilled, sincere, and completely one-sided — and from the inside, it all feels like simply *following the evidence.* Now here's the unsettling part: that attorney isn't a person you hire. It's the default operating mode of your own mind, running silently, on every belief you hold, without ever asking your permission.

That habit has a precise name. **Confirmation bias** is the tendency to *seek out, favor, interpret, and remember* information in ways that support what you already believe — and to ignore, discount, or forget information that contradicts it. The term was coined by the English psychologist **Peter Wason** in the 1960s (you'll run his famous experiment yourself in the next lesson), but the phenomenon was diagnosed four centuries earlier by **Francis Bacon**, who wrote that *"the human understanding when it has once adopted an opinion draws all things else to support and agree with it."* That phrase — *draws all things else to support and agree with it* — is the whole model in eight words. The mind doesn't weigh evidence neutrally and then form a belief. It forms a belief and then bends the evidence-gathering machinery to feed it.

<Callout variant="info" title="Two names, one idea">
**Bacon** (around 1620) described it; **Wason** (1960s) named it "confirmation bias" and demonstrated it in the lab. Bacon's line — *"draws all things else to support and agree with it"* — and Wason's experiments are the same model, four centuries apart.
</Callout>

The single most important word in that definition is one you might skim past: it operates **unconsciously.** This is not a decision you make. You do not sit down and resolve, "I shall now ignore disconfirming evidence." The bias works *upstream* of awareness — it shapes what you even notice, which sources you click, how a neutral sentence lands on your ear. It is not a flaw in your conclusions so much as a tilt in your *perception*, and a tilt in perception is invisible from the inside, because perception is the only window you have. You can't perceive that your window is tinted; the tint just looks like the color of the world.

**Worked example.** You've decided a coworker, Daniel, is unreliable. Watch what your mind quietly does over the following weeks. He misses a Tuesday deadline — *typical Daniel*, you note, and the memory files itself cleanly. He delivers a flawless report on Thursday — but you don't really register it, or you think *the bar was low* or *someone must have helped him.* A month later, asked for your honest read on Daniel, you sincerely report: "unreliable — and I've watched closely." And you *have* watched closely. That's exactly the trouble. You watched closely through a filter that admitted the confirming events at full volume and muted the disconfirming ones. You didn't lie and you didn't refuse to look. You looked, carefully, at a curated subset — and mistook the curation for the truth.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The trap in one line">
Confirmation *feels* like evidence. Every fact that fits lands with a little click of *see, I was right* — and a hundred clicks feels like proof. But a belief that has only ever collected supporting facts has been **tested** by none of them.
</Callout>

### When to use it

Reach for this model the moment you notice a belief of yours feeling *obviously, comfortably correct* — especially if every recent piece of information has happened to confirm it. The smoothness is the warning sign. A belief that the world keeps agreeing with might be true, or it might be one your mind has been quietly defending. The question that turns the lawyer back into a scientist: *"What would prove me wrong here — and have I actually gone looking for it?"*

## The three flavors

Confirmation bias is not one move; it's a tag team of three, and they hit you at three different stages of dealing with evidence — *before* you gather it, *while* you read it, and *after*, when you recall it. Naming all three is what lets you catch the bias at whichever stage it's currently operating. (Lessons 3 and 4 take each apart in depth; here we just install the three labels.)

**Biased search** — *the evidence you go looking for.* You preferentially seek information likely to confirm your belief and skip the tests that might break it. This is the purest form, and it's so basic that people fail it on a puzzle with no emotions attached at all: in **the next lesson** you'll run Peter Wason's `2, 4, 6` task and watch yourself hunt for *yes* — proposing only triples you expect to pass — when the only informative move is to propose one you expect to *fail*. One-line definition: *seeking confirming evidence and avoiding the disconfirming test.* Example: you suspect a diet works, so you read the testimonials page and three glowing blog posts — and never once search "does [diet] actually work" or look for the people it failed.

**Biased interpretation** — *how you read what you find.* Hand two people the same ambiguous or mixed evidence and each reads it as supporting their prior view. The data didn't change; the lens did. One-line definition: *reading neutral or mixed evidence as support for what you already think.* Example: a study finds your favored policy has "modest, mixed effects." A supporter reads that as *it works!*; an opponent reads the identical sentence as *it barely works.* Same words, opposite takeaways.

**Biased memory** — *what you later recall.* Even when you took in balanced evidence, your memory selectively serves back the confirming pieces and lets the rest fade. One-line definition: *preferentially remembering the evidence that fits.* Example: you "remember" your team winning most of the close games this season, because the wins are vivid and the close losses quietly slipped away — so your sense of "we're clutch" rests on a doctored highlight reel your own memory edited.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Before, during, after">
The three flavors map onto a timeline of any evidence: **search** is *before* (what you go get), **interpretation** is *during* (how you read it), **memory** is *after* (what survives in your head). The bias can grab you at any of the three — which is exactly why naming all three matters.
</Callout>

## Why it's a real model, not just "being stubborn"

It's tempting to file confirmation bias under "people are stubborn" and move on. That would be a mistake, because it lumps together three very different things — *stubbornness*, *lying*, and *confirmation bias* — that need to be told apart if you want to actually defend against the last one. Stubbornness is conscious refusal: you know the counter-evidence and dig in anyway. Lying is conscious deception: you know the truth and say otherwise. Both involve a person who *can see the other side* and chooses against it. Confirmation bias is the strange and more dangerous case where there is **nothing to refuse and nothing to conceal** — because the other side never fully reached awareness in the first place. The honest, open-minded reasoner has it too, and feels perfectly fair the whole time.

So what actually distinguishes confirmation bias as a genuine cognitive model? Four properties, none of which "stubbornness" captures:

- **It's automatic.** No decision is made; it runs on its own, below awareness. You can't introspect your way to catching it in the act, because the editing happens before the footage reaches you.
- **It's sincere.** The biased person isn't pretending. Their stated belief and their actual belief are the *same*. An honesty test passes, because nothing is being hidden.
- **It afflicts honest, capable reasoners** — often *hardest*. As the intro showed, a sharper mind is a better lawyer: it builds more sophisticated cases for the conclusion it already wanted (psychologists call this **motivated reasoning**). Intelligence is horsepower; the bias just points it at defending the preferred answer.
- **It self-seals.** This is the engine that makes it so durable: **belief steers perception steers belief.** Your belief shapes what evidence you notice and how you read it; that curated evidence then "confirms" the belief; the now-stronger belief curates even harder. Round and round, each loop tightening, every lap feeling like fresh confirmation.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The self-sealing loop">
Belief → steers what you **search for and notice** → which mostly **confirms** the belief → which makes the belief **stronger** → which steers your perception even harder. A belief inside this loop can grow unshakeable while collecting *zero* genuine tests. That's why it's a model, not a mood: it has a mechanism.
</Callout>

This also connects directly to the model just before it on this path — **incentives**. In that course you met *incentive-caused bias*: a reward quietly bends what a person sincerely believes. Confirmation bias is the cognitive machinery *underneath* that. Put plainly: **incentives supply the motive; confirmation bias supplies the method.** The paycheck (or the identity, or the side you picked) gives your mind a conclusion to prefer; confirmation bias is the standing tendency that then gathers and weighs evidence in that conclusion's favor. You can also get the method with no paycheck at all — sometimes the only "incentive" is that it was simply the belief you happened to form first, and the mind defends it just the same.

## When to reach for it

A model is only useful if you know its trigger conditions — the situations where you should consciously pull it off the shelf and aim it at your own thinking. Confirmation bias has two reliable triggers, and learning to feel them is most of the skill.

**Trigger one: whenever you feel certain.** Certainty is the smoke alarm. The feeling of *obviously, I'm right* is not produced only by being right — it's also exactly what the self-sealing loop produces after it has muted all the disconfirming evidence. So the feeling can't distinguish the two cases, which means a strong sense of certainty is precisely the moment to ask whether you've actually run a test that could have come back *no*. The more sure you feel, the more overdue the disconfirming search.

**Trigger two: whenever a belief is tied to your identity, your side, or your prior investment.** The bias gets dramatically stronger when being wrong would cost you something beyond the fact itself — your self-image, your group's position, the time and money you've already sunk. A belief you hold loosely ("this restaurant is probably fine") barely activates the lawyer. A belief that *defines* you ("people like me are right about this") puts the lawyer on retainer full-time. So the second you notice a belief is *yours* in that deeper, identity-shaped way, treat every confirming fact that arrives with extra suspicion.

<Callout variant="tip" title="When to use it — the two triggers">
Pull this model off the shelf and aim it at yourself whenever **(1) you feel certain**, or **(2) the belief is tied to your identity, your side, or something you've already invested in.** Both are conditions under which your mind has the strongest motive to play lawyer — so both are exactly when to ask: *"What would change my mind, and have I looked?"*
</Callout>

And now the pitfall — the one almost everyone walks into the instant they learn this model.

<Reveal client:visible label="You've just learned to spot confirmation bias. Here's the trap waiting on the other side of learning it." buttonLabel="Show the pitfall">
The moment people grasp confirmation bias, they start spotting it *everywhere* — in the relative who won't read past the headline, the colleague who only cites the studies that flatter his view, the whole opposing political tribe. What they almost never spot is the one instance that matters: **their own.** This is the **bias blind spot** — the well-documented tendency to recognize bias in others while feeling personally immune, because your own biased beliefs arrive labeled *obviously true*, not *conveniently true.* Confirmation bias is not a flaw that other, weaker minds have. It is the standard equipment of *every* mind, including the sharp, fair, well-read one currently reading this sentence and quietly thinking "yes, but I'm pretty even-handed." That thought is the bias talking. We give the blind spot a full lesson later (lesson 5, the antidote), because turning all of this hardest on *yourself* is the entire point — a defense aimed only at other people is no defense at all.
</Reveal>
