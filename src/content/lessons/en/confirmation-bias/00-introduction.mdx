Imagine two minds approaching the same question — *does this medicine work?* The
first is a **scientist**: it lists what it believes, then deliberately goes hunting
for the result that would prove the belief *false*, because that's the only test
worth running. The second is a **defense attorney**: it has already decided the
verdict and now combs the world for anything that supports the case, while quietly
filing away whatever doesn't. Here's the uncomfortable news this course is built
around: left to its own devices, your mind is almost always the second one. It is a
brilliant, tireless lawyer for whatever you already believe — and it never tells
you it's doing it.

That habit has a name: **confirmation bias** — the tendency to seek out, favor, and
remember information that confirms your existing beliefs, and to ignore, discount,
or forget information that contradicts them. It is probably the single most
documented bias in all of psychology, and the most consequential, because it
quietly corrupts every *other* kind of thinking. You can know statistics cold and
still only run the analyses that flatter your hunch. You can be scrupulously honest
and still never notice the evidence you didn't look for. The bias doesn't make you
dumb or dishonest. It makes you a one-sided researcher who feels like a fair one.

## The one sentence to take away

Before five lessons of detail, here is the whole model in a line:

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
**The mind defends its beliefs like a lawyer, not a scientist: it looks for what confirms and overlooks what refutes — so the test that matters is the one you'd rather not run.** If you remember nothing else, the question *"What would prove me wrong, and have I actually looked for it?"* will do more work than any amount of raw intelligence.
</Callout>

The trap is that confirmation *feels* like evidence. Every fact that fits your view
lands with a little click of *see, I was right* — and a hundred of those clicks
feels like proof. But a belief that only ever collects supporting facts has been
**tested** by exactly none of them. A theory you can't imagine being wrong isn't
strong; it's unfalsifiable, which is a different and much worse thing. The skill
this course builds is learning to feel suspicious of that comfortable click, and to
go looking, on purpose, for the fact that would wipe the smile off your face.

## Why a smarter mind can fall harder

The most counter-intuitive fact about confirmation bias is that intelligence is not
a defense — and can be a liability. We'd love to believe that smart, well-informed
people are inoculated. The opposite tends to hold. A sharper mind is a better
*lawyer*: give it a conclusion it wants and it will generate more sophisticated
arguments for that conclusion, find subtler flaws in the opposing evidence, and
construct a more airtight rationalization — all while feeling like rigorous
analysis. Researchers have a name for this: **motivated reasoning**, the use of our
reasoning power not to find the truth but to defend a preferred answer. The clever
person doesn't escape the bias. They build it a cathedral.

This is also why confirmation bias connects to the model just before it on this
path, **incentives**. There you saw that a reward can quietly bend what a person
sincerely *believes* — incentive-caused bias. Confirmation bias is the cognitive
engine underneath that: the mind's standing tendency to gather and weigh evidence
in favor of the conclusion it's already leaning toward, whether the lean came from
a paycheck, a prior commitment, an identity, or just the belief you happened to
form first. Incentives supply the motive; confirmation bias supplies the method.

## The map of the course

Four short teaching lessons, then one exam you can't undo. The route:

1. **What Confirmation Bias Is** — the precise definition, why it's a genuine model
   and not just "people are stubborn," and the **three places** it operates: biased
   *search* (what evidence you look for), biased *interpretation* (how you read what
   you find), and biased *memory* (what you later recall).
2. **The Wason Task: Hunting for Yes** — biased search, up close. You'll run Wason's
   famous `2, 4, 6` experiment yourself and watch how seeking *confirmation* (a test
   you expect to pass) teaches you nothing, while seeking *disconfirmation* (a test
   you expect to fail) is the only move that finds the truth. Falsification, made
   physical.
3. **Same Evidence, Opposite Conclusions** — biased interpretation and memory at
   scale: how two camps read the *identical* study and both walk away more sure of
   themselves, what motivated reasoning and **myside bias** are, why echo chambers
   and algorithmic feeds supercharge all of it, and the surprising reason the bias
   was useful enough to evolve.
4. **Arguing Against Yourself** — the antidote. A small kit of deliberate moves:
   **consider the opposite**, **steelman** the other side, actively **seek the
   disconfirming case**, **pre-register what would change your mind**, and build
   dissent into your environment. Plus the final trap — the **bias blind spot**,
   the reason you must turn all of this hardest on yourself.

Then a **Final Exam** — graded, one question at a time, one-way: once you answer, it
locks. No back button, no retries. You'll be ready.

### How to use this course

One rule does most of the work: **guess before you peek.** When you hit an exercise,
commit to an answer before revealing anything. The small sting of being wrong is
what makes the idea stick — and given the subject, notice the irony: a smooth,
nodding read-through is *itself* a tiny act of confirmation bias. The exercises are
where the learning happens; the prose just sets them up.
