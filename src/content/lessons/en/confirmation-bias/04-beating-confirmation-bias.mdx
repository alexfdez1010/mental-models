By now the diagnosis is grim. Your mind defends its beliefs like a lawyer, the
bias hides in your *search*, your *interpretation*, and your *memory*, intelligence
makes it worse rather than better, and the whole machine runs so smoothly that
biased conclusions arrive feeling exactly like the truth. So here is the
reasonable next question: if confirmation bias is automatic, universal, and
invisible from the inside, what on earth are we supposed to *do* about it?

The honest answer is the one nobody wants to hear: you cannot simply *decide* to
stop. "I'll just be more objective from now on" is the cognitive equivalent of a
New Year's resolution — sincere, and useless by February. Willpower loses to a
reflex every time. But there is good news, and it's the entire point of this
lesson. Confirmation bias is beaten not by *trying harder* but by *running
different procedures* — small, concrete, repeatable moves that don't require you
to feel objective, only to follow the steps. A pilot doesn't avoid crashes by
being calm; they avoid them by running the checklist. This lesson hands you the
checklist. Five tools, then the trap that makes you turn all five on the person
you trust most: yourself.

## Consider the opposite — the single best move

Of every debiasing technique psychologists have tested, one stands out for being
both dead simple and genuinely effective. It's called **considering the
opposite**, and it does exactly what it says.

In 1984, the psychologists Charles Lord, Mark Lepper, and Elizabeth Preston ran a
now-famous follow-up to the "same study, opposite conclusions" experiments you met
last lesson. They again gave people studies on a charged topic and again watched
**biased assimilation** kick in — each side reading the ambiguous evidence as
support for what they already believed. But this time they added a group with one
extra instruction. Before evaluating the evidence, those people were asked a
single question: *"Would you have made the same high or low evaluations of this
study had it produced results on the other side of the issue?"* In short — *would
you accept this evidence if it pointed the other way?* That one prompt, asking
people to imagine the opposite finding, sharply **reduced biased assimilation.**
The lawyer, forced to argue the other side's case for a moment, started behaving a
little more like a scientist.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The whole tool in one question">
**Consider the opposite:** before you accept a piece of evidence, ask *"If this exact same evidence pointed the other way, would I find it just as convincing?"* If the answer is no — if you'd be poking holes in it the instant it threatened your view — then you aren't evaluating the evidence. You're auditioning it.
</Callout>

Why does such a tiny move work when "try to be objective" doesn't? Because vague
good intentions give the bias nothing to push against, but considering the opposite
forces a *specific* counter-thought into existence. Your mind's default is to
generate reasons you're right; the technique simply makes you also generate the
reasons you might be wrong — reasons that were always available, you just never
went looking. It's **inversion**, the model that says to attack a problem
backwards: instead of asking "why is my belief true?", ask "how would I know if it
were false?" You don't have to *want* to be wrong. You just have to run the
question.

**Worked example.** You're a manager convinced a candidate is perfect for a role —
sharp in the interview, great résumé, you can already picture them on the team.
The lawyer in you is now quietly assembling the hire-them case. Run the tool: *if
this exact résumé and this exact interview belonged to someone I'd already decided
to reject, what would I notice?* Suddenly the "sharp answers" look a bit rehearsed,
the "great résumé" has a two-year gap you'd glossed over, and the confident manner
reads as someone who might not take feedback. You haven't decided to reject them —
you've just forced the disconfirming evidence into the room, where it can be
weighed instead of skipped. The decision is now made on two-sided evidence rather
than a one-sided brief.

### When to reach for it

Pull out *consider the opposite* whenever you notice the comfortable click of
*see, I was right* — that little hit of confirmation is the cue. Use it before any
decision where being wrong is expensive: a hire, a diagnosis, a big purchase, a
strong public claim. It's the highest-leverage tool in the kit because it costs ten
seconds and needs no one else's cooperation. The rest of this lesson is, in a
sense, four sturdier ways of doing the same fundamental thing: dragging the other
side of the argument into the light.

## Steelman, don't strawman

The crudest way to "consider" an opposing view is to consider a *stupid* version
of it. We do this constantly, and it has a name.

A **strawman** is a deliberately weak, distorted caricature of the other side's
argument — built precisely *because* it's easy to knock down. You're not allowed to
beat the actual position, so you stuff a scarecrow with the dumbest possible
version of it and triumphantly set it on fire. *"People who disagree with me just
want chaos."* *"The other side literally thinks money grows on trees."* It feels
like winning. It's confirmation bias wearing a victory lap: you've "engaged with
the opposition" while never letting a real opposing idea anywhere near your belief.

Its opposite is the **steelman**: you construct the *strongest possible* version of
the argument you disagree with — the version its smartest defender would actually
endorse — and you defeat *that*, or admit you can't. The bar is specific and
brutal: would an intelligent person who holds the view nod along and say "yes,
that's exactly my point"? If they'd wince, you've built a strawman with better
posture.

<Callout variant="info" title="Strawman vs. steelman">
**Strawman:** attack the weakest, dumbest version of the other side — a caricature you built to lose. **Steelman:** build the *strongest, smartest* version of the other side, the one its best defender would actually claim, and engage *that*. Only the steelman tells you anything true. Beating a strawman just confirms what you already wanted to believe.
</Callout>

Steelmanning is the direct cure for **biased interpretation**, the second face of
confirmation bias from last lesson. Biased interpretation reads opposing arguments
in their weakest light and your own in their strongest; steelmanning forces the
reverse discipline. It also quietly enlists two other models you've met.
**Hanlon's razor** — never attribute to malice what is adequately explained by
something more ordinary — stops you from caricaturing opponents as evil or idiotic,
which is the emotional fuel of most strawmen. And **Occam's razor** keeps the
steelman honest: the strongest version of their argument is usually the *simplest*
charitable reading of what they actually mean, not an elaborate one you invented to
make them sound deranged.

**Worked example.** You think a colleague's proposal to delay a launch is overly
cautious nonsense. Strawman: *"He just wants to stall forever and never ship
anything."* Now steelman it: *"He's seen launches blow up when one untested
dependency fails in production, the cost of a public failure here is reputational
and hard to reverse, and a two-week delay buys a full integration test for a small
known cost."* Stated that way, you might still disagree — but now you're arguing
with the real idea, and you might even discover he's right. The strawman could only
ever have confirmed you. The steelman could actually change your mind.

## Seek the disconfirming case

Back in the Wason task lesson, you watched people try to crack the *2, 4, 6* rule
by proposing triples they *expected to pass* — `8, 10, 12`, `20, 22, 24` — and
learned almost nothing, because the only informative test is the one you expect to
*fail*. That experiment was a lab-sized version of a life-sized habit. The third
tool simply makes the Wason lesson a standing reflex: **actively seek the
disconfirming case.**

The move has two parts, and skipping either one defeats it. First, ask the
question Wason's smartest subjects never did: *"What evidence would change my
mind?"* If you can't name anything that would, your belief isn't strong — it's
unfalsifiable, which means it's untestable, which means you don't actually know if
it's true. Second — and this is where almost everyone bails — *actually go look for
that evidence.* Naming the disconfirming case and then never running the test is
just the lawyer drafting an objection and filing it in the trash.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The pitfall: token disconfirmation">
There's a counterfeit version of this tool that *feels* like fairness but is pure confirmation bias in disguise: **token disconfirmation.** You "consider the other side" — but you carefully pick the *weakest, most easily refuted* counter-argument you can find, knock it over, and award yourself a medal for open-mindedness. It's the strawman's sneakier cousin: instead of caricaturing the opponent, you cherry-pick their worst actual point. Real disconfirmation hunts for the *strongest* evidence against you — the version that would genuinely hurt. If your "fairness" never makes you uncomfortable, it's fake.
</Callout>

So the test of honest disconfirmation is emotional: did it sting? If you went
looking for what would prove you wrong and came back relieved and unscathed every
time, you didn't really go looking — you took a victory lap through the safe part
of the evidence. The disconfirming case worth seeking is the one you're a little
afraid to find.

**Worked example.** You believe a new feature is a hit because usage is up. Token
disconfirmation: "Sure, maybe a *few* people didn't like it" — then you read three
mild complaints, shrug, and move on, feeling thorough. Real disconfirmation: you
deliberately ask *what would prove this feature is failing?* — and decide in
advance it's "engagement up but retention or revenue down a month later." Then you
go pull the retention and revenue numbers, the ones that could actually wreck your
story. *That's* the test. If you only ever check the metric that's already smiling
at you, you're running Wason's `8, 10, 12` for the rest of your career.

## Pre-register what would change your mind

Here's a maddening property of confirmation bias: it doesn't just bend what you see
*now*, it quietly rewrites what you *predicted* and *believed* before. After the
fact, the evidence that confirmed you feels like the evidence you always expected,
and the threshold that would have changed your mind mysteriously moves to wherever
the results didn't land. *"Well, that one study doesn't really count."* *"I never
said it would work in **all** cases."* This is **biased memory** at work, and the
fourth tool exists to nail your past self to the wall before your present self can
edit the record.

The move is **pre-registration**: write down, *in advance and in specific terms,
the observation that would prove your belief wrong* — and then keep the note. The
practice is borrowed straight from good science. To stop researchers from
fishing through their data and "discovering" whatever result they were hoping for,
journals now ask them to **pre-register** their hypotheses and analysis plan before
collecting a single data point. You declare what you predict and what would
falsify it *up front*, so you can't quietly reinterpret a miss as a hit. You can run
the same protocol on your own beliefs.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Pre-registration in one move">
Before the evidence arrives, write down — for real, where you can't pretend later — *"I will count my belief as wrong if I observe X."* Be specific: a number, a date, an outcome. Then, when the evidence comes in, you have a fixed yardstick your future self can't bend. You've made it impossible to move the goalposts, because you wrote down exactly where they stood.
</Callout>

This connects directly to **base rates** and **thinking in probabilities**. A
belief held well isn't a flag you plant and defend; it's a probability you're
willing to *update* — a posterior, in the language of Bayesian reasoning, that
should move when the evidence does. Pre-registration is how you make updating
honest. If you wrote down "I'm 80% confident this launch beats target, and I'll
revise down hard if week-one sign-ups come in under 5,000," then a 3,000 result has
to *cost* you something. Without the note, the bias simply absorbs the miss and
your confidence sails on untouched. The discipline of **keeping score** — actually
tracking your forecasts and grading them later — is what turns a opinion-haver into
a calibrated forecaster. People who keep score get better; people who trust their
memory of how right they've been get more confident and no more accurate.

**Worked example.** Two analysts both predict a product will succeed. Analyst A
just "has a strong feeling." Analyst B writes, before launch: *"Success means
30,000 active users by day 90. If we're under 15,000, I was wrong and we pivot."*
At day 90 the product has 12,000 users. Analyst A, consulting memory, recalls that
he "always had some doubts" and argues the real test is year one. Analyst B looks
at her note, says "I was wrong," and the team pivots three months earlier than they
otherwise would have. Same evidence; one of them learned from it. The note is the
difference between a forecast and a feeling.

## Build dissent into your environment

The first four tools are things *you* run inside your own head — which is also
their weakness, because the bias lives in that same head and gets a vote on whether
you run them. The most robust defense, then, is to stop relying on your solo
willpower and **engineer dissent into your environment** so the disconfirming view
shows up *whether you went looking or not.* This is **inversion** applied to your
information diet: don't ask "how do I become more open-minded?", ask "how do I build
a system that hands me the counter-argument even on the days I'd rather not have
it?"

A handful of concrete structures do this:

- **A red team:** a group whose explicit job is to attack the plan, find the holes,
  and argue for failure. Their assignment makes dissent *expected* instead of
  rude.
- **A designated devil's advocate:** one person formally tasked, in every important
  decision, with making the strongest case *against* it — a built-in steelman of
  the opposition.
- **Advisors who disagree with you:** deliberately keep people in your circle who
  see the world differently and who'll tell you when you're wrong. Surround
  yourself only with people who already agree, and you've built a flattery machine.
- **Diverse information sources:** read the smartest people you *disagree* with, not
  just the ones who comfort you.

The danger this guards against is the **echo chamber** from last lesson — the
algorithmic feed and the like-minded crowd that quietly serve you only what you
already think, until a one-sided diet *feels* like the whole world. An all-yes feed
isn't reassurance; it's confirmation bias with infinite scroll.

There's a final, crucial piece, and it's where this connects to **incentives** —
the model underneath this whole course. People will only voice dissent if it's safe
and rewarded to do so. If the person who points out the flaw gets frozen out,
labeled "not a team player," or passed over while the cheerful yes-man gets
promoted, then your red team will quietly learn to nod, and you'll have all the
forms of dissent and none of the substance. **Align the incentives so people are
rewarded for finding flaws, not for agreeing with you.** Praise the colleague who
catches the fatal problem before launch; thank the advisor who told you the thing
you didn't want to hear. Remember: *what gets rewarded gets repeated* — and if
agreement is what pays, agreement is all you'll ever get.

## The bias blind spot

You've now got five tools. So here's the question that decides whether they ever
actually help you: *who are you most likely to think doesn't need them?*

The psychologist **Emily Pronin** and her colleagues documented the answer, and
it's the cruelest twist in the whole subject. They found that people readily
recognize cognitive biases in *others* while believing themselves to be largely
*immune* — a meta-bias they named the **bias blind spot.** When you ask people
whether the average person is swayed by self-interest or motivated reasoning,
they say *obviously yes.* When you ask whether *they themselves* are, they say
*no, not really — I see clearly.* Everyone's a sucker except me.

The mechanism is exactly what this course has been circling from the start. You
judge *other* people's bias by their behavior — you can see them rationalizing. But
you judge *your own* bias by introspection: you look inside, find no feeling of
being biased, and conclude you aren't. The catch is that bias doesn't *announce
itself* in your conscious experience. Your biased conclusions don't show up stamped
"conveniently true." They show up stamped **"obviously true."** The whole problem
is that being biased and being right feel *identical from the inside*.

<Reveal client:visible label="You've spent this lesson learning to do this to other people's reasoning. Now turn every tool on your own." buttonLabel="Turn it on yourself">
Here's the part that's supposed to make you uncomfortable. Reading this lesson, you've almost certainly been picturing *other people* who need it — the colleague who never steelmans, the relative trapped in an echo chamber, the pundit who moves goalposts. That reflex, the one that exempts *you* while indicting them, **is the bias blind spot operating in real time.** The consider-the-opposite question, the steelman, the disconfirming case, the pre-registered falsifier, the engineered dissent — they are worth almost nothing aimed outward at people who annoy you. They only pay off aimed *inward*, at the belief you're most certain of, on the day you least want to question it. The feeling of certainty you're carrying right now is not evidence that you're the exception. It's the exact symptom the whole course warned you about. *You are not immune. Especially not now, when you feel like you finally get it.*
</Reveal>

So the tools come with a usage instruction that doubles their difficulty: turn them
*hardest* on the beliefs you hold *most* confidently, the ones tied to your
identity, your past decisions, your side. Those are precisely the beliefs your mind
guards best — and therefore the ones a deliberate procedure, run on purpose against
your own comfort, is most needed to crack. The lawyer never cross-examines his own
client. Your job, from here on, is to be the one mind that does.

willed away — but it can be *out-procedured.*
