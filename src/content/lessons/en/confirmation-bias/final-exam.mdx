It pulls together the whole course: the lawyer-versus-scientist model and the precise definition, why a sharper mind often defends a belief *better* rather than checks it, the three places the bias lives (biased search, biased interpretation, biased memory), Wason's 2-4-6 task and the logic of falsification, motivated reasoning and myside bias, how echo chambers and feeds amplify all of it, why the bias was useful enough to evolve, and the deliberate toolkit that fights it. ## Key takeaways

<Callout variant="success" title="Key takeaways">
**Confirmation bias** is the mind’s habit of reasoning like a **lawyer, not a scientist**:
it **searches** for evidence that fits, **interprets** ambiguous data to fit, and
**remembers** the hits while forgetting the misses — all bending toward the belief you
already hold. **Intelligence is no defense**; via **motivated reasoning** a sharper mind
just builds a more elaborate case for the answer it wanted ("Can I believe it?" for
welcome claims, "Must I believe it?" for unwelcome ones). **Wason’s 2-4-6 task** shows
why: a pile of confirmations proves nothing, because only a **disconfirming** test can
rule a hypothesis out — that’s **falsification**. At scale it fuels **biased
assimilation** and **attitude polarization** (the same evidence drives two camps apart),
**myside bias**, and **echo chambers** built by feeds optimized for engagement rather
than truth. It’s near-universal because it was once **adaptive** — cheap, persuasive,
and socially cohesive — but dangerous now. The antidote isn’t willpower, it’s habit:
**consider the opposite**, **steelman** the other side, **seek the strongest
disconfirming case**, **pre-register what would change your mind**, **build dissent into
your environment**, and — because of the **bias blind spot** — turn every bit of it
hardest on yourself. The one reflex worth keeping: *"What would prove me wrong, and have
I actually looked?"*
</Callout>
