This is the final exam for **Punctuated Equilibrium**. It pulls the whole course together: the picture of change as *lumpy, not smooth* — long flat stretches of **stasis** snapped by sudden lurches — and the machinery behind each part. You'll be tested on why stasis is **active, not passive** (stabilising selection, developmental and genetic constraints, gene flow across big populations averaging change away) and why a flat line should be read as **stored pressure**; on where the fast change actually happens (at **speciation** events, in small **peripheral isolates**, driven by founder effects, reduced gene flow and strong local selection) and why "rapid" means *geologically* fast — thousands to tens of thousands of years — **not** a one-generation leap; on why the fossil record's famous **gaps are data**, not embarrassment, and how the whole thing reads on a **fitness landscape** (stasis = a population pinned to a local peak; punctuation = a **valley-crossing** a small isolate can make and a big population can't); on the *same rhythm* showing up in technology, organisations (**Tushman & Romanelli**), science (**Kuhn**), habits and politics; and finally on **where the model lies** — it is *not* saltationism, *not* anti-Darwinian, *not* an either/or war with gradualism, *not* a licence to sit and wait, and *not* proof that a flat line means nothing is happening. Take your time; several questions look easy until you notice the "sudden" is being misread as magic, the confident date is over-fit, the stasis is being read as absence, or gradualism and punctuation are being forced into either/or.

**Punctuated equilibrium** says change is *lumpy, not smooth*: long flat **stasis** snapped by sudden
**lurches** — and every part of that sentence is doing work. Stasis is **active, not empty**: stabilising
selection culls the extremes, developmental and genetic constraints (canalisation) buffer the form, and
gene flow across a big population averages novelty back out — so a flat line is **stored pressure**, not a
dead one (the horseshoe crab, coelacanth and Phacops trilobites are stasis made visible). The **punctuation**
is fast but not magic: it happens at **speciation** in small **peripheral isolates** (Mayr\'s allopatric
speciation, founder effects, reduced gene flow, strong local selection), triggered when a shock removes a
constraint, an isolate breaks free, or pressure tips a threshold — and "rapid" means *geologically* fast,
thousands to tens of thousands of years of **ordinary microevolution**, never a one-generation leap. The
record\'s famous **gaps are data**: rapid change in a small, local population rarely fossilises, so scarce
transitionals are exactly what the model predicts (reframing Darwin\'s worry). On a **fitness landscape**,
stasis is a population pinned to a **local peak** and punctuation is a **valley-crossing** a small isolate can
make — via drift and founder effects, or when a shifting environment deforms the terrain — that a big
population can\'t. The *same rhythm* runs through **technology** (plateaus + platform shifts, QWERTY lock-in),
**organisations** (Tushman & Romanelli\'s convergent periods broken by revolutionary reorientations, structural
inertia as stabilising selection), **science** (Kuhn\'s paradigm shifts once anomalies pass a threshold),
**habits** (the discontinuity window a life shock opens) and **politics** (stability while pressure builds, then
collapse). Hold it honestly: it is **not saltationism** and **not anti-Darwinian**, it is **not either/or** with
gradualism (the debate is relative *frequency*, and it\'s contested), you should **engineer** a punctuation
rather than wait for one — build an isolate, remove the constraint, push to threshold — and beware the
**hindsight trap**: every "sudden" lurch had a long invisible build-up, so don\'t over-fit a tidy trigger, and
never mistake "no visible change" for "nothing happening."
