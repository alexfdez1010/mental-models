Give a small child a hammer and, the old line goes, everything they meet suddenly needs pounding. The lamp, the cat, the coffee table — all of it gets read as a nail, because a nail is the only thing a hammer knows how to see. It's funny in a toddler. It is not funny in a grown, intelligent adult who has exactly one good idea about how the world works and quietly bends every situation they meet until it fits that idea. And here is the uncomfortable part: **that adult is the natural endpoint of studying models one at a time.** Learn only supply and demand and every problem becomes a market. Learn only psychology and every failure becomes a bias. Learn only game theory and everyone is a strategic player. The single sharpest model in your kit, held alone, doesn't make you clear-sighted. It makes you a person with a hammer.

This course is the cure, and it's the reason all the earlier courses existed. You didn't collect **inversion**, **base rates**, **feedback loops**, **incentives**, **supply and demand** and **game theory** so you could pick a favourite. You collected them so that when a real, unlabelled situation lands on your desk — a bank rumour, a job offer, a product that's suddenly everywhere — you can run *several* of them over it at once and build one judgment from what they collectively say. That interlocking mesh of models, read together, is what Charlie Munger called a **latticework**, and using it is the last and highest skill this whole site is built to teach.

## The one idea to take away

Before five lessons unpack it, here's the whole model in a line:

<Callout variant="tip" title="The one-sentence version">
A **latticework** is a set of mental models from *different disciplines*, held at once and run over the *same* situation, so that they **check** each other (one vetoes another's blind spot), **complete** each other (each explains a different part), and sometimes **stack** the same way (a lollapalooza) — turning many partial lenses into a single, better judgment.
</Callout>

Three verbs are doing the work in there, and the whole course hangs on them. Models **check** each other: a thrilling story runs into a cold base rate and the base rate wins. Models **complete** each other: supply and demand explains the price while game theory explains the players — different parts of one picture. And models **stack**: when several independent models all point the same way, their forces multiply into the **lollapalooza effect** you met last course — except now you're the one holding the models, not the one being carried off by them. The skill is knowing which of the three is happening, because each means something completely different for what you should do next.

## Watch models agree — and clash — on one decision

Reading "models check and complete each other" is one thing. *Watching* it is another. Below is a **decision desk**. Pick a scenario — a bank rumour where a queue is forming, or a viral product your company could bet on — and you get a panel of model-lenses. Switch them on one at a time. Each lays its own reading on the desk and nudges a needle toward one of two poles.

Two things are worth hunting for. First, switch on lenses that all point the **same way** and watch the needle pin hard to one pole — that's a **stacked**, lollapalooza reading, where many independent models converge. Second, switch on lenses that point **opposite ways** and watch the needle fall back toward the middle while the desk flags a **tension**. That flagged clash is not a failure of the method — it *is* the method working. The disagreement between your models is the most valuable thing the desk can show you, because it points straight at what you don't yet understand.

Carry two things out of that panel. First, **a latticework doesn't hand you a verdict — it organises the argument.** When lenses stack, you get conviction *and* a warning. When they clash, you get a map of exactly where to look. Either way you're better off than with one model shouting alone. Second, **which lenses you switched on was a choice** — and a decision you don't know how to read (you're outside your **circle of competence**) is one where you can't tell which models even apply. That meta-skill of *choosing the lenses* is what separates a real latticework from a person flipping switches at random, and it's what lesson 4 is about.

## Why one model always feels like enough (and why that's the trap)

Here's the psychological catch that makes this hard. From the inside, one model never *feels* like too few. It feels like clarity. The person who explains every event through incentives isn't aware of forcing anything — they genuinely see incentives everywhere, vividly, and the explanation feels complete every single time. That's the danger of the hammer: it doesn't announce itself as a hammer. It announces itself as *insight*. The single model you love most is precisely the one you'll over-apply without ever noticing, because a good model is genuinely illuminating right up to the edge of where it stops applying — and it gives you no warning at that edge.

<Callout variant="warning" title="The trap in one line">
Your favourite model will feel like enough on every problem, including the ones it's wrong about — because a model that fits *most* of the time gives no signal when you've wandered past its edge. The defence isn't a better single model; it's the deliberate habit of asking, out loud, "what would a *different* model say about this?" — which is exactly why the latticework has to be run on purpose, not trusted to instinct.
</Callout>

## The map of the course

Five teaching lessons build the capstone from the ground up, then one exam you can't undo. The route:

1. **Why One Model Is Never Enough** — the man-with-a-hammer tendency in full: how a single model distorts, why the model you know best is the one you'll over-apply, and how your **circle of competence** tells you when a situation is one your models can even read.
2. **The Latticework** — Munger's actual idea: models from many disciplines as an interlocking mesh you hang experience on, why *cross-disciplinary* breadth beats depth in one field, and the handful of big models that pay their way across almost every domain.
3. **Three Ways Models Combine** — the engine of the whole skill: models that **stack** the same direction (a lollapalooza), models that **check** each other (base rate vs vivid story), and models that **complete** each other (price, players, dynamics — one situation, several lenses).
4. **Running the Lattice: A Case Study** — a full worked decision (a bank run, read live through incentives, game theory, feedback loops and social proof at once) plus the meta-skill of *choosing* which models a novel situation needs.
5. **The Checklist — and Where the Lattice Lies** — turning the latticework into a written checklist and pre-mortem you run on purpose, and the honest limits: forcing a model, double-counting one effect under two names, confirmation-shopping for the flattering model, and paralysis-by-lattice.

Then a **Final Exam** — graded, one question at a time, one-way: once you answer, it locks. No back button, no retries, 70% to pass.

### How to use this course

One habit does most of the work: **before you read our analysis of any scenario, run your own lattice first.** Ask what three different models say about it, out loud or on paper, *then* read on. And live in the decision desk — switch lenses on until you can feel the difference between a stacked reading (all agreeing, needle pinned) and a flagged tension (clashing, needle centred, something to investigate) in your hands. A latticework you've *watched* organise an argument sticks far better than one you've only read described.
